Data Collection — Find Email Addresses

The goal: Find the Financial Aid Department email addresses for a list of colleges.

Task Setup

This task has two steps for the worker:

  • Find the financial aid section on a college website; and
  • Copy and paste the best email address that can be used to contact the department.

(Sometimes a page has multiple email addresses, so we'll rely on human intelligence to collect the most useful one.)

Next, imagine that we have a spreadsheet listing information about colleges. Each row contains the name of a college, the college's main Web address, and the state where it's located. And every row in the spreadsheet represents a unit. Those units will represent the answers we need.

The sample task is represented below, as a worker would see it:

The final spreadsheet will contain the initial information plus the answers from the workers.

How do we know the answers are good?

We verify the answers using CrowdControl™, which includes two key components.

1. Gold Standard

Take a set of known answers, a gold standard, to ensure quality results. In each task, we mix in the known items with unknown items to determine how well workers are doing. If a worker makes a mistake on one of our known results, we can notify the worker where they erred, thereby ensuring higher work quality.

The gold-standard enabled training mechanism gives us better results, as shown in the graph below:

Without the gold standard, the error rates nearly double. Using a gold standard:

  • improves worker accuracy by showing them what results are expected; and
  • allows our system to quickly determine who is giving the highest quality results.

2. Confidence Rating

Our system computes a confidence score on every result. You can use the confidence score to determine which results have lower chances of errors or ambiguity.

We compute the confidence score from: 1) the workers' historical accuracy and 2) the level of agreement among workers on the task.

The next graph shows how answers with higher confidence tend to have fewer errors. For example, on this task, results with confidence values above 0.5 had no errors. Of course, if we collected more data, we might find a small number of errors in the higher confidence answers.

This graph also shows that using our gold standard improves worker accuracy over a larger range of confidence values. The solid gold line shows the accuracy of our system with three workers including gold data, while the dashed green line shows the accuracy of our system with three workers and no gold data. Additionally, the gold data increases the overall confidence scores of all the data.
