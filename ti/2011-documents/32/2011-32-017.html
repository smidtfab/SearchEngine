Contemplating crowdsourcing: mass collaboration under the microscope

Jun 19th, 2009
by Kirsty Yeomans.

Having been headline news for so long, when Parliament finally released MPs’ expenses a clamour for analysis of the data was inevitable: who’s spent the most; what’s the average claim; who’s the cheapest to run..?

But when the data was finally made publicly available, it came in .pdf format, not tidy excel spreadsheets - no quick-win pivot tables then. Moreover, the .pdfs had been redacted making analysis that bit trickier. The upshot was that every page would need to be individually viewed and analysed, and with approximately 26,000 pages that was going to take quite some time via conventional methods.

The answer? Crowdsourcing apparently.  Both The Guardian and these guys developed software to allow the public to do the legwork, significantly reducing the time delay between the release of the data and publishing of the analysis. In a nutshell, the software allows the ‘analyst’ to:

  1. Read the .pdf page
  2. Determine what is on it
  3. Summarize it for analysis
  4. Categorise it e.g. ‘worth investigating’, ‘already been in the news’, ‘boring’.

From a technical perspective this is quite brilliant, especially given the time constraints the developers will have been working under.  But from an analysis perspective I have a few issues:

  1. The analysts are unknown. We don’t know their literacy and numeracy levels, so how do we guarantee they can read and understand the documents?
  2. Who is back-checking the analysis and how thorough will they be?
  3. How defined are the categories for further analysis and how can we expect consistency when hundreds, if not thousands, of people are making judgment calls for each page?

So while the public may get the analysis quickly, will it stand up to scrutiny? I’m not convinced.  This tool is undoubtedly powerful and crowdsourcing may have some useful applications in research but until these issues are resolved I’m not sure we at FreshMinds Research will be recommending it to our clients just yet.

    Excellent post - a fresh insight into an area where there’s a mountain of blather.

    I think your question about whether it will stand up to scrutiny is a good one. Being politically sensitive material, there is also the important question of the motivations of the individulas doing the legwork. Whereas the problem of crowdsourcing is usually one of too much fuss (everyone shouting “Look what I’ve found!”),* in this case the real danger is the opposite.

    That might seem abn odd claim given the media’s seemingly unending fascination and outrage in this matter. But when someone tags a page as ‘worth investigating’, it will by definition be investigated, and subsequently ignored if they were doing so without justification. The real problem will be those people who decide to bury a dodgy page with the heading ‘nothing to see here’.

    I’ve got some experience with crowdsourcing, both professional experience with trying to develop it for ConsumerReports and personal interest being a methodology geek.

    The best crowdsourcing apps tend to rely on the crowd to police itself.

    If members of the crowd are extracting numbers from the text, then you’d set a threshold so you don’t believe any number until the same number’s been extracted from the same source x number of times without a bi-tri-etc-modal distribution.
