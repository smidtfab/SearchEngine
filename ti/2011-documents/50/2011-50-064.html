Social Media in Real Life: Crowdsourcing at the Walker Art Center

Think museums are old and stodgy? Think again! Minneapolis’s Walker Art Center recently opened an exhibit entitled “50/50: Audience and experts Curate the Collection” in which- you guessed it- the museum actually crowdsourced visitors to decide on works for their upcoming exhibition.

How did they do it? The museum’s press release states “Last summer, the public was invited to view 183 images of art from the Walker’s renowned collection of works on paper. Using a digital kiosk in a Walker gallery and an online survey at walkerart.org, individuals cast nearly a quarter-million votes on whether particular artworks should “definitely” or “maybe not” be included in the exhibition 50/50: Audience and Experts Curate the Paper Collection. Meanwhile, chief curator Darsie Alexander was considering artists who have been collected in depth and, inevitably, bringing an element of personal preference to the process of organizing the “expert” half of the exhibition.”

The final product is a 200 work show— part social experiment, part exploration of the Walker’s permenant collection. In total, 137,844 votes were cast in the Walker’s lobby. In the end, 100 works were chosen from visitor votes and the other half were chosen by the Walker’s chief curator Darsie Alexander, who said “Seeing and knowing the results was an important premise of this entire effort, both for the ‘public’ side and ‘mine,’”

Works chosen by citizens were mounted on a wall (close together, “salon” style) standing opposite works chosen by museum visitors. Top competitors among the “citizens choice” were Edgar Arceneaux’s graphite and gouache “House Upside Down” (2000) with Chuck Close’s “Self-Portrait” coming in third. Fiona Banner’s “Break Point” won the cake with 868 total votes. Other popular artists in the fans’ section were Warhol, Picasso, Hockney, Ruscha and Matisse. Alexander’s opposing wall contained works by Joseph Beuys, Jasper Johns, Kiki Smith and Kara Walker. “I waited to see what people voted for before I planned my side,” stated Alexander, regarding her own curatorial selections.

Can’t wait to see what the folks in the museum world think of next! With more and more cultural institutions making their way into social media— by way of Facebook and Twitter— it seems like it’s only a matter of time before we see another project happening like this one using Facebook fans and Twitter followers as the crowd to crowdsource from!
