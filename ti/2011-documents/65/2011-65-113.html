Why Amazon's Mechanical Turk is a Turkey

Feb 14, 2011 Amanda Duffy

Amazon's Mechanical Turk Landing Page - Screenshot: Amanda Duffy
It is almost five years since Amazon released its crowdsourcing platform Mechanical Turk but, sadly, it bears little relation to Amazon.com's slick image.

Amazon.com is synonymous with easy navigation, choice and an attitude to customer service that puts most other retailers to shame. However, do some random Googling and there are rumblings of discontent from both, in Mechanical Turk’s language, the requesters (program developers) and the providers (workers)?

The Beginnings of Mechanical Turk

The name originates from an 18th century machine that not only played chess, but played well enough to beat its human opponents. Of course, it was a hoax, as inside the machine was a human chess master. Amazon’s Mechanical Turk was originally developed as an in-house tool to test information for consistency using humans rather than computers, as computers are unable to perform some tasks as well as humans.

Amazon were so enthralled with the results they saw a niche for the application in other business areas and Mechanical Turk, the crowdsourcing platform was launched to the wider world in November 2006. So, now it is used by program developers to put up jobs, or HITS (Human Intelligence Tasks), and workers accept the HITS, complete them, return them to the developer and await payment.

Mechanical Turk Beta

Initially released in beta testing stage, nearly five years later, it is still in beta with no-one seemingly able to answer why. It doesn’t take a genius to work out that an unfinished, clunky interface could be a major contributor to a lot of the problems with Mechanical Turk.

The basic interface is a source of frustration for users and there are blogs and forums on the internet venting their spleens at the lack of clarity from Amazon. One major gripe is the lack of a forum on the site for developers and workers to contact each other. In fact, there is no forum for anyone to interact with anyone.

Workers have set up their own forums elsewhere with some developers discussing issues with them there. Amazon has maintained that they are not getting involved between developers and workers, but this is leading to considerable problems.

What's So Bad About Mechanical Turk?

Some scurrilous requesters are putting blocks on workers so that workers cannot accept a HIT. Sometimes blocks are accidental but a worker has no way of knowing that they have received a block, as it does not show on their interface.

The first they know of any problem is when they have received three blocks – because this results in an automatic ban. The most sinister thing about this is that it there is no redress for the worker, no investigation, no form of dispute resolution – it is three strikes and you’re out, with your earnings forfeited.

Amazon stress that they do not want to get involved, but it is them waving the Draconian stick.

If this happens to a worker, the only recourse is emailing Mechanical Turk where you will get a very timely, nicely-worded reply, which contains little substance, and the contact details of the Requester with directions to sort it out directly with them. (See Brokenturk Too Little Too Late: The New Amazon Way,)

The Problem with Amazon's Hands-Off Approach

Amazon’s hands-off approach is actually quite worrying for all users of the platform, as well as other interested parties. Associate Professor Panos Ipeirotis from the Stern School of Business of New York University has recently undertook some telling research, which concluded that over 40% of HITS from new requesters are spam and that 30% of new requesters are spammers.

The full results were published in his article Mechanical Turk: Now with 40.92% spam at behind-the-enemy-lines in December 2010. This does not bode well as a business interface and will only become more of a problem unless Amazon does step in and administer proper checks.

One of the problems is the lack of reputation profiles for both providers and requesters. The current system for providers is based simply on ‘number of hits completed’ and ‘approval rate’, not on experience, skill or qualifications. The number of hits completed is meaningless. It doesn’t differentiate between someone clicking a mouse button and someone who has written a 7,000 word review. ‘Approval rate’ is almost as arbitrary.

You finish the task correctly, you get an approval. Don’t forget, these tasks are not brain surgery, but if you feel you can’t do it you just return it and that’s that. No reflection on your approval rate. From a provider’s perspective, there should be some way to measure a requester’s trustworthiness and, moreover, if there is an issue to have some recourse to address it.

Finally, the other issue is with regard to payment of international workers. It does state on Mechanical Turk’s website that only US workers can have cash paid into their bank accounts but international workers will get gift vouchers for Amazon.com. Given that the site has already pointed out that international workers will not be paid, wouldn’t it be a reasonable assumption to assume that the gift vouchers paid would be redeemable at, say, Amazon.co.uk?

Apparently not. The only way to spend the vouchers is through Amazon.com, incurring extortionate postal charges and a long, long wait for shipping. When I inquired further about this I received the nicely worded reply that they are ‘truly sorry for any misunderstanding’ and ‘hope to offer additional payment services to international members in the future’ although, of course, they couldn’t tell me how near in the future.

Having trawled the internet I see that other people received the same response in 2008. Don’t hold your breath then.

So, is there Anything Good About Mechanical Turk?

For developers wanting quick, accessible workers, with quality results crowdsourcing is, without doubt, very beneficial, getting the public to do their work for them, for very little recompense. Crowdsourcing is now part of the mainstream with everyone getting in on the act.

From newspapers, such as The Guardian, whose portal Investigate your MP’s Expenses used its readers to search through MP’s expense claims and decide whether they were interesting enough to warrant further investigation, to the current UK Chancellor who, according to the article Chancellor crowdsources for budget ideas appearing in The Guardian 4 February 2011, is opening an online portal for the public to submit ideas for the budget on 23 March 2011.

When considering that all the attendant contributions an employer has to make for an employee regarding tax, national insurance, office space, sick pay, etc. notwithstanding adherence to the minimum wage and the slew of employment legislation, and it is immediately apparent who gets the greatest benefits from crowdsourcing.

That is not to say that there is no benefit to workers. Some, obviously, need the cash. Some do it out of boredom. Some see it as way to earn some money whilst doing something else, like watching television. Some enjoy it. The thing is, it's all rather tenuous and, really, does anyone actually care about these workers? The requester; Mechanical Turk; Amazon?

Amazon puts such a lot of effort into managing the reputation of Amazon.com, it’s a shame it cannot put the same effort into managing the users of Mechanical Turk.
