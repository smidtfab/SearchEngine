Three Dutch trends in Information Retrieval

Last week was the Dutch-Belgian Information Retrieval Workshop. As one of the sponsors, the technical staff of Teezir was present to give demonstrations of eCare [1], to meet (new) friends, and to stay informed about the current developments in the field.

The following is an overview of notable trends that were seen in the various presentations and demonstrations:

Search in context. The first trend is a movement from seeing a search-query in isolation to seeing a search-query as part as of a larger (goal-oriented) task. This implies that traditional information retrieval evaluation measures such as Recall and Precision cannot be used in isolation (as Nicholas J. Belkin explained).

It also means that a search system is helped by creating a model of the type of task that the use is conducting; Suzan Verberne talked about how to construct such a model and what type of information the model should contain.

Furthermore, talking about tasks implies that a user might not always be looking for the single most relevant document, but instead wants to get an overview on the different aspects surrounding her search-query. In this direction the research of Peter Lubell-Doughtie and Katja Hofman is relevant, who presented a method to improve result diversity.

Within our eCare environment we see the same behavior; a user almost never poses a single search query. Instead a user reformulates a query to include more aspects of a brand or product and zooms in by clicking on the termclouds and other widgets. This is the reason why querying in eCare is not restricted to single pre-defined queries, and next to research on ranking and sentiment-mining algorithms we, for example, also do research on the best way to choose the terms in a term-cloud.

Sentiment analysis. The second trend of research was research on sentiment analysis of text. Not only presented Alexander Hogenboom the research of Bas Heerschop on accounting for negation in sentiment analysis; research that was done at Teezir. Also, Maral Dadvar presented similar research. Interesting conclusions of both methods is that, although there is some improvement, it is much less than one would initially expect.

Another way of improving sentiment analysis that was presented was research by Valentin Jijkoun on using focused topic sentiment lexicons. The idea is that positive and negative words depend on the topic and it is possible to learn these topic-dependent words. Although the results were not yet statistically significant, the approach and the initial results call for more research in this direction.

Crowd sourcing. Wikipedia defines crowd sourcing as outsourcing a task to an undefined, large group of people or community (a crowd), through an open call.  Although there was only one invited presentation about crowd-sourcing (from Gabriella Kazai) the potential of this area is quite big. Crowdsourcing can namely be used as a means for creating evaluation-corpora, but also for manual sentiment analysis on sentences that are difficult to grasp for a computer (e.g. detecting sarcasm).

Since people who participate in a crowd sourcing task (usually via Amazon Mechanical Turk / Crowdflower) are undefined, the main research focus is on quality control. The hopeful conclusion of the talk of Kazai was that if you can remove the spammers, then you can get reasonably high quality for low payment. In her task: “Find evidence in books to confirm or refute a factual statement” the wage was $0.01 per page.


As you read in this post, we had quite a good day at DIR. Therefore we want to thank the organizers: Corrado, Katja, Valentin, Edgar, Maarten, and Wouter.

If some (or all!) of these subjects interest you, please contact us to discuss possibilities for Master assignments, using our API, or other ways of cooperation. 

All mentioned papers can also be found in the DIR proceedings, available at: http://ilps.science.uva.nl/dir2011/proceedings.html
