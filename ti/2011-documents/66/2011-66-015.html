This investigation conducts measurement and evaluation of a multidimensional model of workplace motivation and engagement from a construct validation perspective. Two studies were conducted, one using the multi-item multidimensional Motivation and Engagement Scale-Work (N = 637 school personnel) and one using a parallel short form (N = 574 school personnel).

**********

For a number of reasons, work has presented many challenges for employees. In almost every organizational and industry setting, the workplace has been constantly changing and at an increasingly rapid pace because of growing competition fueled by globalization (Schabracq & Cooper, 2000). Estimates of time spent at the workplace have indicated that most employees work long hours, often not amply remunerated (Dollard, 2006). Reports of an increasing lack of control, less input into decision making, and less involvement in the scheduling of work tasks and methods of work have been consistently associated with poorer well-being (Karasek & Theorell, 1990). Indeed, stress-related workers' compensation claims have continued to increase at an alarming rate. For example, stress-related claims in Australia (the context of the present investigation) increased by 62% between 1996/1967 and 2002/2003 (Office of the Australian Safety and Compensation Council, 2006). This finding is consistent with indicators in other developed countries. For example, more than half of working adults in the United States have reported that they are concerned about the amount of stress in their lives (Stambor, 2006). Considering these findings, strong grounds clearly exist for the conduct of research emphasizing motivation, engagement, resilience, and satisfaction in the workplace and for the broader issue of career and employment measurement and evaluation, counseling, and development.

The present investigation focuses on a workplace known to be subject to many of the pressures and challenges just described. Specifically, this investigation conducts measurement and evaluation of workplace motivation and engagement in the school setting among teachers, counselors, psychologists, executive personnel, and administrative staff. Previous research has identified stress, disengagement, heavy workloads, little support, and high turnover in this challenging setting (Fry & Martin, 1994; Mayer, 2006; McCormack, Gore, & Thomas, 2006; Richardson & Watt, 2006; Smithers & Robinson, 2003). Such factors can significantly hamper individual career and employment development. The school setting, therefore, represents and important context in which to measure and evaluate not only motivation and engagement but also cognate constructs such as enjoyment of work, workplace participation, career aspirations, and workplace buoyancy that are relevant to thoroughgoing measurement and evaluation of individuals' career and employment and the implications such measurement and evaluation have for career and employment counseling and development.

THEORIES OF MOTIVATION AND ENGAGEMENT IN THE WORKPLACE

Many theories of workplace motivation and engagement have been suggested. Needs-based theories have assessed the needs the workplace meets or what needs the employee strives to have met (Brewer & Skinner, 2003). Maslow's (1954) need hierarchy proposed that individuals will experience greater job satisfaction. Herzberg's (1966) two-factor theory proposed two basic needs in the workplace: the need to avoid unpleasantness and pain and the need for growth and actualization. According to need achievement theory (Atkinson, 1957; see also Langan-Fox & Roth, 1995, for more recent work), individuals are motivated by the need to succeed or the need to avoid failure. McGregor (1960) posited two perspectives on workplace motivation: Theory X in which workers are seen to dislike and fear work (leading to laziness and disengagement) and Theory Y in which workers have the psychological need to work, to meet responsibilities, and to achieve.

Values-based theories of workplace motivation and engagement have also been proposed. According to equity theory (Adams, 1965; see also Greenberg, 1990; Summers & Hendrix, 1991, for more recent work), people prefer or value conditions of fairness and equity in their workplaces (e.g., in terms of pay, promotion, opportunities, and resources). People judge what they put in and get out of their jobs to develop their valuing of their work. According to theories promoting goal setting (e.g., Locke & Latham, 2002) and goal commitment (e.g., Erez & Zidon, 1984), the presence of a goal motivates people to strive to meet it. Furthermore, according to social learning theory (Bandura, 1997), self-regulation is a means to attain these goals in the workplace (Kanfer, 1990). According to expectancy-value theory--first addressed in the organizational domain by Vroom (1964; see also Van Eerde & Thierry, 1996, for more recent work)--individuals make decisions about behavior on the basis of perceived consequences of that behavior and their perceptions primarily constitute the strength of their expectancy that effort will translate into performance and how attractive the consequences of that performance is. Social learning theory is also relevant to expectancy-based research in that its central tenet, self-efficacy, is closely aligned to workplace expectancies (Locke & Henne, 1986) as is the issue of control (Rotter, 1966; see also Luthans & Martinko, 1987). These values-based theories have emphasized the consequences and returns of one's actions, which also brings into consideration operant theories (Skinner, 1948, 1971; see also Stajkovic & Luthans, 1997, for more recent work) and their more recent organizational applications such as organizational behavior management, which has emphasized the consequences of workplace behavior as motivators.

In summary, no one motivation theory in organizational, workplace, and career psychology is widely accepted. Because of this, some commentators have suggested that empirical research into such psychology has been fragmented. Many studies have addressed only a few aspects of workplace motivation and engagement and these aspects have not been embedded into a broader program (Thierry, 1998). The field of educational psychology is worth noting because it has proven to be a relatively fertile ground for integrated motivational theory and practice. Recent research in the educational domain has explored the validity of encompassing models of motivation (e.g., Pintrich, 2003). In this context, the Motivation and Engagement Wheel (Martin, 2001, 2002, 2003, 2007a, 2007b) was developed.

THE MOTIVATION AND ENGAGEMENT WHEEL

The Motivation and Engagement Wheel is composed of 4 higher order factors and 11 first-order factors (see Figure 1). Higher order factors (and their corresponding first-order factors) are adaptive cognitions (self-efficacy, mastery orientation, and valuing), adaptive behaviors (planning, task management, and persistence), impeding/maladaptive cognitions (anxiety, failure avoidance, and uncertain control), and maladaptive behaviors (self-handicapping and disengagement). Initially, this Motivation and Engagement Wheel was developed to measure and evaluate motivation and engagement in the educational domain (see Martin, 2001,2003, 2007a); however, Martin (2004a, 2004b) more recently has suggested that the Motivation and Engagement Wheel can be readily adapted to the workplace and has specifically articulated how this can be achieved.

Several theories are reflected in the adaptive dimensions of the Motivation and Engagement Wheel: (a) social learning theory (e.g., Bandura, 1997) and expectancy-based research (e.g., Van Eerde & Thierry, 1996; Vroorn, 1964) are reflected in the self-efficacy dimension; (b) social learning theory and its self-regulation component (e.g., Farr, Hofinanii, & Ringenbach, 1993; Kanfer, 1990) are reflected in the planning, task management, and persistence dimensions; (c) valuing (e.g., Adams, 1965; Van Eerde & Thierry, 1996; Vroom, 1964) is reflected in the valuing dimension; (d) self-actualization and the need for growth (Maslow, 1954) along with goals-based research (see Erez, 1984; Locke & Latham, 2002) are reflected in the mastery orientation dimension; and (e) goals-based research is also reflective of workers' inclination for planning and task management (goal setting; Locke & Latham, 2002) and persistence (goal commitment; Erez & Zidon, 1984). Other theories are reflected in the impeding and maladaptive dimensions of the Motivation and Engagement Wheel: (a) need achievement (e.g., Atkinson, 1957; Langan-Fox & Roth, 1995) and two-factor approaches (e.g., Herzberg, 1966; McGregor, 1960) are reflected in the anxiety; failure avoidance, self handicapping, and disengagement dimension, and (b) control is reflected in the uncertain control dimension (e.g., Luthans & Martinko, 1987; Rotter, 1966).

MEASUREMENT AND EVALUATION UNDERPINNING THE MOTIVATION AND ENGAGEMENT WHEEL

Alongside the Motivation and Engagement Wheel is its counterpart instrument: the Motivation and Engagement Scale (MES). Typically administered to high school students, the MES-High School (MES-HS; Martin, 2001, 2003, 2007a, 2007b) has demonstrated a strong factor structure and reliable and normally distributed factors, has consistently identified gender and age effects, and has predicted a variety of educational outcomes (such as enjoyment of school, classroom participation, and educational aspirations) and achievement-related outcomes (such as school grades, literacy, and numeracy). The present investigation assesses an adaptation of the MES to the workplace using the MES-Work (MES-W). Specifically, it conducts measurement and evaluation from a construct validation perspective using within and between-network approaches.

WITHIN-AND BETWEEN-NETWORK APPROACHES TO CONSTRUCT VALIDITY

Researchers in psychometric psychology have increasingly emphasized the need to measure and evaluate frameworks and instrument within a construct validation framework (e.g., see Marsh, 1997, 2002). Studies that have adopted a construct validation approach can be classified as within-network studies or between-network studies. Within-network studies explore the internal structure of a construct. Beginning with a logical analysis of internal consistency of the construct definition, measurement instruments, and generation of predictions, within-network studies typically have used empirical techniques such as confirmatory factor analysis (CFA), exploratory factor analysis, and reliability analysis. Between-network studies attempt to establish a logical,, theoretically consistent pattern of relations between constructs. This research considers these relations by adopting statistical procedures such as correlation, regression, or structural equation modeling (SEM) analyses to examine relationships between measures and instruments.

In reality, few studies have adopted this dual (within-and between-network) approach to validity; thus, in this sense, such studies have provided relatively limited input into understanding the constructs and measures under focus.
