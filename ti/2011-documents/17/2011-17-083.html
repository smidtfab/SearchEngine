Harry Houdini

Hooper sold it to Jim Smith, who moved it to Coney Island. One opponent there, who happened to be a sore loser, took a shot at the Ajeeb, which killed the operator, but this was covered-up by Smith.

The murder remained undiscovered until much later.

Starting in 1898, the operators of the Ajeeb started to complain about strange occurrences, such as it moving on its own. It was believed that it was haunted.

One operator even refused to play anything but checkers.

It is interesting to note that The Ajeeb was, like The Turk, destroyed in a fire (1929).

To this day, there are stories of sightings of the Ajeeb. Many claim it is residing somewhere in Ontario.

Kempelen gave the world quite a precious gift, although he might not have thought so at the time. He inspired others to dream and to consider the fact that impossibilities may indeed be possible.

The Turk
The 18th Century Chess-Playing Machine

During the 18th century, automatons were the rage in Europe and they were mainly built for the enjoyment of the wealthy. They were machines that seemed to mimic life. One such automaton was a mechanical flute player displayed by Jasques de Vancanson in 1737. It could play a tune while altering its lips and fingers. But his most famous automaton was a mechanical duck that would take grain from a spectator and eat it, drink, quack, splash in the water and digest it's food like a real duck and excrete it. It was the first time that a tube made of rubber from India was use, and this lead to the invention of flexible rubber tubing.

Jasques de Vancanson

Vancanson's main interest in inventing these types of machines was to understand the biological systems of man so to cure their diseases. In 1741, he was hired to modernize the French weaving industry. He drew up his plans that were to be tested in the city of Lyons by the silk workers. However, he had to flee for his life due to the riots that were created by the workers. They were afraid that they would become human parts of a huge automaton. So he quickly returned to Paris and became the official examiner of new mechanical inventions for the Acad�mie des Sciences. Members of the Acad�mie des Sciences did scrutinize the Turk, but they were unable to learn its secret.

There are, of course, many myths written about the Turk. One such myth is a story in the Encyclopedia Britannica (1911), explaining its secrets by Jean Robert-Houdin. His theory was that a Polish soldier by the name of Worousky, who lost both his legs in Russia during a rebellion, was smuggled out of Russia by Klempelen [the creator of The Turk] to tour the world inside the automaton.

Jean Robert-Houdin

Another myth was that Klempelen was a Baron, but this was not true.

The idea of a chess-playing machine became a reality in 1769, when Wolfgang von Klempelen created an automaton called 'The Turk' for the Empress Maria Theresa. He boasted at court that he could build a chess-playing automaton better than the famous builder of automatons (Vancanson) in 6 months.

Klempelen wanted to upstage the magnetic tricks that were used, although he did rely on magnets himself in the chess pieces.

Wolfgang von Klempelen
Empress Maria Theresa

Within six months (1770), he returned to the court at the Sh�nbrunn Palace in Vienna. The Turk looked like a turban figure sitting behind a short, wooden cabinet with a chessboard. The cabinet was approximately 4 feet long, 2.5 feet deep, 3 feet high, and rested on four brass casters. Because of this, it could be moved around the room freely to be observed from all angles. The cabinet was divided into three doors and had a long drawer along the bottom. The wooden figure's right arm rested on the top of the cabinet and its left hand held a long Turkish pipe.

Sh�nbrunn Palace in Vienna

Kempelen announced that his mechanical man would play the white pieces and would make the first move. A large key was then used to give the illusion that he was winding up the machine. Kempelen then asked for a volunteer to play against it.

Count Cobenzi was the first person to play against the Turk. He was defeated quickly.

The Turk was an aggressive player that could usually defeat most opponents within a half of an hour of play. However, The Turk did not always win. There was a time when a man by the name of Philidor (considered the finest chess player in Europe) defeated the Turk in the summer of 1783, which only increased the automaton's popularity.

Francois-Andre Danican Philidor

The Turk actually moved the chess pieces, and shook his head if an illegal move was made or occasionally knocked all the chess pieces off the table when he became angry. The machine had various gears, rods, and other parts inside the cabinet, which Klempelen would occasionally adjust, but these were all part of the illusion.

Overnight, Klempelen became famous, and he toured the palaces of Europe for the next 13 years. Many well known figures of the time played against the Turk, such as Benjamin Franklin, Catherine the Great, Charles Carroll of Carrollton (the last surviving signer of the Declaration of Independence) and the famous Prussian King, Frederick the Great, paid a small fortune to learn its secret.

The Turk was a remarkable invention, but it soon became a thorn in Kempelen's side, so to speak. He was quite an ingenious man who was well versed in physics, mechanics, and hydraulics, but because of The Turk's popularity, it overshadowed his real passion: a device that simulated speech.

At one time, he falsely claimed that it was broken. However, this did not work as planned. In 1783, Emperor Joseph II ordered him to fix it and go on tour. Von Kempelen exhibited the machine until his death, in 1804.

In 1821, the English mathematician Charles Babbage played two games against the Turk and lost both. Babbage agreed with Willis's theory as far as the Turk being a hoax, but he did believe in mechanical intelligence, which gave him the idea of a mechanical calculator. He published a plan for his machine and won a medal from the British Astronomical Society. His idea of a mechanical calculator is directly connected to the popularity of the Turk, since at that time the question of mechanical reasoning was considered being possible.

Kempelen is often referred to as the first experimental phonetician. In his book 'Mechanismus der menschlichen Sprache nebst Beschreibung einer sprechenden Maschine'  (Mechanism of the human language together with description of a speaking machine 1791), he included a detailed description of his speaking machine.

He wrote the book in hopes that someday it would be perfected.

His speaking machine was kept at the k. k. Konservatorium f�r Musik in Vienna until 1906, when it was donated to the Deutsches Museum (von Meisterwerken der Naturwissenschaft und Technik, (of masterpieces of the natural science and technology)) in Munich. There, it is exhibited in the department of musical instruments. Although, this machine differs somewhat from the one described in his book.

There were no real advancements in the area of speech synthesis until 1835, when Joseph Faber of Vienna included a tongue and a pharyngeal cavity that could change shape.

Joseph Faber's Euphonia (Talking Head)

In 1789, Freiherr Joseph Friederich von Racknitz built an automaton very similar to the Turk, just to demonstrate that a hidden player could be hidden in such a machine. He did not venture to guess how it was done, only that it was possible, and published a book on the subject ('Ueber den Schachspieler des Herrn von Kempelen und dessen Nachbildung'  (Over the chess player of the Mr. von Kempelen and its reproduction)).

The flaw in his theory was that the person hidden in the machine must be less than 4 feet tall.

In reality, Napoleon was actually playing against his own chess teacher, Johann Allgaier. Maelzel being the showman and Allgaier being the brains of the operation. It was said that a dwarf or child was hidden in the cabinet to accomplish this illusion, very similar to the way magicians or mentalists work today.

Deeply in debt from buying the machine, being a terrible businessman, and stealing the design of the metronome, Maelzel fled to America in 1825, taking the Turk with him. Maelzel died penniless in Cuba in 1839.

In 1821, Robert Willis published many books about the Turk to try to explain how it worked, which came as a relief to the populous, since there was a fear that machines might one day replace humans. "Automata may be able to do clever things", he assured, "but they can not respond to events."

Johann Maelzel

Napoleon Bonaparte

After Von Kempelen's death, the machine was sold to a Bavarian musician, Johann Maelzel. He was a maker of musical automatons, often referred to as the inventor of the metronome, which is not true, considering that he stole the design. Maelzel took The Turk on tour, in which the machine played three games against Napoleon Bonaparte in Vienna, in 1809. The Emperor lost all three games and stormed out after losing. Napoleon even tried to fool the Turk by making deliberate incorrect moves, in reply to which the Turk refused to play against him. Surely, this must have been a relief to Napoleon.

Joseph Friedrich Freiherr von Racknitz

In 1832, a best-selling book called 'Natural Magic' compared the Turk and Babbage's machines.

Babbage abandoned his original design in the mid-1830s for a more complicated calculator, the Analytic Engine, which he suggested could be programmed to play tic-tac-toe, checkers, and chess. Despite his blueprints and the grants given to him, neither of these machines were ever constructed due to the poor technological advances at the time. Babbage was the first person to come up with an algorithm, or mathematical formula, for playing games with movable pieces.

After seeing the Turk, Edmund Cartwright built the first power loom. His thinking was that if a machine could be built to play chess, then it was not unreasonable that a machine could be built to weave fabrics.

 David Brewster

Edgar Allen Poe was quite fascinated with the Turk, and wrote an expose on it, which was published in the Southern Literary Messenger (April 1836). Most of the information in the expose came from others.

However, the information was not completely accurate. Regardless of the inaccuracies, he brought forth process reasoning. He later switched topics and wrote about Von Kempelen in 1850.

Edmund Cartwright

In 1826, there was even a poem written in the Saturday Evening Gazette about the Turk by Hannah Flagg Gould. She is the first American woman to be mentioned in relation to chess.

In June of 1827, the truth of the Turk was told by two boys to the Baltimore Gazette. They had seen William Schlumberger, European chess grandmaster, get out of the Turk's back, gasping for air and suffering from heat exhaustion, but no one seemed to take notice of this.

In James Cook's book, 'The Arts of Deception' he devoted a chapter to the Turk and argued that the reason the public took no notice was not that they believed it impossible, but that they were intrigued with the possibility of artificial intelligence and wanted to figure the mystery out for themselves.

In the 1830's, David Brewster, a popular British scientist, said, "Those mechanical wonders which in one century enriched only the conjurer who used them, contributed in another to augment the wealth of the nation; and those automatic toys which once amused the vulgar, are now employed in extending the power and promoting the civilization of our species."

Edgar Allen Poe

The Turk's final resting place was the Philadelphia Museum, where it sat for years and was of no interest to anyone any longer, until a fire destroyed it in 1854.

In 1857, the last owner of the Turk, Dr. Mitchell, wrote an article called, 'The Last of a Veteran Chess Player' revealing its secrets in Chess Month magazine.

In 1864, a Bristol cabinetmaker by the name of Charles Hooper decided to build a copy of the Turk, which he named Ajeeb. In 1868 it made it's first appearance at the Polytechnical Institute in London. It resided at The London Crystal Palace until 1876, and after that it was displayed at The Royal Aquarium in Westminster.

Ajeeb

Hooper took it to Berlin and then to New York in 1885. Among some of its opponents were Harry Houdini, Teddy Roosevelt, Sarah Bernhardt and O. Henry. In 1886, it was placed in The Eden Museum in New York City.
