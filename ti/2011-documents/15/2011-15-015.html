The Mechanical Turk: the True Story of the Chess-Playing Machine that Fooled the World

Geographical, August, 2002 by Winnie Liesenfeld

Tweet
  • 1
  • 2
  • Next

by Tom Standage, Allen Lane, hb, pp274, 12.99 [pounds sterling]

During the dawn of the Industrial Revolution, automata, or self-moving machines, toured the courts of Europe in the form of mechanical tigers, ducks and dancing ladies. A multi-talented civil servant at the Viennese court, Wolfgang von Kempelen invented an automaton that would confound scientists and experts for decades to come. He created a life-size man capable of playing chess. Dressed in the Turkish style fashionable at the time, the device was known as `the Turk' and toured extensively throughout Europe and America,When wound up it usually beat its opponents in under a half hour. Many suspected foul play, but no-one could figure out how it worked. Despite trying to fool the automaton by making incorrect moves, neither Benjamin Franklin, Edgar Allen Poe nor Napoleon Bonaparte could beat the Turk. Unwittingly, Kempelen's invention paved the way for the development of the power loom, the telephone and the computer. In this fascinating tale, author Tom Standage paints a picture of an exciting era and demands respect for automata, the forgotten ancestors of modern technology.
