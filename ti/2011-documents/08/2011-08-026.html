CRM in 2020: The Gartner Team May 12
What percentage of Fortune 1000 companies will use active crowdsourcing as part of the new product or service development process in 2020? (Fewer than 10% currently do)    
Ed Thompson: "The new product development team has asked customers what they think for the past 50 years, but the effort and cost to collect opinions limited the sample size. The Web and its communities mean mass feedback can be collected and voted on for consumer products and services. Fifty percent of organizations have more of a B2B focus, which means the crowd's views are not as relevant, and more-traditional focus groups and feedback mechanisms will be more useful (for example, explaining what a medical device does then asking opinions on how the product should evolve could be dangerous or even illegal). So, for B2C organizations, this is likely to rise to 80%-plus, but for B2B, I think it will have limited adoption — perhaps 10%."

Michael Maoz: "True. Crowdsourcing will remain very low for companies overall — fewer than 15% through 2020. Crowd-informed processes will be very high — at least 80%. The difference is not subtle. In the former, crowdsourcing, a loosely organized and defined set of participants is handed the task of process development. As a corporate leader, I am not doing that. But I am very interested in maintaining ownership of the outcomes as I support and observe the crowd, listen, analyze and respond."

Gareth Herschel: "True. Crowdsourcing will remain an incredibly niche activity, with few companies pursuing it with any degree of consistency. 'Crowdsourcing lite,' such as soliciting online product ideas, will be a much more popular activity, albeit one driven more by branding and advertising priorities than any real expectation that good ideas will surface. Traditional market research (focus groups, surveys) will not be replaced by any form of crowdsourcing as the primary customer input to the product development process."

Johan Jacobs: "The only thing that has changed in the past 100 years when gathering feedback and input from a group of people is the size of the group. As corporations realize the power that expansion of their typical focus groups brings, and as they learn to harness social knowledge, crowdsourcing will become a mainstream practice. I would suggest that, by 2020, the use of crowdsourcing will be around 65%."

Kimberly Collins: "Customers are often the missing stakeholder in a company's CRM strategy. This is changing rapidly with social networking and enterprise feedback management. The 'voice of the customer' will take on new meaning for companies, and companies that listen and take action will be more likely to survive. I'd say around 60% will use active crowdsourcing for product and service development by 2020."

Adam Sarner: "Over 80%. There are many methods for crowdsourcing that do not involve large Facebook or sharing video projects. Asking prospects and customers to press 'thumbs up' if they like a review or if they thought a newspaper column was interesting is a many-to-many crowdsourcing technique that benefits both company and customer. Crowdsourcing should be considered a basic shared, but controlled/limited feedback mechanism that should be part of any CRM strategy. There are far more crowdsourcing opportunities than Facebook, Twitter and message boards."

Michael Dunne: "Assuming this question is heavily weighted toward a B2C business model, I think about 20%. Essentially, this consists of a baseline of companies that normally goes through some serious focus group procedure (entertainment programs, consumer goods, etc.) as part of its product development and go-to-market processes, complemented by lifestyle products and/or durable goods with specific subculture followings (e.g., guns, power sports, furniture, etc.) that can be easily presented, examined, discussed, etc., via the Web. From the B2B side, it will be interesting if the idea for electronic marketplaces returns, which also allowed elements of crowdsourcing to be implemented among approved/invite-only audiences (for example, for specific government purchasing requirements, such as police equipment, or enterprise software or some other realm). In short, one out of five consumer packaged or durable goods receiving serious input and discussion from target audiences seems about right. One out of four would seem a stretch, while one out of three seems too much — not everything is worth the attention, and not every company will want to do this (for example, have to assume generics and other variations would not receive crowdsourcing treatment due to cost considerations)."

Matthew Goldman: "I think that more than half of B2C companies will incorporate some form of crowdsourcing into their business models by 2020. Beyond looking for faster, cheaper ways of obtaining insight and feedback, the crowdsourcing process also serves as a viable mechanism for drawing more attention to services, products, causes or initiatives."

Gene Alvarez: "I think 75% will use some form of crowdsourcing within the new product introduction process. It will begin with leveraging data gathered from product reviews as input to new designs, and will transition to having the community design the product. We have yet to discover all the way crowdsourcing can be used and we have not seen all of its pitfalls yet either."

Chris Fletcher: "Sixty percent to 75% of companies developing consumer products will use crowdsourcing or some form of social knowledge/social CRM as part of the product development process. In the B2B segment, that number will fall to 30% or fewer."

Jim Davies: "I think the figure will be higher than the one posed (perhaps up to 60%), regardless of the B2B versus B2C distinction. Both types of organizations can and will benefit from engaging with customers/partners. The degree of involvement will vary by industry, but even in highly regulated environments, there will be subtle, crowd-based enhancements that can be adopted."
