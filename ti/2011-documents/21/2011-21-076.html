“Influentials in Co-creation” research results.

Posted on September 9, 2010 by Tim Meuleman,

Dear Redesigners,

It has been nearly 6 weeks since I requested you, on RedesignMe.com, to fill in my survey concerning my crowdsourcing thesis. I am pleased to announce that your responses have helped me a lot and resulted in a thesis which was satisfying on both scientific as on practical grounds!

Maxim has asked me to write a blog about my findings. Of course, I was honored. Summing up a study of 65 pages in such a small space is hard though, but to save everybody the always-good-to-have time and energy, I have tried my very best to obtain this. Furthermore, to benefit the level of your joy in reading this blog I decided to write it as a sort of interview. I hope you’ll enjoy it and more importantly that you’ll learn something from it. I most certainly did.

What was the aim?

In the last couple of years, more and more research has been done on crowdsourcing. Studies were often devoted to people’s motivations and in particular the motivations of one specific crowd. For example scientists, students, amateur photographers, t-shirt designers, open source software programmers and even computer-controlled music instruments developers were subjects of several scientific studies. One crowd that has never solely studied before are marketing and sales professionals (with at least 3 years of working experience). The objective was to find if there was any influence between several motivational rewards and the these professionals in order to participate in crowdsourcing communities.

Is crowdsourcing one of the first methods where the consumer is ‘used’ by a company?

No. The term crowdsourcing, together with the terms open innovation and co-creation, exist for no longer than 4 – 10 years, but before these terms were developed, similar practices used the force of voluntary labor as well. Even though independent expressions weren’t created before, the consumer already was partly used in the ‘new product development’ and the ‘idea generation’ processes. Furthermore companies also made big use of forms as ‘market research’ and ‘service recovery’ to learn and know more about the consumers in order to develop better, fitting, products. All of the formed expressions fit in the new product development process, of which most of them can be organized into the Fuzzy Frond End (the creative, messy, unstructured part of NPD). Because the complete terminology can be confusing I have formed an overview (see Figure 1), combining a number of scientific sources in order to distinguish the different used expressions.

Fig. 1 detailed close-up of the Fuzzy Front End from the NPD process

Some consider the relatively new terms to be a sort of trend or fashion. There are numerous highly respected scientists and business leaders that think differently about this though. According to them a fundamental change is occurring in the way products are developed. The fact that consumers are becoming more important and receive a more central role is a sort of ‘revolution’ in doing business, which is not something that will disappear in the short term.What were the most important motivational rewards that were found?

By studying a (great) number of scientific journals in total I have found 28 different motivational rewards that could influence one’s motivation in order to participate in a crowdsourcing community. Out of these 28 rewards, which were divided into intrinsic and extrinsic rewards, 4 seemed to make a big difference in a number of studies, devoted to a number of different crowds. As intrinsic rewards, ‘personal fun’ and ‘the possibility to develop skills’ seemed significantly important in nearly every study. Furthermore, as extrinsic rewards, the ‘monetary reward’ and ‘peer recognition’ were considered to be one of the biggest influencers as well. For this reason I decided to do research on these four motivational rewards concerning the motivation of marketing and sales professionals in crowdsourcing communities. Figure 2 gives an overview of this decision.

Fig. 2 Conceptual model

What are the more detailed aspects concerning the actual research part?

After sending the survey repeatedly as a ‘discussion’ to a number of LinkedIn groups that had mainly marketing and sales professionals as its members and after asking the same to the community of RedesignMe, in total 134 respondents filled in the questionnaire either partly or completely. 122 of them filled in the entire questionnaire, of which 73 were actual marketing and sales professionals. On this group a multiple logistic regression is used, but that’s a somewhat boring, technical story of which I will save you the trouble telling it. The most important thing to know is that every condition of the statistical analysis was obtained, meaning the results can considered to be significantly correct.

Considering the conclusion itself. What is the most important conclusion that this thesis has provided?

[Conclusion] Actually several, some unexpected, conclusions arose from this thesis, but because ‘time is money’ and this blog is becoming large enough as it is already, I will only state the most important conclusion. This single most important conclusion states that 3 out of the 4 stated hypotheses are incorrect. It turned out that ‘personal fun’, ‘monetary reward’ and ‘peer recognition’ are of no significant influence on the motivation of marketing and sales professionals in order to participate in crowdsourcing communities. This is in great contrast  with the results of earlier studies about other type of participants in crowdsourcing. This study shows that they are neither of positive as of negative influence. The possibility to develop skills is the only reward that actually has a significant positive influence. Figure 3 gives a graphically overview of these results

Fig. 3 Proven model

[Discussion] The reason why this deviation with other studies occurs can’t be scientifically explained in this thesis. My personal view on this is that marketing and sales professionals do not consider crowdsourcing communities as a hobby, but more as a sort of training. Apparently for most of them this is not a quick, lucrative business but more a good way to develop their own skills. The solutions that these professionals produce are of no real importance towards other professionals, as a sort of contest, but solely to their selves. Naturally, this view can’t be considered scientifically correct (yet). Future research should find it this is really the case. Until that moment this explanation is nothing more than a hunch. A hunch with a good foundation though, based on the scientific facts of this thesis.

[Implications] What is important to know now is that, if you are managing a crowdsourcing community solely focused on marketing and sales professionals, money should not be considered to be a main motivator. Nor should peer recognition and personal fun be. This thesis states that you should mainly focus on ways to improve your member’s skills as good as possible in order to get the best results from your community.

Furthermore, if you are thinking about doing research on the motivations of a different crowd, be cautious with your assumptions as the unexpected can occur.

For companies that are thinking about using the consumer more as a source for new product development, I would like to refer to my theoretical framework. Here, numerous examples of successful companies are given together with the opinion of numerous respected scientists of who most of them are quite positive towards this change of doing business. My only advice here is to follow their opinion and change your way of doing business as the opportunities are big and new visions can be followed together with new, lucrative, business models in nearly every branch!

If you are interested more in crowdsourcing or you disagree with one or several points, please do not hesitate to contact me. As it is with the crowdsourcing principle itself: the more views on this, the better the results and information will be obtained…
