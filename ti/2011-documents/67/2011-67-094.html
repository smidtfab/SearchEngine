    Are Crowdsourced Content Sites Dead?

    Posted on March 24, 2011 by Arica Mooney

    A recent Fox News article titled “Heads Up, HuffPro! The Mob Is Turning on Crowdsourcing”  suggests that sites featuring crowdsourced content are suffering on both ends- contributors are tiring of submitting content for free, and readers are getting smart and recognizing the possible biases and fallacies in user-generated content.

    The article, however, ignores that user-generated content and paid content, where contributors are compensated for their work, often have different readerships.  Sites which pay for their content, even if it’s sourced from a crowd rather than tradition journalism, are not suffering the same fate as sites relying on free, user generated content.

    Demand Media, which compensates users for contributions, claims that despite the “content farm” label some have placed on their sites such as eHow.com, there is still a strong market for well organized, useful content– even if it’s not winning any Pulitzer Prizes.

    As Demand Media CEO Richard Rosenblatt has said, “If 100,000 people are searching for how to make a paper lantern, 20,000 of them will end up at eHow.”

    The following chart shows the monthly visitor trend at eHow.com.

    There is always a place for content crafted by highly-skilled journalists. But by the same token, there are plenty of readers who just want to know how to make a paper lantern, and there is still plenty of room for widesourced paid content.

    Broadening your translation repertoire: Which foreign language should you learn (next)?

    Posted on February 9, 2011 by Mark Chatow

    Perhaps you’re already using your fluency in a second language to provide translation services, hopefully through CloudCrowd. (And if not, get credentialed now! Our clients have work for you!)

    Or perhaps you’ve always meant to learn a second language for fun, or your job, or so you can understand the telenovelas on Telemundo or enjoy imported Hong Kong martial arts films without dubbing. The potential to make some money on the side by contributing to the quickly growing translation industry is another great reason to make the plunge.

    Which second, or third, or fourth language should you learn? The most useful (and relatively easy) one is English, but if you can read this, we’re going to presume you have that one down.

    Best for business and (relatively) easy to learn

    Want to learn a useful second language without studying until 3 a.m. every night? Give these a shot.

    SPANISH — Mandarin might be the cool new kid on the block, but after English, Spanish remains the most useful language to know. It is the second-most spoken language in the United States, a country that boasts more than 500 Spanish-language newspapers. Being English-Spanish bilingual can be a big asset in getting a job, and English-Spanish translation skills are always in demand. It’s also comparatively easy to learn for speakers of other Romance languages (such as English).

    Alternate: GERMAN. So what if it’s not the loveliest language? It’s still extremely useful for business, with 200 million speakers worldwide (120 million native, 80 million nonnative) and the learning curve is relatively low for native English speakers.

    Big potential benefits, but high degrees of difficulty

    Prefer a challenge? These languages are growing in usefulness to native English speakers, but you’d better be willing to commit plenty of time and focus.

    MANDARIN — The advantages are obvious: English-Mandarin speakers are quite rare and thus in high demand, and the changes in China make Mandarin an increasingly useful language to know. However, keep in mind that while Mandarin is spoken in Mainland China, the vast majority of Hong Kong and Taiwan residents speak Cantonese. Mandarin also is one of the toughest languages to learn. You might only want to consider it if you’ve mastered a few other languages without great difficulty.

    Alternate: JAPANESE. Japan has one of the world’s largest economies and it is a massive trading partner with the United States. Written Japanese is a bit easier to learn than Mandarin, but it’s still extremely challenging.

    Cultural considerations

    What’s the point of learning a second language if you can’t show it off at a fine restaurant or on an overseas vacation? These languages balance business utility with style points.

    FRENCH — Though this isn’t the top dog for business considerations, it is still extremely useful: More than 500 million people worldwide speak it as their main language, with 200 million more as a second language. France also is the most visited country on earth. As a Romance language, it’s relatively easy to learn, it sounds beautiful, and is associated with a great appreciation for arts and culture.

    Alternate: KOREAN. Why? Because Italian is lovely, but it’s not useful. Korean is sort of a utility player: Although certainly more difficult to learn than the Romance languages, it is easier than Mandarin or Japanese. Korean has 71 million speakers worldwide, and learning it opens you up to a rich, deeply historic culture.

    “Therefore” and Nine Other Terms or Phrases Your Writing Probably Doesn’t Need

    Posted on February 4, 2011 by Mark Chatow

    One of the biggest mistakes writers make is stuffing their copy full of unnecessary words. A big part of professional proofreading and editing services is a focus on trimming these unneeded words to improve conciseness.

    The best way to communicate clearly and forcefully to your audience is to scour your writing for terms and phrases that can be easily omitted. Here are 10 to get you started:

    1. Therefore — Every sentence you write should follow naturally after the one that preceded it. If you’re doing that, there’s no need for therefore. It’s implied. This also holds true for moreover, furthermore, accordingly, hence, and other terms editing services commonly trim from students’ papers. These terms can sometimes improve clarity, but usually you can leave them out.

    2. In order to — The in order part can often be dumped in favor of simply to. However, retain in order if removing it would be confusing.

    3. Then — There are two ways then is unnecessarily used. The first is in an “if-then” statement, which relates to the logic problems we all intensely hated intently studied in high school. In writing, however, you most often should drop the then: If we go to Camp Crystal Lake, then Jason likely will kill us.

    The other way then is misused is reminiscent of a small child telling you a story: Then we went to the zoo and saw the zebras. Then we got pretzels and Pepsis. Then we went to see the elephants. Then Davey got sick from too many pretzels… As with therefore, then is often unnecessary if your sentences follow naturally from one another.

    4. The result was that — Because Davey ate too many pretzels, the result was that he felt sick the rest of the day. Dump this four-word sentence cholesterol before it clogs up your paper beyond repair.

    5. In light of the fact that / given the fact that — Replace with the simple because. (However, do not use since. This serves as a good opportunity to note that—despite its increasing informal use—since is not synonymous with because. You can probably get away with since in a highly informal document, but in professional proofreading, the standard is to make this because.)

    6. Despite the fact that — Similarly to the previous example, you can save yourself three words by replacing this with although.

    7. Whether or not — In many cases, the or not is unneeded. We are trying to decide whether or not to take Davey back to zoo, because it didn’t go well last time.

    8. To be — This treatment is considered to be effective.

    9. It should be noted that — You can almost always leave this out. Of course it should be noted; otherwise, you wouldn’t be noting it.

    10. The reason that…was to — The reason that Jason wore a hockey mask was to make sure he didn’t get a sunburn. Go with Jason wore a hockey mask so he wouldn’t get a sunburn.

    Those are just a few for now. Another big wordiness concern in professional proofreading is the sometimes-unnecessary use of that as a conjunction, but that is an issue best left for another time. Meanwhile, keep scrutinizing your copy (and copy you edit) for unneeded words.
