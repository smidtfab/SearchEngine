Crowdsourcing a better search engine using games

General Commentary

There are a lot of ways you can get the crowds to do something for a business or initiative, but one of the most creative and clever ones is to make a game out of it.

We already mentioned that one of the incentives for participants in crowdsourcing is the”addiction” element. But even in this cases, the game elements are sort of mingled in the system and are not a flat out game.

One of the examples of clearly a game, one that helps search engines to be better, is from a company called Gwap. Their first game (and most successful) was called ESP Game, and in it you are paired with another player, and both of you have to write labels to an image that appears onscreen. If you and the other player enter the same label, then it becomes a label to the image and it’s fed to earch engines image search.

This technology was originally created by Luis von Ahn from Carnegie Mellon University, and then built intothen licenced to Google, as Google Image Labeler with the same kind of features.

The game mechanics are obvious, and as you get more and more points you can earn several gifts and prizes, so the incentives are not just the fun of it (and it is fun, addicting really).

Other games from Gwap have been going live, ranging from tagging tunes, to matching images, to tracing pictures Â (they claim they can also find out your gender by asking you which images you like the lost from 10 pairs!) All this fuels search engines to make them better at what they usually suck the most, and teaching them how to see things as humans do.

So they’re doing all this by having created a service that allows you to have fun while doing work. Even if this wasn’t at all enough, they’re even giving you prizes if you get to a certain number of points.

Recently, Museums started using crowdsourcing to sort through their collection (like Victoria and Albert Museum in London), and also tag images (like the Brooklyn Museum), and while their system is not as elaborate as the ESP Game and other Gwap games, maybe they don’t need to be. Their goal isn’t as high as the previous games: to make search engines better.

Another example of this kind is an iPhone app called The Extraordinaries that lets you do small tasks in your spare time (its CEO calls it microvolunteering), from taking photos of your surroundings to tagging images, to helping raise awareness to social causes. Again, the goal here is quite different from ESP game, as are the mechanics (and the incentives of doing social good).

These are all good examples of crowdsourcing, but the ESP and GWAP games stand out to me as really smart ones, since they ask for something from the crowd but immediatly give back in the form of fun for playing a game.

It will be interesting to see in the future other models like these coming out, as it would be very interesting to see the level at which search engines have improved with the direct effect of this game.
