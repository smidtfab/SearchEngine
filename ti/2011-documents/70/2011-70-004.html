Super Bowl Aftermath: Crowdsourcing 1, Agencies 0


Click to enlarge

As the post-Super Bowl week analysis comes to a close, one winner appears to be the crowdsourcing model.

Exhibit A The memorable "Pug Attack", part of the Crash the Super Bowl contents sponsored by Doritos and now in its fifth year. A pug racing is for a chip that is being tantalizing held out to him behind a glass door. With blaring music and close up shots of his muscular race across the year, he successfully brings down the glass door on the hapless taunter. The last shot is of the pug gorging himself on a bag of Doritos.

It was, according to several agencies that monitor the reception to these ads, one of the most popular (via Gather).

Exhibit B is a Groupon ad that its CEO Andrew Mason has disavowed after deciding that it went too far. The ad, which appeared to make light of the situation in Tibet, were the creation of Crispin Porter + Bogusky, Fast Company reports.

Versions of the ad uploaded to YouTube by Groupon were set to "private" and not viewable by the general public.

The Collective Wisdom of Crowdsourcing

There is much to argue about the drawbacks of crowd sourcing: it can be time consuming, the voting can be rigged by participants in some cases (a complaint, interestingly enough by another Pepsi crowdsourcing initiative, its Refresh project). Still, however, as the Groupon ad makes clear, sometimes many voices - at least some of which may flag a possibly offensive ad - are better than just a few.
