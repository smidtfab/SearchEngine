DARPA Red Balloon Challenge

What: DARPA invited teams to race to identify the location of ten eight-foot-wide red weather balloons placed conspicuously across the United States. The winner would receive $40,000.

When: December 5, 2009

Who won: The MIT team

How long did it take: Approximately eight hours

How did they win: MIT apportioned the prize money such that the ten people credited with finding and reporting the balloons would each receive $2,000, the ten who shared the project with them $1,000 each, the ten who invited them $500 and the person who invited that person $250.

Mechanism: This functioned for users much like a lottery. The cost of signing up and participating was extremely low, and the potential benefit very high, with a certain monetary reward. Presumably people used their broad, weak links to increase the likelihood of being part of the winning team. The structure has been described as a reverse Ponzi scheme. The second-place team used a promise to give the entire reward to the American Red Cross to recruit spotters.

Under the hood: Despite the seeming simplicity of the project, the MIT team claimed use of a sophisticated (though undescribed) algorithm to assess whether reports were valid or not. A Scientific American article, however, stated that this team simply cross-checked IP addresses of reporting emails with the supposed location of the balloon, and requesting finders to submit photographic evidence.

Crowdsourced context: The contest itself was also an example of crowdsourcing, using a contest format and a cash prize to motivate highly dedicated teams in a dispersed environment to focus on a concrete objective.
