Companies are Opening up to Crowdsourcing


Introduction
Imagine being an out of work designer looking for your next job? Where would you look? Nowadays, designers can find non-traditional design work on websites such as CrowdSpring.com that offers companies the chance to work with many different designers, professional or amateur, and gives designers the chance to show off their work and get paid for their chosen designs [1]. This is just one example of what crowdsourcing is all about.


Body
What is crowdsourcing? Crowdsourcing is when a company takes a job and outsources it to a large group of people or to the general public, sometimes offering some type of pay or prize incentive, but not always [5]. Crowdsourcing is typically done using the internet through different websites or by using social networking sites. There are many different ways to crowdsource projects. A good example of crowdsourcing would a company holding a contest to design its new company logo or website, using the many ideas from a large group of people to create a design or product for your company rather than using a traditional design firm. Other examples of crowdsourcing include customers helping to create and influence new products using company created websites. Companies who have taken these creative measures include Nike, Dell and Starbucks [1].


Companies use crowdsourcing to tap into new ideas and concepts, find talented employees, market their products, and to save time and money. Facebook, who had used crowdsourcing in the past on different projects, wanted to see where else they could use it. They discussed creating a short animated film using crowdsourcing with Yair Landau, who is the head of the production company Bedouin Media. Landau then led the Mass Animation Project, partnering with Facebook, Dell and Intel to create a five minute animated film. Around 50,000 people on Facebook were fans of the project, while almost 17,000 people from all different countries participated in the creation of the film by either animating different sections, creating fan pages, or just by voting. Participants were rewarded $500 for each winning clip and computers were given away every week by Dell and Intel. This project ended up costing around $1 million and took less than a year to complete. Landau said, "Producing the same five minutes of film by traditional methods would have cost millions more and taken at least six months longer." [2] They were able to save time and money while creating a completely unique film using people from all around the globe.


There are many different crowdsourcing websites that have been created. CrowdSpring was started by Ross Kimbarovsky and Michael Samson and their website offers companies the chance to hold design competitions while offering a prize for as little as $200 on jobs such as company logos. On their website, customers can load some specifications about what they would like and then designers compete for the winnning design. CrowdSpring also offers a Pro option for larger companies who want their projects to be private. This professional option costs a minimum of $1,000 and also includes contestant screenings [4].


Samson says,
Quote:
The beauty of our site is that it doesn't matter if you have a degree from the Rhode Island School of Design or if you're a grandma in Tennessee with a bunch of free time and Adobe Illustrator. If the client likes the grandma's work better, then she's going to get the job [3].



Not everyone has been so accepting of the new idea of crowdsourcing. Many people mention how the designers who don't get picked have created specific designs for no pay at all and that can't be used for other projects [4]. Regardless of whether people are for or against crowdsourcing, it does open up so many doors for small businesses that can't afford to hire professional design firms and also for designers looking for work or people trying to get started in design for the first time.


Conclusion
Whether or not crowdsourcing will continue to grow in the future, only time will tell. For now it seems that it can be beneficial to both companies and participants. The ideas and options are endless when using such a large group of people from virtually anywhere in the world to help with projects. I think that crowdsourcing will not only continue to grow but might possibly continue to shape the design industry as well as others. It is a great opportuinity for people to have their designs noticed and to give small designers a chance to work with some really great companies.

Are businesses prepared for crowdsourcing?
As LGrundy stated in the post, "Companies Are Opening Up to Crowdsourcing", companies in need of solutions use crowdsourcing
as a vehicle to present problems to the masses. Crowdsourcing is a type of outsourcing which generally utilizes the Internet. The main difference is that outsourcing involves a problem for a specific vendor, whereas crowdsourcing involves a specific problem for no particular vendor.

Crowdsourcing has been used in many industries. Creative endeavors seem to top the list - logo designs, photography, and clothing designs. However, companies searching for solutions to complex scientific questions have taken advantage of crowdsourcing. Some of the business utilizing the crowdsourcing websites are not small, just-starting businesses, although those businesses can benefit from hiring the crowd, too. Procter & Gamble and Colgate-Palmolive are just a couple of the
larger firms fishing for ideas. Of course, these companies tend to pay a little more for innovations, so they often attract those with specialized knowledge. Other crowdsourcing websites pay next-to-nothing, if anything, for the completion of tasks that humans are better equipped for than computers [2].

Changes are sure to come in the way businesses handle this new workforce. Some of the critics of the crowdsourcing technology claim companies are merely looking for cheap labor. Why pour money into research and development when you can find willing participants to work for little compensation? Some worry that this necessarily means cutting research and development staff. However, before the term crowdsourcing was coined in 2006, many companies were cutting the time and money
spent on the research and development of new products. Oftentimes, product design was outsourced or companies would capitalize on someone else's idea - either by imitation or purchasing the rights [4].

From a marketing perspective, crowdsourcing can be seen as an extension of consumer research. Not quite like a focus group nor a survey, crowdsourcing can provide insight into what consumers want. Another concern is taht someone who might have ill-feeling toward the company might take advantage of this opportunity to air grievances. Of course, the threat of something like this is usually present in even the most carefully planned market research. Another potential advantage is
that the marketing and advertising is occuring before the product has even come to fruition [3].

Managers will still need to make decisions regarding the use of this technology and must question whether implementing crowdsourcing provides a competitive advantage. Decisions involving the benefits of possible cost-reduction must be weighed against the potential vulnerabilities the company might face. Companies will most likely crowdsource those tasks which are not seen as being the companies' core competencies and strengths [4]. If workers are displaced, managers must deal with the consequences of the companies' actions, to those inside and outside the firm. Companies that choose to crowdsource must keep
in mind that a certain degree of control over the product and the process will most likely be lost. They must also examine privacy and copyright issues as if the idea came from one of their employees [1].

However, crowdsourcing does not necessarily mean jobs lost. Restructuring may be needed, as ways to handle the information becomes more important than creating the idea. Some of these websites are dealing with thousands of ideas, but don't have the proper technology in place to make sense of the information being gathered. In addition, some of the ideas and designs turn out to be a waste of time. Filters would make the use of these websites more cost-efficient. Crowdsourcing doesn't always conserve the companies' resources. Sometimes, more money and time is spent in the process. Although, some websites
allow users to vote until the best of the best are left, and this is when the company steps in [5].

Jeff Howe, the reporter who coined the term crowdsourcing, believes that not all companies will know how to process the information that can come from the masses, and these companies will not benefit from crowdsourcing. It seems the most important step businesses can take is to be innovative with their handling of the crowds' innovations [5].

How Mozilla is Using Crowdsourcing
The number of people employed by Mozilla, the maker of Firefox, has grown in the last few years but they still have about 40% of their work done by volunteers. Crowdsourcing is working well for Mozilla and their volunteers are really contributing to their business. Recently a legally blind volunteer created a tool for their new video player on the Firefox browser that made the player more user friendly for people with vision problems. Recently, Mozilla wanted to add a private browsing mode to their website which wouldn't record history when a user was looking up things on the internet. Mozilla developers couldn't find a way to develop it yet a volunteer could. This is just another example of how Mozilla is using outside people to further the technology developed by their company.
Mike Beltzer, who runs Firefox says,
Quote:
There's structure in it. But at the same time you allow people to innovate and to explore and [give them] freedom to do what they want along those edges - that's where innovation tends to happen in startling and unexpected ways [1].

Research Post: Web 2.0 and Crowdsourcing Applications in Business
Introduction:

As discussed in the previous research post entitled;"Companies are Opening up to Crowdsourcing," crowdsourcing is
a way of letting anyone and everyone design or have a say in business decisions, where the best ideas are usually rewarded
with monetary prizes and awards. While crowdsourcing is a fairly new and creative way to cut costs from the companies bottom
line, by only paying for good ideas, it is just another application of Web 2.0. Web 2.0 is a term invented by Tim O'Reilly,
which refers the the second generation of the World Wide Web, that enables platforming for Web-based communities of interest,
collaboration, and hosted services [6]. Web 2.0 supports mashing, which essentially creates new programs and services from
reusable components, applications include many topics that have been discussed in the forum this semester, including but
not limited to social bookmarking, calendaring, VoIP, and Really Simple Syndication (RSS) [6]. These are certainly not all
the possible outcomes and services that can be reached using web 2.0 but are some of the more popular in terms of general
acceptance and use. A further look at Web 2.0 and all the ways it can be utilized will be examined and relevant examples
will be discussed.

Web 2.0

As previously mentioned Web 2.0 was pioneered by Tim O'Reilly, in which the web could be used as a platform without boundaries. At the center of this concept would be a platform, in which the users control their own data, and emphasize participation, cost-effective scalability, remixable data sources and data transformations, and collective intelligence [3]. The idea is to increase the sharing of knowledge and information and that in turn leads to competitive advantages through innovative business
processes utilizing new technology. If companies would be more reluctant to allow the flow of information, then new and improved ideas would be developed faster and at a lower cost. This is done in many ways, including the use of:

* Blogs
* Large knowledge repositories such as Wikipedia
* BitTorrent
* Gmail
* Ebay and Amazon

These are just a few of the common ways that Web 2.0 is being used to create businesses and even entire industries that could not have existed even 10 years ago. These applications when used correctly with the right business processes can lead to afore mentioned core competencies which would include; services, not packaged software, control over unique data sources, trusting users as co-developers, harnessing collective intelligence, customer self-service, and lightweight user interfaces just to name a few [3]. There are currently thousands of companies using some form of Web 2.0 to enhance either their business processes, services, or both. Here are a few present day examples:

U.S. Patent System Using Web 2.0 to Crowdsource

Currently the U.S. patent office is using a Peer-to-Patent website in which it uses the internet to tap the wisdom of the masses [2]. This is done by using search engines to search through the database of older patent applications. If the technology can find any prior evidence or the existence of similar innovations and therefore doesn't deserve a patent [2]. The goal is to find previous patents that actual people that work for the patent office may not have found on their own. This will increase the level of innovation as the ability to get a patent will become harder to attain, therefore making the ideas better. This would also reduce the number of patent related lawsuits [2]. This is a different way of using the crowdsourcing idea, but it certainly has changed the way the patent office works. Patents can be reviewed and processed much faster which decreases costs and improves service to the American people.

Ernst & Young Recruit New Talent Using Facebook

Currently Ernst and Young have over 17,500 'fans' on its Facebook web page [1]. This has allowed the company to find more
quality applicants because it is cheaper than screening all candidates via telephone or some other media, and it is also
more efficient, the computer can pre-screen before the actual interviewer pre-screens, which leads to looking at a greater
number of candidates and then narrowing them down to only the higher quality ones. E&Y also have a discussion board which helps
applicants decide if E&Y is even right for them, before they would take the process any further [1].

BestBuy Receives Employee Feedback

BestBuy launched blueshirtnation.com about two years ago in an attempt to gain insight from its employees about how certain
products work and what processes and strategies seem to work best [1]. This has given the firm a huge advantage that was not
even practical a decade ago. Employees feel more a part of the company when they are allowed to speak their minds. The firm
becomes aware of corporate strategic faults much quicker than before. Problems can be solved much faster as someone with
first-hand knowledge can give an opinion at anytime for all of management to see. The site is relatively inexpensive and
it is hard to put a number on the amount of money it has made the company.

Turbo-Tax Uses Others to Field Common Questions

The idea of this Web 2.0 application is easy. A Turbo-Tax customer needing help with the software and their taxes goes to the customer-support chat room for help. The question will be answered by an employee but may be answered by another customer first if the employee has yet to answer [1]. The employees will confirm that the question was answered correctly and post it for all to see. This may sound like its not that inventive of an idea, but the truth is that questions are answered faster and fewer questions are answered because the more answered questions stored on the website means a reduction in redundant questions. This Web 2.0 program shares all the traits of the other applications and results in the same higher levels of customer service and lower costs.

The main application of Web 2.0 is crowdsourcing. An easier way to define crowdsourcing would be to describe a system, that
continually builds off of the collective knowledge of its users. Currently it is used for helping to name new products and
services, designing new services, finding better and more efficient programmers, software testing and feedback, and word of
mouth marketing (through blogs) [4].

Conclusion

Web 2.0 tools and applications certainly have changed the way companies do business and the levels of service and product
qualities that customers expect. With more and more companies beginning to use these technologies, better more efficient business
models will result. It also allows smaller firms with little capital to jump into the game because of the very low start-up costs and barriers to entry. Firms will be able to know exactly what customers want and when they want it. These new processes will eventually lead to higher levels of innovation and increased standards of living. It is hard to say were society will be 10 or even 20 years from now, but it is certain that the average person or the average Joe will be apart of the growth and decisions to follow.
