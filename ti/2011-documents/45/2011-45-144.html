Robotics for Gentlemen

November 23rd, 2010 § Leave a Comment

Good Evening.

To repeat the circumstances, to which you may be savvy, I received this injury on my shoulder many years ago – on a night I shall never forget. I was riding on my elephant in the Osmanic Colonies, when my butler, Jeeves, walked over, rather exhausted, and delivered to me the results of the contest I had been organizing for the last month – a full and thorough vetting of the renowned and infamous Mechanical Turk.

This is the face of the machine that shall drive our finest chess players out of a job.

We had been touring around our Mechanical Turk in an attempt to test its worth – a touring test, if you will. You see, my colleague, Sir Hanske at the Institute for Gentlemanly Sciences had constructed what he called a ‘labori‘, a mechanical man, designed for a simple number of tasks. This was many years ago – the precursor to what my psychic, Mr. Cayce, a kindly old Greek chap, had assured me will one day be known as the ‘robot‘, or the ‘computer‘.

I should have known those psychic transmissions could never reach his head through that bowler hat.

My colleague, Sir Hanske, a Finnish gentleman, tells me that this labori works like a butler: it is a simpler version of man, designed to accept input and output in the forms of ones and zeroes, akin to a butler accepting instructions and responding only in the dry, flat tones of ‘Yes, sir’ and ‘No, sir.’ Mr. Cayce assured me that when, in the future, such labori are instructed to speak, they will retain the flat, distinctly British tone of the butler, a tone so laborious to cultivate.

No, not this Butler.

Such Computers, Gentlemen, are the subject of much fascination – and are to be the topic of many further discussions, but I would simply like to outline for you the workings of such a Computer. They do what you tell them – no more, no less. Like butlers, they are intelligent and diligent, but cannot think for themselves. They require maintenance and fine-tuning, and it as we are dependent upon our butlers now, Gentlemen, so we shall be dependent on our computers in this ‘future’ Mr. Cayce always went on about. Here is his description of the future:

“The world will be a dark place – full of tiny metal men, obeying our every whim. Like the opium I cannot live without, so shall be the nature of the future – or, rather, the un-nature of it. For there shall be no trees in this future, and the district of York shall have been rebuilt even more terribly and with even more destruction of property than it is now. These are terrible times, to be sure, but it only gets worse from here on out.” – Mr. Cayce.

I stray. The point, Gentlemen, is that I organized this touring test to prove that Sir Hanske was mistaken – that such a labori could never best a gentleman such as myself in intellect. Of course, I could never be bothered to compete myself, so I hired the finest gentlemen in the Colonies to play this Mechanical Turk in the fine game of Chess. The contest took hours. Sir Hanske was determined to beat me. Towards the end of the third day, I got cocky – I had been celebrating, pre-emptively, it seems, my victory, which Mr. Cayce had predicted with his psychic abilities.

I suppose all those fuzzy hats did not possess the Chess genius I so desired.

Mr. Cayce and I had gone out to the Timbuktu marketplace, as he wanted some new apparel to replace the thrift-store clothing he was generally inclined to wear as a psychic. As he was trying it on in the changing room, I waited outside, sipping my champagne on the back of my elephant. Suddenly, Jeeves ran up, astonished. I turned to him, and he informed me that this Mechanical Turk had bested my finest Chess players and defeated my challenge. Upon the reception of this news, I fell off my elephant and onto Jeeves, who kindly broke my fall.

And that, Gentlemen, is how I received this injury on my shoulder. Needless to say, I fired Mr. Cacye on the spot. Never trust Greeks wearing Thrifts.

Of course, this is how I had the idea for the rubber contraceptive and made my fortune.

And so, this was my first foray into Robotics. Gentlemen, you will find that one of those among you is, in fact, a labori. I will leave it to the House Detective to determine which of you it is. That will be all. Godspeed, Gentlemen.

- Charles Westerford-Smythe
