Can Games Change the World? The Founders of Gameful Think So.

I just became a backer of this project on Kickstarter. It’s called Gameful, and the idea is to build a network that will allow collaborators, programmers, artists, idealists, and others who want to make games that make a positive impact in people’s lives to come together and make big things happen.

Want some examples? Check out: EVOKE, Fold It!, The Epic Win App, Flower, Code of Everand, SuperBetter, The MP Expenses Game, Budgetball, the Pokéwalker, Quest to Learn, Little Big Planet: Gamechangers, World Without Oil, Seek ‘n Spell, Goal Mafia,and Conspiracy for Good.

For more, check out Jane McGonigal’s TED talk, “Gaming can make a better world”

    Very interesting. Thanks once again for a very thought-provoking post.

    I’ve watched my mother sitting on the couch doing Sudoku hour after hour for years. And I’ve often thought about the immense computation going on in her head and millions of other heads across the world to solve the little puzzles they plough through. And I’ve often thought, “If only you could harness that power to solve some big problem, like cancer.” What if, somehow, with all these people playing games and enjoying their blissful productivity, we could somehow harness their effort to do something hugely useful. I’ve thought about this a lot.

    This is similar to what you’re talking about. Especially “The MP Expenses Game”, which of course isn’t really a game. It’s just harnessing the power of thousands of people to farm out a really expensive task to thousands of people.

    I stumbled across this http://video.google.com/videoplay?docid=-8246463980976635143 a few years ago*, and the resulting games, The ESP Game (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESP_game) and Peekaboom (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~biglou/Peekaboom.pdf), really blew me away. In The ESP Game you’re paired with a partner (a random fellow human who happens to be on-line at the same time). You’re both presented with the same image. You each have to type in a word that best describes the image. When you both type the same thing you’re presented with a different image and you both get a point. Either player can “pass” which means you both move on to the next image and the person passing drops a point. This game of course generates metadata (tags) for the images (which could be scraped from flickr for example). Peekaboom was the really impressive thing. Again, you’re paired with a random (human) partner. One takes the turn of “revealer” and the other is the “guesser”. The revealer is presented with an image and a word; the guesser is presented with a blank screen and a text box. The revealer uses his/her mouse to click on the part of the image that best represents the word. As he/she clicks, that part of the image is revealed on the guesser’s screen. The guesser’s job is to type guess the word.

    So – I upload a holiday snap of me and my wife standing in front of some mountain. The ESP Game acquires the image and presents it to two randoms. Both at some point type the word “mountain” which is now associated with my image. Later, Peekaboom presents the same image to the revealer, together with the word “mountain”. Immediately the revealer starts to click on the mountain (not on me and my wife), which is the quickest way to get his/her partner to guess the word “mountain”. So, from nothing but a picture, we not only have the metadata “mountain” _but we also know where on the image the mountain actually is!!_. That’s awesome. And of course, at some point The ESP Game would have generated the words “man” and “woman”, and Peekaboom would have logged where on the image me and my wife are standing. Amazing. And the games are fun too. And suddenly millions of public domain images are being tagged every second by people who are having a fun time, and Google Image Search gets a whole lot better for everyone.

    There’s more on this topic: check out Amazon’s Mechanical Turk (and research the amusing meaning behind the name while you’re at it!). Tagline: “artificial Artificial Intelligence”. I once earned about $3 tagging “assets” (lampposts, drains) on pictures of streetscenes uploaded by an innovative British local authority that had taken pictures of their streets and wanted to update the database they have as to where all their lampposts are. The most interesting thing about that was that the tagging itself was a “Level 1″ task: once you graduated to “Level 2″, you moved on to a different task where you’re shown pictures of streetscenes complete with tags and you have to score the accuracy of the tags. You get paid more for Level 2 tasks as well -it’s like you’ve been promoted to Supervisor. In other words – it’s self-correcting! Any streetscene tagged incorrectly by someone doing Level 1 is rejected by a Level 2-er and can be put back straight into Level 1 for someone else to re-tag. All this without the “client” – the local authority in this case – ever having to see the incorrectly tagged streetscene. Interestingly, if this happened, the Level 1-er didn’t get paid for those (incorrect) tags, so the incentive structure is in place too. The most amazing thing about Mechanical Turk? It has an *API* infront of it. Meaning – some computer somewhere calls a method that takes an image as a parameter and returns a matrix of tags. It’s a very slow-running method, but that doesn’t matter… the fact is that you now have an API you can call that can perform some task that a computer cannot perform. So you have a building block you can use in some overarching grandiose task. By the way, I couldn’t cash my $3 because I don’t have a US bank account – the b*stards.

    This is all super interesting stuff – it really amazes me how the world gets more and more efficient every single day – possibly the efficiency of the human race as a whole is actually increasing exponentially – as we invent ever cleverer ways to achieve new things.

    So where do you go from here? Well I love ESP and Peekaboom, but they’re not “generic” – they are highly customised, clever solutions for one specific problem – tagging images. If only there was a way to make this generic – a framework where given any real-world problem a game can be created that allows bored humans to get a kick out of solving it. This is where I totter on the fringe of madness, of course. But then again, that’s what they would have said to Turing in 1930. In 1930 everyone in the world was using _custom made physical machinery_ to crank out specific tasks – e.g. insurance actuarial table calculation – a different physical machine consisting of cogs and metal and oil for every specific task. In 1950 Turing produced ONE PHYSICAL MACHINE THAT CAN SOLVE ANY COMPUTATIONAL PROBLEM. And I’m using one right now – the Universal Turing Machine. So maybe one day we’ll be able to harness game-players’ powers to solve any generic problem at all – and *THAT* is where Jane McGonigal comes in.

    Food for thought.
