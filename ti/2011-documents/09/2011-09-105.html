Resources to keep your logo sharp

Every business needs a logo. It's an important part of marketing any product or service. Whether you don't like your logo or just want to give it a more up to date look, there are many great resources on the Web to make this less of a chore. Why would you want to edit your logo? Well maybe the first version was done by an intern who thought that a logo could be made with Microsoft Word. Or perhaps you've added services to your business' portfolio that don't come across in your first version. Whatever the reason, it's a good idea to keep your logo and marketing materials current so that your customers and potential customers know that you can change as they do. Let's get started!

9 Essential Logo Resources  

1. 99Designs.com - 99designs enables businesses to quickly and inexpensively source custom designed logos, and other graphical element by tapping into a community of over 32,000 graphic designers. Clients simply post a description of what they are looking to have designed, and what they are willing to pay for their project.

2. SixRevisions.com - An effective logo sets the tone and public perception of your brand, and therefore careful thought and creativity must be put into constructing it. In this article, you'll find a large collection of tutorials and resources on the topic of designing logos by some of the most well-respected logo designers in the industry.

3. LogoDesignLove.com - From time to time I'll see a list of "logo design resources" being circulated, but more often than not, I'm left disappointed with the quality of links. So I've searched my bookmarks, culminating in this choice selection of useful websites and articles curated by the experts at LogoDesignLove.

4. LogoPond.com - LogoPond showcases the best in identity work from around the web. Logo artist from all development stages and areas of the world frequent this site. You will find thousands of images that will provide creative inspiration for any logo projects you may be working on for your own business or clients.

5. LogoMaid.com - An assortment of exclusive and non-exclusive logo designs to meet any corporate identity requirement. Their fully customizable logo templates can be implemented with minimum fuss. Because your logo is not just a fancy graphic adorning your website or stationery - it's a representation of your business.

6. ElitebyDesign.com - Here you can find 105+ logo design tutorials and resources to help you take your logo design to the next level. These resources are meant to give you the tools you need to design the perfect logo. However, nothing will help you more than practice, discipline, and endurance. So enjoy...

7. LogoWorks.com - With Logoworks, you don't have to sacrifice quality even if your custom design project is on a tight deadline. Once you send them a design brief about your company and project, their designers immediately go to work to create your new company logo. You'll see your first logo design concepts in just 3 business days!

8. PixelLogo.com - Looking for a logo design that will give your new business the edge, but won't break the bank? Pixellogo may be the answer! They're a professional graphic design agency that takes the cost out of creating a new company identity. Now there's no need to spend thousands on a logo design.

9. SmashingMagazine.com - Here you'll find a collection of 40 more beautiful and creative logo designs to inspire you. Browsing through the showcase, you will notice many trends. Some of these include the usage of simple shapes to create something complex, origami shapes, coloring and transparency trends, and many more.

What is crowdsourcing? 

Putting the power of the crowd to work for your business

Crowdsourcing is one of the latest Web 2.0 buzzwords. The nature of crowdsourcing is that it takes tasks that were historically completed by full-time resources or outside contractors and puts the work to be completed into the hands of a group of unknown people. Sound scary? It's really not. Let's walk through an example.

Let's say that you want to have a logo designed for your pizza business. You can use a logo crowdsourcing website to have a ton of great designs created in a short period of time. You start with a design brief, which is any direction about your logo project that you can detail to the designers. If you have some illustrations of what you are hoping for or any reference materials, you can upload them as well. On this crowdsourcing website, designers read your brief and decide whether to enter your logo contest. They can also ask you questions about different implementation options.

When a designer decides to work on your logo design project, they submit the designs through the website. You, the contest holder, can review them one at a time while also rating them and providing feedback. At the end of the contest, you decide which designer has submitted the best logo and that individual wins the contest prize. The rest of the submissions are not compensated in any way.

The pitfalls of this type of design are pretty simple - the designer may not be compensated for their time that they invest. In some cases, hundreds and even thousands of submissions are entered for a logo contest. This all is dependent on the subject matter (how interesting the contest is), the prize money and how easy the contest holder is to work with. When you provide lots of great feedback, you will get more submissions as the designers feel that they have a better idea of what the contest is all about.

When you need a logo from scratch 

Comparing the costs and quality of from-scratch logo services

First, let's say you have a friend, relative or eager intern that can create a logo for you. On the plus side, they are probably cheap and available for you to meet in person to get your logo design project off the ground. The problem is, if you use a personal resource, you may feel like you have to keep them happy instead of keep your eye on the prize - getting a great logo. For this reason, unless you are totally strapped for cash, this is not a good idea. Just because someone has the software needed for a project doesn't mean they have the means to listen to your ideas and can effectively incorporate them into a design. More often than not, you will have a logo that your friend enjoys making and not something that is effective for your business.

Second, you can consider hiring a logo designer. It's important to look over their portfolio before setting up a time to chat about the project. Has the designer had a wide variety of projects? Do the logos all tend to look the same? If the logos seem to have a large amount of common traits despite the designs being for different industries, you may have a one-track mind designer. This should be avoided at all costs.

Design firms work very much like independent logo designers except they are usually much more per hour or per project.

The last option to discuss is crowdsourcing. We don't mean last as 'last resort' just as another option. Each project and person has unique requirements that will determine the best route to take. We'd suggest crowdsourcing if you don't have an exact design in mind. Crowdsourcing is a great resource if you want to get a ton of ideas and then refine from a single idea that you like best. Sometimes you can do this refining in the original crowdsource project, and sometimes you need to take the closest submission and have a local design make the final refinements.

How to write a logo design brief 

Paramount to any logo design process is that you create a logo design brief. Even if you are a great speaker, some folks may not catch every detail that you attempt to get across in a conversation. Also, many arguments can be avoided by capturing as many important aspects of your logo design project up front as possible. We realize it's not always fun and interesting to write down your goals for a project such as when creating a logo, but it's time well spent.

Here are some items to consider including in your logo design brief:

1. Company profile - Summarize what your company is all about. Do you do business locally, over the phone, via mail order or on the Web?

2. Your goals - Is the goal of your logo that people understand everything about what your business does or sells or does it set an image? Neither is the correct answer and depends on the type of business that the logo is for. Many legal firms want their last names in the logo, while consumer products that wouldn't make sense at all.

3. Target audience - Are you targeting expecting moms or Harley-riding thrill-seekers? The audience in this and most cases matters. Even huge brands such as Coke or Pepsi try to build their business by targeting their brand messages to one audience or another.

4. Design ideas - If you want your logo to incorporate a vintage bicycle, get that down on paper. Don't assume that the designer will come to that same conclusion. If you have a preference for a certain image but are open to other ideas, let that be known as well. Consider the design brief a brain dump. The more information that you can give your designer(s), the better the outcome.

5. Design examples - Even if you don't have a general sense of the direction you want the logo to take, if there are websites, logos or other examples of design and illustration that you like, pass them along to the designer. Most talented designers focus on getting their client to say 'approved' as quickly as possible and rather than refining your ideas over revision and revision, it's better to get out on the table exactly the style you are looking for.
