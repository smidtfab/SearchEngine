I'd be a lot more active on GalaxyZoo if I could do it more easily from my iPhone.  I think better mobile support would really boost the project (especially on iPhone... if only for the marketing value!)

I think a mobile-specific website would be better than a stand-alone app.  I mean, even an app is going to have to download the images... I don't see a way around the over-the-air bandwidth requirements besides reducing the amount of data being transferred.  (clearly, the database is too big to include WITH the application!)  A mobile site would do this by eliminating the top banner or offering a smaller version, getting rid of Flash, and... well, I don't know what all they do, but they're fast on mobile devices. Many of the sports sites are moving to this model: see nhl.com and mlb.com; you will be directed to m.nhl.com or m.mlb.com when accessed from a mobile device.  Look at them again from a regular computer or laptop to see the differences between the mobile and non-mobile versions. 

A mobile site, rather than an app, would be a benefit users of *all* mobile devices, too.  This makes it a much better investment for a group that's possibly limited by research dollars.  Device-specific apps could be produced to simply link to a mobile version of the site.  A good example of this is Google's iPhone app... it just links to their many mobile-specific sites.  You wouldn't notice except that you see Safari load (on an iPhone.)

Third, a mobile site might be more appealing to the good folks running the project because they don't have to give 3rd parties API-level access to their database... THEY still own the code (and can be blamed for bugs. )  As a scientist on this project, I'd be concerned about a 3rd party writing an app that posts directly to my database, simply because the consequences of a bug could be so devastating to the data set. 

That's my 2 cents (or 5, as it may be.)  I hope I haven't been too discouraging.   Mobile GalaxyZoo is a great idea, no matter what form it comes in!

-Brian

Application Programming Interfaces is something I know little about, and I am not yet an iphone user.
It would seem to me that although the application programming might be achievable with people's
kind help, the main sticking point would be the licensing.

The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) own these pictures, and so presumably you would need to get their
agreement to use their pictures, which in GZ2's case is over a quarter of a million of them. Also, who would
be making money out of this- Apple perhaps?

I am absolutely in favour of differing ways of GZing, but the people to talk to are surely the people who
own the pictures. I have a feeling that lots of legalities would have to be talked through before your, our,
dream could become a reality. I just write this as once upon a time I was going to use their pictures in
a poster; I contacted them but never heard back!

Difficult, especially where dosh is involved (as it would have to be in this), even with voluntary code writing.
