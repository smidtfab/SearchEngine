Can Drug Development Benefit from the Crowdsourcing Model?

Posted on09 September 2010. Tags: crowdsourcing, drug development, orphan drugs

A business model based on crowdsourcing and “the only metric that matters”—bringing needed drugs to market—has made the nonprofit Medicine in Need (MEND) an interesting case study in how we can develop drugs not traditionally viewed as viable or lucrative. MEND taps a global network of scientists to find ways to breathe life into formulations that were left for dead, or to reformulate successful molecules so that they’re more practical (e.g., no refrigeration needed) for the developing world.

Its business model is certainly not one that for-profits can emulate, but perhaps learn from. As MEND CEO Andrew Schiermeier says in the video below, the organization aims to survive without charitable donations. Rather, it aims to “disrupt the status quo”, forsake a brick-and-mortar approach, and instead enlist the support of a “virtual network of technology practitioners”—aka, crowdsourcing. (The Crowdsourcing Blog by Wired’s Jeff Howe is a great resource for those who want to learn more. Howe’s quick definition of crowdsourcing: The application of Open Source principles to fields outside of software.)

MEND intends to license its intellectual property to drug companies interested in emerging markets. Royalties from the licenses go right back into the company to further its mission. This strategy, Schiermeier claims, will allow MEND and its licensing partners a “reasonable” economic return for their investments, even in the developing world.

How does MEND measure success? The only metric that matters, Schiermeier says, is the introduction of a new or improved drug or vaccine to a country that needs it.

It’s a noble mission, and an innovative approach. Even in the dog-eat-dog world of for-profit drug manufacturing, could crowdsourcing—tapping the collective expertise of global science and technology professionals—lower the costs of bringing products to market?

–Paul Thomas
