CloudCrowd Takes On CrowdFlower To Outsource Labor To The Cloud
  • Tweet
  • View Comments
Leena Rao
Mar 19, 2010

TechCrunch50 startup CrowdFlower has been attracting a lot of attention, even raising $5 million in funding recently. CrowdFlower is a labor as a service startup that helps businesses outsource mundane or repetitive tasks to the cloud. Now the startup has attracted a competitor, recently launched CloudCrowd, which also promises increase efficiency and lower costs to companies by breaking large projects into smaller tasks, and distributing them to its virtual workforce.

Once a client assigns a task to CloudCrowd, the company will distribute tasks its work force of more than 100,000 workers. With each task completed, a worker earn a credibility rating that determines the types of tasks they are offered. Workers who don’t have a rating yet are assigned basic tasks until they develop a reputation. Workers are able to see how much each separate task pays, and earnings are distributed through PayPal.

Tasks range from content moderation, internet research, audio and video transcription to data entry. Since the company’s launch a few months ago, CloudCrowd has completed over 500,000 tasks for a variety of clients, including USC and RentCycle.

As I wrote above CloudCrowd will face competition from CrowdFlower, but it seems that the model is attracting the attention of businesses to perform mundane tasks, so there should be room fr several players in the space.

  • http://www.cloudcrowd.com Alex Edelstein

    Alex the CEO here. I thought I’d answer some of the comments.

    A few people asked about how we compare to Mechanical Turk.

    We respect Mechanical Turk as a pioneer, like SETI At Home, in the space, but we started the company because we saw that limitations in their 1st generation system prevented it from being used for real, meaty (and sometimes high-paying) work.

    Some of the elements incorporated into CloudCrowd that make us a “2nd generation” crowdsourcing platform:

    1) Unlike Mturk, Cloudcrowd has a real mechanism for tracking the quality of workers.

    Mturk tracks the amount of times a worker was paid, but that’s not very accurate because it simply measures whether the worker got paid, and since clients either have to pay, or go each answer by hand, they invariably pay.

    You can’t get real, scalable work done if you have to go through each answer by hand!

    CloudCrowd addresses these issues at a very primal level. Almost all work done on the CloudCrowd Workspace is peer-reviewed. That means that after one worker does some work, another worker is paid to quickly review it. This constant feedback enables CloudCrowd to track the credibility of each worker. Indeed, each worker gets a distinct Credibility rating.

    Another challenge for people trying to get work done on Mturk is that there are no tools for linking simple projects into a complex whole. A typical Mturk project is an isolated standalone project. A typical CloudCrowd project has 4-6 interlocking stages, with all sorts of feedback loops. If you think about an auto assembly line, each individual robot or workstation is responsible for one repetitive action. But by carefully linking these actions together with a good quality system, the factory can turn out very complex products. At CloudCrowd the work to link the projects together is done for you.

    Other elements unique to CloudCrowd include a multi-stage referral program that pays workers for work done by their Personal Crowd, an Appeals system that allows workers to challenge rejections, a colorful and convenient Facebook interface, and a PayPal-based payment system that opens our platform up to workers and work providers from around the world.

  • http://www.cloudcrowd.com Mark

    Mechanical Turk is more like a “day labor” market, while CloudCrowd has built the tools to help businesses create “virtual factories”.

    CloudCrowd can handle much more complex projects and workflows than Mechanical Turk. Output from one task can flow immediately into another task for review by other workers, and then on to another task or project for additional processing.

    CloudCrowd also has a large library of worker interface tools that they will customize for specific business applications.

    If you have ever submitted a project on Turk, you know that one of the biggest problems is figuring out how to tell if the work submitted is actually any good.

    Too many people put up 1,000 (or 10,000) tasks on Turk and then don’t have time to sift through all of the answers to weed out the gamers, spammers and just plain wrong answers. They’ll often just accept everything– which means that every worker who did a task in that project just had their “approval” rating go up, even if they were blatantly scamming.

    CloudCrowd has a much more robust credibility system. Every task is reviewed by other workers, typically multiple times, and that data is tracked to create a complete and accurate work history for every worker on the system.

    This means that businesses don’t have to do the filtering and extensive review they typically have to do with work posted on Turk.

    It also means that if a worker does bad work they’re quickly suspended from the system.

    The technology was developed by Jordan Ritter, founder of Cloudmark– which protects more than a billion email subscribers from spam.

    From the worker perspective, CloudCrowd pays every business day via PayPal. Mechanical Turk pay is dependent on when the person who submitted the work feels like paying (assuming the approve the work fairly).

    CloudCrowd also has built-in appeals which allow a worker to easily request that a rejected task get a closer look.

    CloudCrowd is also a Facebook application, and allows workers earn money when friends they invite to the application do work– without any annoying wall updates.

  • http://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=609818489 Mark Chatow

    Freelancer, eLance, and oDesk have their place, but they are really going after a different market than CloudCrowd.

    People who try high volume projects on these first generation crowdsourcing sites realize quickly that it it’s not about how many workers a site has, but rather how you access, manage, and review the workforce.

    For larger projects it would take a huge amount of time to post jobs, screen workers, check the results, and manage the workflow on sites like Freelancer, eLance, and oDesk, and there is no easy way to tap the workforce of any of those platforms in parallel like there is on CloudCrowd.

    That’s fine if you have a project such as writing a single piece of code where management and input is part of the normal process, but it doesn’t scale for tasks like content generation, tagging images, data extraction, filtering web content, or categorization.

    With projects like these a client typically wants to be able to hand off high volumes of data and get back quality results– without having to manage individual workers.

    CloudCrowd’s platform was specifically designed to process high volume tasks without requiring the client to manage anything.

    CloudCrowd breaks projects down into separate tasks that can be handled more cost effectively, engineers a workflow, and automatically distributes and manages every single task from start to finish.

    The key is that each task is reviewed by other workers, each of whom have a credibility score based on their prior work history.

    The review systems eliminate the need for management, but still ensure quality output.

    The end result is that a client can bring us a large project and simply let us handle it from start to finish, something can’t be done on first generation platforms.

  • http://www.facebook.com/profile.php?id=609818489 Mark Chatow

    Credibility is the single most important piece of the crowdsourcing puzzle, but Mechanical Turk only tracks one metric: “percentage of tasks approved”.

    The problem is that getting a task “accepted” on Turk doesn’t mean you got it right, and doesn’t mean that you are qualified to do other tasks.

    A great example is the project Amazon put up when Steve Fossett’s plane went down. The task was to view segments of satellite photos and report whether you saw anything that looked like it might be a plane.

    There were tens of thousands of tasks available, and it appeared that every single one of them was accepted.

    Workers who wanted to game the system could easily rack up high, but meaningless, “approval” scores.

    The same thing happens when a requester puts up a task and then doesn’t have time to review all of the answers (or in the case of a survey doesn’t need to). In those cases, every answer is approved.

    The end result is a one-dimensional rating that means very little.

    At CloudCrowd, every single task is reviewed and/or compared with results from other workers.

    CloudCrowd’s model is also much more granular, and the quality of a task can be graded along points on a review spectrum.

    This works particularly well for tasks such as writing or editing for content generation where a simple “right or wrong doesn’t provide enough information.

    One important distinction between oDesk/Elance/Guru/TopCoder and ‘crowdsourcing’ is that those sites enable companies to find that one person/company from amongst the crowd (aka: single-winner approach).
