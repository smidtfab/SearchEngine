How To Get The Most Out Of Crowdsourcing

"If you build it they will come" may have worked for Kevin Costner in Field of Dreams but such is not always the case in shopping the design of your next company logo through crowdsourcing. Clicking on a link that reads crowdsourcing / logo design and offering a prize for the best logo does not guarantee your results will match all those glowing testimonials from other who have used the process. Most of those successes came as a result of understanding the basics to hosting a productive contest. Here are few essentials to getting the most of out of your crowdsourcing experience.

Logo Design Crowdsourcing 

Choose the Right Job

For large companies the right kind of contest for a crowdsource project is often the launch of a completely new product, reinvention of a product design, or an overhaul of the company's brand. Such projects require a specific set of skill sets and often involve a team of designers competing for such projects.

Small businesses do better hosting a contest with a creative brief for one focused task. It's better to start a contest for a logo design rather than an entire web site. Such contests are manageable and will also attract a greater variety of qualified participants than more complex jobs. If you need more, split the tasks up into several contests.

Write a Good Creative Brief

Remember, the whole idea is to get a large pool of designers working independently to come with the best design for your business for the best competitive price. The only way to get good ideas is to give those designers something to work with. Describe your business, product or service, and competitors. Never assume they will know what your business is about. Communicate your target market and how you would like to set yourself apart. The rule here is to write enough for the designers to work with but not so much they are unwilling to take the time to digest what you are looking for.

Feedback, Feedback, Feedback

This is the point where many newcomers to crowdsourcing hit the wall. When running a contest, there is an inclination for many contest holders to disregard designs that are less than perfect. If entrants are making submissions that are obviously unprofessional then drop them from the contest. Otherwise, a little feedback may just guide that less than perfect entrant in directions that are exactly what you need. Feedback on the fly is an important part of the design.

Make a Decision

Making a decision at the end of a design contest can be challenging if you have even 25 designs to choose from especially if you have little experience in marketing and branding. Bringing others into the process is important before making a decision. Ask other business people you trust what they think of the designs you have narrowed things down to. If you want to really ask some experts, poll some of your customers and see which design they like the most.
