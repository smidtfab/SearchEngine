When is using machine learning better than social engineering/crowd-sourcing? What are the aspects to consider before making such a decision?

This is a follow-up question to How has Quora utilized machine learning and information retrieval to improve its user experience?.

A real life example of this question might apply to Quora. Currently Quora doesn't do this, but it can recommend tags based on the content of a question using ML or it can ask ppl to add tags and then have moderators check that the tags actually make sense(crowd-sourcing). An ideal solution to this would be some hybrid of crowd-sourcing and ML. This question aims to understand which technique should be used to what extent in similar problems.

While it depends on the complexity of the task and the desired quality of the results, if you need to guarantee high quality results, it is best to augment ML with crowdsourcing. 

In fact, over the next few years, I think you can expect to see an increasingly symbiotic relationship between crowdsourcing and machine learning. 
  • Crowdsourcing is an infant industry, the inner dynamics of which are poorly understood and for which there is only the scantest academic research. The innovation that's happening at companies like CrowdFlower (disclaimer: the CEO is an advisor to my company) and SpeakerText (disclaimer: this is my company) is just barely scratching the surface of what can be done by applying ML and advanced statistics to crowdsourcing processes. Expect this space to heat up in a massive way over the next five years. 
  • Crowdsourcing is the most cost efficient way to get labeled training data, and for certain kinds of ML problems––most notably speech-to-text––the size, quality and relevance of your training data set matters more than the algorithm itself. Historically, speech companies budgeted millions of dollars just to acquire a sufficient set of training data. Now, because of the internet and things like Mechanical Turk, you can easily tap into cheap, on demand labor and even turn what was once considered "work" into a game played by people across the globe.
  • ML + Crowdsourcing = Supervised Learning. Quite simply, if you first attempt to solve a problem with an ML algorithm––like say speech-to-text––and then assign a human to correct any errors, you can––if you do it right––not only reap huge short term efficiency gains (vs pure human effort), but also improve machine accuracy over the long term by adding more and more labeled training data into the system. The result: constantly improving labor efficiency, lower costs, and smarter machines––coupled with consistently high quality results.

8 votes by Adam Bossy, Eric Schoonover, Nizameddin Haşim Ordulu, (more)Adam Bossy, Eric Schoonover, Nizameddin Haşim Ordulu, Ohad Samet, Matt Mireles, L. Ruby, Alex Kamil and Manu Raghavan
Key question: What is the up-front and then the marginal cost of each action vs. what is the benefit provided?
 
Machine learning works when after a training period the marginal cost for each decision from the learning method drops to near zero (cost of computation) and there is some non zero benefit (you see more hits, more engagement, more ad clicks etc.). It's not a silver bullet: you will pay an up front cost in training and in just plain old figuring out which thing works. Machines are also better at scaling up to massive data sets than humans - at some point you will hit the limit of available qualified human time for your task.
 
Humans work for settings where machine learning has such a high up front cost as to be prohibitive (e.g. no one knows how to do a particular task with acceptable quality), or for settings where we don't yet know of a way to train a machine learning algorithm well enough to drop marginal costs (e.g. performing an in depth tricky analysis of a protocol or a design...we might make a machine do it, but it may not generalize to the next case). The problem is that even mechanical turkers need to be paid more than the cost of electricity. While great startups like Crowdflower are helping drop this marginal cost, there will always be a gap. Therefore humans are best for cases where there is a large and clear payoff.
 
There's a mushy middle for tasks like transcription: tasks where machines can provide a low quality output, humans can provide a high quality output. The end application dictates what quality you need and how well you can live with the cost profiles of each approach. (Searching video by keywords in the audio stream: machines. Transcribing doctor's orders for medicine: humans.)

I like David's answer, and I'll add to it from a product design perspective.

The answer to the first question implies that machine learning and crowdsourcing are fundamentally alternatives, which they aren't. They may or may not be mutually exclusive depending on the problem at hand.

Instead, let's ask which factors to consider for each one: 

1. When should machine learning algorithms be used to solve a problem?

Machine learning requires lots of data and expertise. I have heard one too many entrepreneurs describe a problem and their proposed solution, *hand wave*, say that machine learning will solve this, *stop hand waving,* and all their customer's problems will be solved.

In reality, machine learning algorithms are fairly complex in the context of computer science and ultimately not that accurate. Although the term is thrown around loosely, they aren't a magic wand. 

If a company is fortunate enough to have access to the expertise required, then it's a matter of having enough data and resources to run computations on that data. Like David says, there's an initial cost and a scaling benefit, which will require significant resources.

2. When should crowdsourcing be used to solve a problem?

As it pertains to product design, crowdsourcing is effective if it naturally fits into a rewarding product. Investors often ask founders which of the seven deadly sins their product taps into exactly for this reason. When your site is vying among hundreds of millions of sites for users' attention there better be an intrinsic, carnal reward to the task that's being outsourced.

In short, you must ensure there are sufficient motivating forces for users to want to solve your problems for you. Luis von Ahn designed a game to have users tag millions of images. These tags were ultimately used for Google's image search. The benefit to the world was simply a positive externality; the benefit to the user was entertainment; the benefit to Google was cheap labor and improved accuracy over an algorithmic approach.

I'll add my two cents at the risk of repeating some of what was mentioned in previous answers. 

I think that David's and Adam's answers covered usage of ML vs. CS to support  a product feature, mostly around recommendation systems; they also correctly highlighted cost structure. I'd like to answer this from a different angle - what to use when a decision needs to be made (e.g. accept or decline a loan application, perform a medical procedure, buy or sell a stock). Though I'm personally a fan of using both methods, roughly I'd say that deciding between the two is affected by two main factors:

  • Problem complexity: anything can be "modeled" using brute-force data crunching. People in the financial industry made a fortune from the lousy approximation called "credit score" [1] (keep in mind: a vast majority of the merchants that make Paypal successful would probably fall off the bottom of the FICO scale. These are the same merchants that will make Square successful[2]. None of them gets a merchant account). But creating a system to correctly forecast requires having an objective function, as well as the ability to formulate cause and effect. Hence, problems with subjective definitions, lack of sample size or lack of information (e.g. the winner of survivor or forecasting the peak of the general public's inflow/outflow of funds to the stock market) are probably best handled by crowd sourcing. BTW, some of these problems are better solved by excellent domain experts or stronger computational capabilities which are, as Adam and David noted, also a cost issue.
  • Probability of bias: I am a big fan of, and have blogged in the past about combining ML and CS. I think these are complementary, and sophisticated systems should be able to combine both. However, you should also ask how CS needs to be utilized - in making the decision or giving feedback to improve the automated system. In my experience, when you are trying to make a decision rather than recommend something, and the more there's a perceived loss or gain to whoever makes that decision, you'll want to use ML rather than CS or any other type of human decision. When introducing gain or loss, people stop trying to make the right decision. That's why science methodology introduced double-blind tests, and why some ESP games don't work (because players try to guess what the other player will say, rather than solve the puzzle they were asked to solve). In the long run, in these cases, properly designed ML algorithms provide better results (empirically proven in decision support systems in medicine, and in a few instances when I worked on risk/fraud decision making).

[1] Not an example of a hard-to-model question IMHO, more an example of dogmatic execution.
[2] How is Square going to give out merchant accounts with minimal verification while still preventing fraud?
