IT Worker Turnover: An Empirical Examination of Intrinsic Motivation1

By Thatcher, Jason Bennett
Publication: Database for Advances in Information Systems
Date: Spring 2006 2006
Share:
Print
More

Introduction

Motivating and retaining high performing employees has never been an easy task for IT managers (Smits et al., 1993). Management theorists suggest that hygiene factors, such as pay, influence employees' motivation, attitudes, and turnover (Herzberg & Mausner, 1959). Congruently, information systems (IS) research has found that when hygiene factors are high, IT workers are likely to continue in their current job (Tan & lgbaria, 1994).

The current study contends that although hygiene factors are important, intrinsic motivation may exert a more powerful influence on IT workers' attitudes and behavior. Couger (1988) reported that IT workers ranked the work itself as the most important feature of their jobs. Furthermore, when employees enjoy the actual work, they report higher job satisfaction, require less supervision, and turnover at lower rates (Eby & Freeman, 1999; Harkins & Petty, 1982)

Few studies within the organizational sciences have simultaneously examined the links between intrinsic motivation, its antecedents, and outcomes (Ambrose & Kulik, 1999). In an influential study, Eby and Freeman (1999) developed and partially tested an integrative framework for understanding the sources and implications of intrinsic motivation. They found intrinsic motivation was influenced by job design and influenced employee intentions and behavior. However, IS research has not extensively examined the sources and implications of intrinsic motivation. Hence, building on Eby and Freeman (1999) framework, this paper tests a model (see Figure 1 ) that identifies potential antecedents of intrinsic motivation and its implications for IT workers' attitudes and intentions.

Intrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic motivation refers to the degree to which an employee experiences positive internal feelings when working effectively on the job (Hackman & Oldham, 1976). Intrinsically motivated employees report a sense of self-determination, competence and interest in completing tasks requisite of their work (Deci & Ryan, 1980). Unlike job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment, intrinsic motivation refers to feelings related to the task and task performance, not overall satisfaction with the position, employer, or organization.

The current study argues that motivators and hygiene factors influence intrinsic motivation (Eby & Freeman, 1999). Motivators are perceived intrinsic job characteristics embedded in the job's design (Herzberg et al., 1967). Consistent with this notion, job characteristics theory posits that job characteristics such as autonomy, feedback, and meaningfulness motivate individuals to perform behavior that results in positive organizational outcomes (Ambrose & Kulik, 1999). In contrast, hygiene factors are perceived extrinsic characteristics found in the broader work environment (Herzberg et al., 1967). Although hygiene factors are important for initial job choices, research suggests that over IT career progression, intrinsic job characteristics become an important source of motivation (Smits et al., 1997).

Motivating Factors

Motivation research has consistently found that job characteristics influence intrinsic motivation (Ambrose & Kulik, 1999). IS research indicates that IT workers who perceive higher levels of autonomy report lower levels of overload (Moore, 2000) and derive greater satisfaction from their jobs (Guimaraes & Igbaria, 1992). Autonomous employees possess the flexibility to structure tasks in ways they find intrinsically motivating and consequently derive greater pleasure from task performance (Wrzesniewski & Dutton, 2001).

H1a: Autonomy will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

Meaningfulness refers to whether employees believe that their work counts to their workgroup, organization or other r?f?rent group. It is a composite of three dimensions - skill variety, task identity, and task significance. IT workers express higher levels of job satisfaction when they are performing high-skill, meaningful work (Couger & Zawacki, 1980). When provided opportunities to use multiple skills and talents, IT workers will perceive their jobs as more important and interesting, and therefore more intrinsically motivating.

H1b: Skill variety will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

H1c:Task significance will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

H1d. Task identity will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

Job feedback is the degree to which the job provides direct and unambiguous feedback (Hackman & Oldham, 1980). Research has found that job feedback positively influences individual's feelings of competence and enjoyment of the task (Jussim et al., 1992).

H1e: Job feedback positively influences intrinsic motivation.

Hygiene Factors

Hygiene factors reflect beliefs about the work context, such as management or pay, that influence diverse work outcomes such as employee innovation (Amabile et al., 1994) or turnover (Tan & lgbaria, 1994). In the practitioner literature, satisfaction with supervision and pay (Davidson, 1999) are frequently cited hygiene factors linked to employee motivation and turnover(Herzberg & Mause, 1959).

Supervisory satisfaction refers to an employee's satisfaction with management's interaction and supervision. Theory underscores the importance of supervision for motivating IT workers (Ferrait et al., 1993). Research has demonstrated that individuals who are satisfied with their supervisor feel a higher degree of competence and social integrity, and therefore may find their job more intrinsically motivating (lgbaria & Guimaraes, 1993).

H2a: Supervisory satisfaction will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

Pay satisfaction implies employees believe they have been treated fairly by the organization (Heneman & Schwab, 1985). Specifically, both the actual level of compensation, and the perceived fairness with which the organization determines salary level have direct influences on satisfaction (Tan & lgbaria, 1994). IT workers who feel they have been treated fairly are more likely to feel they play an important role in the organization and consequently will report greater levels of intrinsic motivation.

H2b: Pay satisfaction will positively influence intrinsic motivation.

Outcomes of Intrinsic Motivation

According to cognitive evaluation theory, intrinsic motivation ties motivators and hygiene factors to job attitudes (Deci, 1971). Deci and Ryan (1980) argue that individuals form feelings of intrinsic motivation by performing the task even when initial motivation was the anticipation of external rewards. In principal, intrinsic job characteristics influence the degree to which an employee may feel performing a task is a rewarding behavior (Hackman & Oldham, 1980). Meta-analytic results (Eby & Freeman, 1999) support the notion of intrinsic motivation mediating the influence of motivators and hygiene factors on job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment (i.e., attitudes). Consistent with this approach, the current study models intrinsic motivation as a direct antecedent to affective organizational commitment and job satisfaction, and an indirect antecedent to turnover intention.

Work-Related Attitudes

Affective organizational commitment. Affective organizational commitment refers to an employee's willingness to exert considerable effort on behalf of the organization and a strong desire to maintain membership in the organization (Mowday et al., 1982). Affective organizational commitment directs respondents' attention to their employing organization (lgbaria & Guimaraes, 1993). Affectively committed employees accept group's attitudes and behaviors as congruent with their own (O'Reilly & Chatman, 1986). It is reasonable to expect that these employees may be more accepting of group values and a greater willingness to engage in extra-role behaviors. We expect that IT workers who are intrinsically motivated are more likely to report higher levels of affective organizational commitment to the organization.

H3a: Intrinsic motivation will positively influence affective organizational commitment.

Job satisfaction. Job satisfaction is a pleasurable or positive emotional state resulting from the appraisal of one's job (Locke, 1976). Job satisfaction reflects employees overall evaluative judgments about the position, affective experiences at work and beliefs about the workplace (Weiss & Cropazano, 1996). Intrinsically motivated IT workers are more likely to perceive their jobs as meaningful, and valuable to others, thus judge their jobs more positively. They will also experience positive affective states on the job with greater frequency and more intensely. We propose that intrinsically motivated employees will have a higher level of job satisfaction.

H3b: Intrinsic motivation will positively influence general job satisfaction.

In addition to intrinsic motivation, substantial literature has found that job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment are strong positive correlates. Although theory suggests that job satisfaction may have a direct effect on affective organizational commitment and turnover intention (Mobley et al., 1979), substantial disagreement exists over the pattern of relationships between job satisfaction, affective organizational commitment and turnover intention (Williams & Hazer, 1986). For example, Mowday, Porter and Steers (1982) argue that satisfaction represents a subset of the factors that determine one's overall commitment to the organization. IS research most frequently models job satisfaction as an antecedent to affective organizational commitment and turnover intention (Igbaria & Greenhaus, 1992a). Consistent with IS research, we propose that as IT professionals express greater job satisfaction they will express more affective organizational commitment and less turnover intention (see for example lgbaria & Guimaraes, 1993).

H4: Job satisfaction will positively influence affective organizational commitment.

We propose that intrinsic motivation's influence on turnover intention is mediated by job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment. Research consistently finds that IT workers who report greater job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment are less likely to report turnover intent (Igbaria & Greenhaus, 1992b; lgbaria & Siegel, 1992).

H5a: Affective organizational commitment will negatively influence turnover intent.

H5b: Job satisfaction will negatively influence turnover intent.

Mediation

Consistent with Eby and Freeman (1999), we posit that intrinsic motivation mediates the influence of intrinsic job characteristics, supervisory satisfaction and pay satisfaction on affective organizational commitment and job satisfaction. Further, we test whether affective organizational commitment and job satisfaction mediate the influence of intrinsic motivation on turnover intent.

H6a: Intrinsic motivation will mediate the influence of job characteristics, supervisory satisfaction and pay satisfaction on affective organizational commitment.

H6b: Intrinsic motivation will mediate the influence of job characteristics, supervisory satisfaction and pay satisfaction characteristics on job satisfaction.

H7a: Affective organizational commitment mediates the influence of intrinsic motivation on intention to quit.

H7b: Job satisfaction mediates the influence of intrinsic motivation on intention to quit.

Method

Questionnaires were distributed to 260 IT professionals in 34 public agencies located in the Southeastern United States. The overall response rate was 87.6%. Table 1 presents sample characteristics. Measures were drawn from the human resource management literature. Where possible, we used measures identified by Eby and Freeman (1999) in their meta-analysis of affective organizational commitment and intrinsic motivation. Construct measures and their sources may be found in the Appendix. We also controlled for gender, age, education, and tenure.

Analysis and Results

Preliminary analysis revealed significant problems with skewness and kurtosis in the data (see Table 2). Therefore, we used the partial least squares structural equation modeling technique, which is robust to nonnormal data (Chin, 1998b). Using a bootstrapping technique (n=200), path estimates and T-statistics were calculated for hypothesized relationships by construct using PLS Graph 2.91 (Chin & Frye, 1996).

At the measurement level, we evaluated the convergent and discriminant validity of the construct measures. To evaluate the construct measures' reliability, we estimated the composite reliabilities and average variance extracted of each construct. Results indicate adequate composite reliabilities and AVEs (see Table 3). Also, when the square root of each construct's AVE is greater than the correlation of the construct to other latent variables, the correlation of constructs demonstrates discriminant validity (see Table 3). Finally, we examined the items loadings and cross-loadings (Chin, 1998a). The model's correlations of constructs factor loadings (see Table 4) demonstrate adequate discriminant and convergent validity.

At the structural level, our analysis provided limited empirical support for the research model (see Figure 2). Overall, the model explained a moderate amount of the variance in the latent constructs (Intrinsic Motivation R^sup 2^ = .23; Affective organizational commitment R^sup 2^ = .48; Job Satisfaction R^sup 2^ = .43; Turnover Intent R^sup 2^ = .33)2.

To test H6, we used a three step approach (Baron & Kenny, 1986). First, we examined direct effects from intrinsic motivators and hygeine factors to job satisfaction and organizational commitment. Supervisory satisfaction (AC, .36, p>.01; JS, .20, p>.05), pay satisfaction (AC, .31, p>.01; JS, .26, p>.05), and Skill variety (AC, .16, p>.05; JS, .22, p>.05) demonstrated direct relationships with affective commitment and job satisfaction. Then, we introduced intrinsic motivation into the structural model. In the presence of intrinsic motivation, the relationship from skill variety to job satisfaction was no longer significant. Finally, we tested a model that simultaneously tested relationships between intrinsic motivators, hygiene factors, intrinsic motivation, affective commitment, and job satisfaction. H6, which posited that intrinsic motivation would mediate the influence of intrinsic motivators and hygiene factors on job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment, was not supported. Contrary to our expectations, pay and supervisory satisfaction explained an additional variance in affective organizational commitment (? R^sup 2^=.11) and job satisfaction (? R^sup 2^ = .13).

To test H7, we used a similar approach to assess the relationships among intrinsic motivation, affective commitment, and job satisfaction. First, we assessed a direct relationship from intrinsic motivation to turnover intention.

We found a direct, negative, relationship from intrinsic motivation to turnover intention. Then we introduced job satisfaction and organizational commitment. The influence of intrinsic motivation on turnover intention disappeared. Finally, we evaluated a model with direct relationships from intrinsic motivation to job satisfaction, affective organizational commitment, and turnover intention. We found that intrinsic motivation influenced job satisfaction, which consequently, influence affective organizational commitment. Hence, we found support for H7, which posited that affective organizational commitment and job satisfaction would mediate the influence of intrinsic motivation on turnover intention.

It is important to note that our initial analysis did not yield a significant relationship from job satisfaction to turnover intention. To further explore this relationship, we dropped the path from affective organizational commitment to turnover intention. In the absence of affective organizational commitment, job satisfaction demonstrated a negative, significant influence on turnover intention (.21, p< .05). Also, when affective organizational commitment was introduced, job satisfaction demonstrated a direct relationship with affective organizational commitment and no longer significantly related to job satisfaction. Further, when the path from job satisfaction to commitment, the explained variance in turnover intention dropped by .05, suggesting that affective organizational commitment mediates the influence of job satisfaction on turnover intention (Baron & Kenny, 1986). Given our results, analysis suggests that affective organizational commitment mediates job satisfaction's influence on turnover intention.

Discussion

This study examined the relationship between IT workers' intrinsic motivation, its antecedents, and its consequences. Contrary to our expectations, intrinsic motivation was not significantly related to task significance (H1c), task identity, (H1d), feedback (H1e), and pay satisfaction (H2b); however, it demonstrated a positive relationship with autonomy (H1a), skill variety (H1b) and supervisory satisfaction (H2a). Support was found for intrinsic motivation having a direct effect on job satisfaction (H3b) and mediated influence on affective organizational commitment (H4) and turnover intent (H5). Overall, support was not found for intrinsic motivation fully mediating the influencing of job characteristics or hygiene factors (H6) on IT workers' attitudes. Also, findings suggest that IT workers' attitudes (H7) mediate intrinsic motivations influence on turnover intent. In addition, supplemental analysis revealed that affective organizational commitment mediated the influence of job satisfaction on turnover intent.

This research has important implications for academics and practitioners. This study underscores the importance of examining intrinsic motivation as a construct distinct from intrinsic job characteristics. Analysis suggests that intrinsic motivation's influence on job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment is distinct from job characteristics.

Although intrinsic motivation did not mediate the influence of autonomy or feedback, it did fully mediate the influence of skill variety on job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment. IT workers' tendency for high growth needs (Smits et al., 1997) suggests they may be intrinsically motivated (Hackman & Oldham, 1975). Hence, we believe that intrinsic motivation is an important construct that merits further theoretical and empirical attention when examining IT workers and their workplace behaviors.

Also, this study highlights the importance of hygiene factors' influence on IT workers. Prior research suggests that extrinsic characteristics have less influence on employee job attitudes over the IT career progression (McLean et al., 1996). Our findings suggest that supervisory and pay satisfaction were significant positive antecedents to job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment. Given hygiene factors may positively impact retention or negatively impact creativity, future research should examine the influence of hygiene factors on diverse workplace outcomes such as IT workers' innovation with IT or organizational citizenship behavior.

This study has important implications for practice. This study provides evidence that intrinsic motivation (i.e., enjoying the work itself) is an important antecedent to job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment. We have identified skill variety as one lever managers can use to increase intrinsic motivation. Given several job characteristics were not predictors of intrinsic motivation, results direct attention to tools other than job design as means to intrinsically motivate IT workers. Rather than job characteristics, our study directs managers' attention to the relationship between different hygiene factors and IT workers' attitudes. Given the importance of hygiene factors such as supervisory satisfaction (Roepke et al., 2000), our research directs managers to consider hygiene factors this study left unexamined such as training, bonuses, or advancement opportunities that could influence intrinsic motivation. Overall, our finding suggests that even when jobs are intrinsically motivating, organizations that do not pay attention to hygiene factors may experience difficulty retaining IT workers.

Conclusions and Future Research

This study modeled and tested the relationship between intrinsic motivation and IT workers' perceptions of their jobs, attitudes, and intentions. Findings suggest that intrinsic motivation is an important construct that demonstrates effects distinct from intrinsic job characteristics such as autonomy or skill variety. Further, intrinsic motivation was distinct from, and demonstrated a positive relationship with job attitudes (i.e., job satisfaction and affective organizational commitment). Given the relationship between intrinsic motivation and attitudes, it is important to identify factors that may intrinsically motivate IT workers. Future research should examine whether intrinsic motivation's influence varies across types of IT workers such as systems analysts or programmers and outcomes such as innovation with IT or task performance (Ferratt & Short, 1986).

Future research should examine additional organizational or job design levers that influence intrinsic motivation among IT workers. For example, because of our interest in examining the Eby and Freeman (1999) framework, we did not examine training or technical growth opportunities. It would be useful to assess how different types of incentives such as opportunities for advanced technical training influence intrinsic motivation. Training and other organizational programs may be important levers that also influence affective organizational commitment and job satisfaction. Through comparing job characteristics, pay satisfaction, and leadership to programs such as training, research may identify more actionable levers to help manage the IT workforce.
