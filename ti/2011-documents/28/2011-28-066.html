The Amazon Mechanical Turk (Mturk)

Online marketplaces come in many forms, designed to achieve various objectives, and one of them is the Amazon Mechanical Turk (Mturk) which is a brainchild of the Amazon Inc, it links US based requesters and providers or workers from around the globe. Originally intended to identify duplicates on its webpages, the concept is now utilized for computer programs to derive assistance from human intelligence so they can undertake tasks they would otherwise be unable to perform. This is done through 'providers' (workers) upon request by the Mturk 'requesters' (program developers) who are in position to issue tasks or HITS (Human Intelligence tasks) which include selecting the most suitable images for (say) a storefront, identifying performers on music CD's or supply written product descriptions. There are monetary benefits for those participating as the workers and the amounts due for each task are set by the requesters and subsequent payment to a worker is dependent on the approval of assignments by the requester.

An application programming interface or the limited Mturk requester site are the available avenues through which requesting programs place HITs, while at the same time it is the prerogative of the requester to ask for qualifications prior to the execution of tasks, and the results produced by the worker can also be rejected on premises determined by the requester. Remittances of successful tasks undertaken can be redeemed through the Amazon gift certificates or transferred to a US bank account, Amazon receives 10% of the monetary value for each successfully completed task. Although it began in 2005 Mechanical Turk is still in beta and some of the regular postings of HITs emanate from Casting Words, while Turkers also rank among the most popular HITs featured at the site, these entail writing or rewriting sentences, paragraphs and even full length articles - they reward the worker between one cent to ten dollars upon successful completion.

Other tasks include surveys, linking to or commenting on a blog or even befriending someone on Facebook, and the providers (workers) from all over the world (up to 100,000) choose the best ones fitting their capabilities or qualifications. The mechanical turks have been used to search for missing persons such as aviator Steve Fosset, and although the search was unsuccessful the idea showed strong merits as satellite data was divided into 85 sq. meter sections, and Mturk workers were asked to flag unusual objects that might indicate the possibility of a crash site. The task of completing HITs is much simpler thanks to the efforts made by programmers, although automating the HITs entirely has been disapproved because of the importance of engaging the human element in tackling them.
