Citizen Scientists Help Model Interacting Galaxies

November 25th, 2009 | Author: Dr. Geller

A new addition to the Galaxy Zoo effort has been announced. The GalaxyZoo organization is another effort intended to make use of what is now called “citizen science.” Anyone with a computer and link to the World Wide Web can take part in an effort to categorize millions of galaxies. In the latest effort, Dr. John Wallin of George Mason University (GMU) has developed software to allow users to help categorize and model galaxies that are interacting with other galaxies. Read more about the effort online here, or go directly to the Galaxy Zoo: Mergers webpage here.

Our Night Sky: August 2009

July 31st, 2009 | Author: TGreiner

Get the Flash Player to see the wordTube Media Player.

This month, I discuss how you, as an amateur astronomer, can contribute to real science and even be included in formal research papers!

Citizen Science refers to research that enlists volunteers who are not necessarily scientifically trained.  Not only do Citizen Science projects enable you to engage in real science, most of these projects are fun and easy to do, they can be done at your own discretion, and they really do lead to new discoveries.

One example of astronomy-related Citizen Science is the Stardust project, where volunteers use a virtual microscope to search for interplanetary and comet dust particles captured by a NASA spacecraft.

There are many Citizen Science opportunities in just as many fields, but I would like to focus on one, in particular; and then I will list several others for you in my blog.

The American Association of Variable Star Observers, or AAVSO, has long enlisted the help of amateur astronomers in the study of variable stars.  Recently, the AAVSO unveiled an exciting new project called Citizen Sky, which will study the mysterious variable star, epsilon Aurigae.

Epsilon Aurigae, a third magnitude star near the brighter Capella, is an interacting eclipsing binary (or double component) star.  In other words, not only is one star passing in front of , or eclipsing, the other, the two stars share close enough orbits that matter is transferred between them. Both the eclipsing and the material transfer processes periodically affect epsilon Aurigae’s brightness – an effect we call variability.

The real mystery surrounding epsilon Aurigae’s variability relates to the nature of its eclipsing component.  While scientists are confident that the visible component is an F-type supergiant, peculiar light curves have raised intriguing questions about the eclipsing companion.

Unfortunately, the infrequency of these eclipses, just once every 27.12 years, limits observations.  And that’s where you come in.  Epsilon Aurigae is on the verge of another long-awaited eclipse, and the AAVSO wants your help; whether through visible observations, spreading the word, or more in-depth photometry.

Citizen Sky is a project of the International Year of Astronomy 2009.  Their website includes everything you need to get ready for this rare and exciting opportunity, including tutorials, forums, and more detailed information about the enigmatic “star” of this research.

So, go ahead – visit Citizen Sky and get started today!

Astronomers Seek to Understand “Green Peas”

July 31st, 2009 | Author: Dr. Geller

In a paper to be published in the Monthly Notices of the Royal Academy of Sciences, available online now here, a team of astronomers review the latest known about an unusual class of galaxies known as “Green Peas.” This is all a result of a project called the Galaxy Zoo, wherein astronomers have enlisted the aid of over 200,000 professional astronomers, amateur astronomers and students in classifying galaxies as seen by the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS). The galaxies nicknamed green peas, are characterized by being low mass and having high star formation rates. Now in this case low mass galaxies have less than 10 billion solar mass equivalent stars, and have star formation rates higher than 10 solar mass stars forming every year. If you wish to participate in the Galaxy Zoo project, you can join in at their website.

Report from the Galaxy Zoo

November 26th, 2008 | Author: Dr. Geller

In a pre-print paper to the Monthly Notices of the Royal Academy of Sciences released today, an international group of astronomers discuss their findings culled from the data collected in the Galaxy Zoo. The Galaxy Zoo is a collaboration among professionals and amateurs around the world, utilizing the world wide web. Whether amateur or professional, or just plain curious with some time to help, you too can assist the Galaxy Zoo project in classifying galaxies, and know that your contribution goes to research such as that reported today. To join the Galaxy Zoo team go to http://www.galaxyzoo.org/ To read the results of research produced from the “largest sample of visually classified morphologies yet compiled” check out the report at http://xxx.lanl.gov/PS_cache/arxiv/pdf/0811/0811.3970v1.pdf
