Google Acquires reCAPTCHA

Published on September 19, 2009 at 1:27 pm 2 Comments

So I’m a little slow to break the news, but on Wednesday, Google announced that it had acquired reCAPTCHA. This makes sense considering that Google has really been focusing on their Google Books initiative, which digitizes books and makes them available to the masses. There is also a similar initiative which focuses on digitizing and archiving news, including old newspapers and magazines.

How does reCAPTCHA compare with other CAPTCHAs?

Google has also been experimenting with their own methods for distinguishing humans from bots (Scripts and spammers), such as methods which show you pictures and ask you questions about them. However, as far as CAPTCHAs go, reCAPTCHA remains undefeated and the best solution yet. reCAPTCHA does not show you a random sequence such as “Xk7Jko”, where you usually have to wonder if case matters (Is that letter lowercase or uppercase? Does it matter?), and is slower to type since it is a random sequence. Instead, reCAPTCHA shows you two words, the majority of the time, making it as simple and quick as typing, well, two words. Also, reCAPTCHA remains undefeated even by the most determined crowds.

Why is it undefeated?

The fundamental way in which reCAPTCHA works is probably the reason why it remains undefeated. The world is in the process of digitizing books for archival. They have industry standard scanners and OCR programs which read the scanned images and convert them into actual text, making it searchable among other things. Sometimes, though, there are words that these OCR programs cannot understand, but the good thing is that they know when they cannot understand certain words. These words are then collected and sent to the word repository for reCAPTCHA. The CAPTCHA is then constructed by taking one of these words, and a word that really is known. These words are then distorted a bit further.

What does this mean? Well, the way in which CAPTCHAs are usually defeated is by enhanced OCR methods which can read and makes sense of the distorted letters. If industry standard OCR programs cannot read these words, it is very unlikely that a hackish script written by someone with access to simple OCR capabilities will be able to read these words, especially since they are further distorted.

What is the purpose of sending these words to users?

The responses to these words are then sent back to reCAPTCHA. When a certain word receives the same response after a while, it is accepted as the correct textual representation of the word. If need be, a human can moderate these responses to make sure they are legit.

What are the advantages of reCAPTCHA doing all of the work from their servers?

reCAPTCHA generates and serves the CAPTCHAs from their own servers. They also do all of the other work that is related, on their own servers. This makes it possible for even low powered web sites to use reCAPTCHA, since ultimately they are just served an image to show their visitors. It also makes it possible to react to attacks quickly. If someone is screwing around with reCAPTCHA, reCAPTCHA can temporarily ban that person from their servers. If there is a vulnerability found in reCAPTCHA, it can quickly be resolved on their own servers. If they are experiencing attacks, they can easily up the distortion on the words. Now that Google has acquired reCAPTCHA, reCAPTCHA’s stability and speed should be further ensured if not increased.

MailHide

reCAPTCHA also has a very interesting project and use called MailHide. MailHide obfuscates email addresses and hides them behind a reCAPTCHA. This functionality is built-in to WP-reCAPTCHA, where it scans for email addresses in posts and comments if you tell it to and obfuscates them using MailHide. Here is an example, supp...@recaptcha.net . To see the email, click on the three dots. A window will pop up showing you a reCAPTCHA. After solving it, you will be shown the email address. The appearance of the obfuscation can be customized to your liking.

Unfortunately it is not as popular as reCAPTCHA itself, but I am hoping that it will eventually gain more widespread use as it is the best way to hide email addresses from spammers. Many people try things like blah AT lol DOT com. People, programmers aren’t stupid. This can easily be parsed, as can most other simplistic textual obfuscations. If you come up with a complicated one which you think won’t be broken, then in reality you have lost, since your visitors will be the ones having a hard time reading what your email is.

My opinion

I have always thought that reCAPTCHA is a perfect idea. The fundamental way in which it works is what makes it succeed. I think what made reCAPTCHA really popular was that it is accessible to anyone. Everyone from Twitter to the Average Joe Blogger has access to reCAPTCHA. Now that Google owns reCAPTCHA, we are bound to see reCAPTCHA on Google websites. One such website will possibly be Google Groups, which already had a similar CAPTCHA system as well as a system similar to MailHide in which it hid email addresses from spammers using a CAPTCHA.

Google is probably my favorite company of all. You can call me a fanboy and whatever you like, but I am not delusional and try to excuse them on everything, but everything they have done has been amazing. I can openly confess that most if not all of my knowledge and my future career is thanks to Google. For this reason, I for one am really glad and excited that Google has acquired reCAPTCHA. I’m sure that as more people understand what reCAPTCHA is and how it works, fewer people will feel annoyed or bothered at having to solve reCAPTCHA images, especially if they know that their responses go to help towards digitizing books and therefore preserving our literature.
