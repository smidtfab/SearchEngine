An Introduction to Crowdsourcing

63
rate or flag this pageTweet this

By esocialtech

The term "crowdsourcing" was first coined by Jeff Howe in the year 2006 .

Crowdsourcing represents the act of a company or institution taking a function once performed by employees and outsourcing it to an undefined and generally large network of people in the form of an open call. This can take the form of peer-production (when the job is performed collaboratively), but is also often undertaken by sole individuals.

The crucial prerequisite is the use of the open call format and the large network of potential laborers.

Successful crowdsourcing involves input from a decentralized collection of individuals combined with a corporate management entity that initiates the project, considers its options, makes the final decision in the best interest of the company, and rewards the contributing member or members of the crowd either directly – a payment or prize – or indirectly – by implementing the idea.

Calls for participation can be made over new or existing social media channels. Tasks can be designed to facilitate competition or cooperation among the members of the crowd. Wiki-style, real-time editing features as well as voting mechanisms can be added to ascertain and analyze the “wisdom of the crowd.”

Conceptually, the idea of tapping into a large audience in order to outsource a specific function in bringing a product or service to market predates the Internet.

Bob Saget: 1980’s TV dad and crowdsourcing pioneer

Remember those Sunday evenings watching Danny Tanner host America’s Funniest Home Videos during ABC’s family hour? Every baseball bat to the crotch and cat sleeping on a sprinkler head was provided by viewers who responded to an open call for video submissions. Now more than 20 years later Bob and his falsetto voice-overs are gone, but the show remains one of ABC’s longest running, and its content continues to be sourced entirely by amateurs who supply the content for free.

1-800-FUN-COLOR

In 1995 M&M’s candies sourced the masses for input on a proposed historic product change.By encouraging the public to call and vote via a 1-800 hotline, Mars candies was using the crowd for product development. This kind of idea vetting is a popular task for crowdsourcing, and can provide valuable information for products and services much cheaper than traditional research involving surveys, focus groups or test markets.

Readers Digest has been inviting readers to submit short, humorous stories and jokes for decades. Political talk radio hosts have built empires around a format that relies on dialogue provided by everyman callers.

Crowdsourcing is easier to implement and less expensive, with no loss in speed or quality – in fact, the “wisdom of the crowd” is likely to promote more accurate and creative solutions to business problems.
