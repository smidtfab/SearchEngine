Cash, the new mother of scientific invention

Large prizes are the new force driving scientific innovation

Bryan Appleyard

How do you make a spaceship or artificial meat? How do you find 10 red balloons scattered across America? How do you design a longer-living mouse? The answer, strangely, is that you offer prizes: other people will do the rest for you.

Sir Richard Branson’s SpaceShipTwo will soon fly paying customers to the edge of space, because Branson invested in the maverick space engineer Burt Rutan, winner of the $10m (£6m) Ansari X prize for building a private-sector spaceship.

Peta (People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals) is offering a $1m prize to anybody who can make meat in a laboratory and sell it by June 2012.

The red balloons were tethered in secret locations by the Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency (Darpa), the super-cool, super-smart US government body that develops high-tech solutions to military problems. They were found last week, for a $40,000 prize, by a team from Massachusetts Institute of Technology. It took just nine hours.

Recently a scientist named Andrzej Bartke made a laboratory mouse live 1,819 days. He did it to win the Mprize, run by the Methuselah Foundation, which aims to find ways of extending human life.

Prizes work, and they are cheap. In return for its $10m, the X Prize Foundation stimulated $100m in private-sector rocket research. A $30m X prize is now being offered for the first private-sector robot on the moon, $10m for a superefficient car and $10m for a way of giving your doctor an instant readout of your DNA. And so on.

Unlike the Nobel peace prize that Barack Obama won for being Barack Obama, these prizes are proactive. They make things happen. They are like the famous prizes of the past — the £1,000 that Louis Blériot won for the first flight across the Channel. They bring an aura of heroism to innovation, an aura lost in the age of big government and multinationals.

In their turn, the winners of such prizes have to be more creative than any government or company. Rutan’s winner, SpaceShipOne, was powered by a rocket partly fuelled by liquid rubber and floated back to Earth like a falling leaf. Its successor, SpaceShipTwo, was unveiled last week in California. Nasa would never have created anything as pretty or as smart.

But the Darpa balloons take the breath away. Finding 10 red balloons across the entire continental United States is something that could not have been done 10 years ago. These days everybody is networked via the internet. Darpa set the challenge to test the power of networks to gather information rapidly and accurately.

The MIT team encouraged participants by saying it would share the winnings along the lines of a pyramid selling system. It explained: “We’re giving $2,000 per balloon to the first person to send us the correct co-ordinates, but that’s not all — we’re also giving $1,000 to the person who invited them. Then we’re giving $500 to whoever invited the inviter and $250 to whoever invited them and so on . . .”

Some unbelievably smart people at MIT set up a website (balloon.mit.edu) of blissful simplicity and, instantly, thousands of balloon-seekers were on the job. The point was that, because of the pyramid system, you could win even if you never saw a balloon.

It all demonstrated some of the most advanced thinking about intelligence-gathering. Many eyes are better than a few and highly detailed information can be assembled in an instant. If, for example, eight or nine years ago Darpa had used similar techniques to look for people taking flying lessons but not learning to take off and land, then 9/11 might have been prevented.

I suspect the balloon prize will inspire many imitators simply because it was so efficient and cheap. Networks can be built almost for nothing.

In fact Netflix, a DVD distribution website, has already triumphantly exploited the power of networks. It offered a $1m prize to anybody who could produce software that would “improve the accuracy of predictions about how much someone is going to enjoy a movie based on their movie preferences”. The competition lasted three years and involved 100m customer ratings of movies and 2,000 teams from 186 countries. Netflix got what it wanted. It could not have been done any other way.

The motivation of the X prize is simpler: beneficial technological development. The secondary motivation is to release innovation from the prison of either government bureaucracy or private-sector anxiety.

The Ansari version (named after the donors) was plainly aimed at Nasa, which, with the ending of the Shuttle programme, will be in the humiliating position of having to rely on Russian rockets to get to the International Space Station.

“Nasa has never been interested in sending humans into space,” Branson told me when he first invested in Rutan’s rocket. “It’s been a very closed, privileged shop. Only about 400 people have become astronauts in 50 years. We’ll make 1,000 astronauts in our first year.”

The fake meat and the freakishly old mice are in a different category. Both are designed to advance causes much more specific than simply doing good. Peta campaigns for animal rights and veganism — so encouraging people to eat “meat” that doesn’t come from animals fits its agenda — while the Methuselah Foundation wants to make us immortal.

“The idea that was capturing me,” Dave Gobel, the Mprize’s founder, told me, “is that nothing seems to have been cured since polio ... I began to ask what was the reason for that.”

Mice are genetically similar to humans and, if not tampered with, they don’t live long. Bartke got his up to 1,819 days — your average mouse would be lucky to make it to 750 days — by knocking out its growth hormone receptor gene. Don’t try this at home just yet.

The Mprize agenda, like that of Peta, is highly ideological. It is based on a belief that it is not only possible but also good to work to extend human life, to make us, ultimately, medically immortal. This is not a goal that either governments or companies could explicitly embrace — too weird, too risky, too controversial — but a prize can make it work.

Proactive prizes are phenomenally powerful tools. They circumvent bureaucracy, investment anxiety and, where necessary, ideology. They exploit networks, causes and frustration with existing establishments such as Nasa.

They also exploit the human will to take part, to compete and to win. They restore a sense of sweaty, balls-out, seat-of-the-pants heroism to innovation. And, with networks involved, they invite us all to play. Prizes work better than work.
