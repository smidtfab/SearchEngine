Crowdsourcing

26 Mar

Jeff Howe, a journalist who worked at Wired Magazine for 10 years and was recently hired by Northeastern, spoke to our Reinventing the News class about crowdsourcing Friday. Howe coined the term a few years ago and has released a book on the topic.

Hoew is an example of the how journalists and thinkers can actually make a concrete impact on business, technology, and “progress.” By crystallizing a phenomenon in its infancy, Howe makes it easier for society to digest the idea and apply it in new, unexpected ways. I first encountered the idea of crowdsourcing through the website Kiva.org, which allows regular consumers to chip in money to poor borrowers around the world looking for a microloan to start small businesses. This has been an innovative way to bring money to these capital-hungry microfinance organizations around the world.

The magnitude of the cost drop that crowdsourcing has brought as been unbelievable; to go from paying hundreds of dollars to license a photo to just a few dollars because of companies like iStockphoto hints at the power for disruption.

Howe brings up the economic phenomenon in the creative industries that leads to this huge cost reduction. There are far more people who want to have these creative jobs than there are positions. Because of that, there are many people who will do creative jobs in their free time for far less than a liveable wage.

Howe uses film criticism as an example. Traditionally, the position of film critic was seen as a cushy beat, something that a veteran reporter might get after spending a few decades working a tougher beat. Going to a movie with the family, filing a critique by eight, and coming in to work at 11, as Howe described it; a pretty nice gig for the reporter, but not a recipe for the most insightful reviews.

Enter the one-in-a-million guy who has unparalleled insight as a movie buff, but works as a lawyer. With crowdsourcing, this guy can now write the critique he is passionate about in his free time after work, says Howe, and the world can see into the mind of a genius critic even though he isn’t in the journalism field at all.

It might be scary news for people in or going into fields like journalism, art, or music. But I’m not a fan of job protection at the expense of the consumer, and it would be hypocritical to defend the journalists or professional stock-photo associations here. Howe’s view is that he is hungrier as a reader for the better, more passionate work that can result from this change than he is concerned for his livelihood as a journalist. I agree.
