The Gap logo pump fake. Back to the original.

On October 12, 2010, in Logo articles, Logo related links, by Leighton Hubbell

4,600 logo concepts and no one got paid.

In an odd and truly strange turn of events, GAP has now decided to go back to the original logo design they were trying to replace. Many are wondering if this was a calculated publicity stunt or just a corporate flub. It’s hard to say, but I think saying it was planned would be giving them too much credit. Sometimes it’s not the logo that needs a refresh, it’s your thinking.

The lame and unfortunate residual effect has to do with the major pump fake that Kristen Holden and GAP corporate pulled when they decided to crowdsource the logo. After all the negative social media response both on Facebook and Twitter to the new design, they decided to run a logo ‘contest’ on one of the major crowdsource sites. Sadly, over 4,600 entries appeared to try and get their shot at high profile glory in the biggest corporate whoops since the Tropicana packaging mess by the Arnell Group.

4,600 entries for a $500.00 contest. Now that they pulled the contest, all that work will get shelved. Oh, well. Absolutely no compensation for anyone there. How convenient for GAP. Of course, $500.00 is a drop in the bucket here compared to what this whole debacle will cost them in the end.

One thing I don’t think anyone has given any thought is, what if they didn’t go back to the familiar blue box icon and went back to the drawing board?

With all of the logo concepts that the contest site compiled, including the hundreds of others that appeared on Dribbble, Forrst and others among the blogosphere, how would you ever create something that wasn’t even remotely inspired by others’ work? Even if it wasn’t, how could you really convince anyone? That could have proven to be an even nastier situation than this one. And judging how the Twitterati reacted to this logo design, you don’t want to open up that hornet’s nest with an overly inspired new design. Better to stick with what you’ve got.

Although I understand that the average consumer was not aware of the logo change, it’s nice to know that all of the design community’s outrage and opinions have made a difference and were being heard. Individuals like Guy Kawasaki and his crowdsourced book cover aren’t really that concerned with upsetting the average design professional. But a major retailer like GAP knows that having a favorable reputation with the design public only works in their favor.

So, now we are back to the familiar blue GAP box. Perhaps all of this attention has given them the feeling that someone really is watching what they’re doing.

What do you think? I’d like to hear your thoughts.
