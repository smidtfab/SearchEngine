How to Use Crowdsourcing Effectively for Your Business

19th Jan, 2011 | Comment | Richard Weinberg | Posted In: Outsourcing Life

Crowdsourcing is an idea that would have sounded crazy a decade ago. Essentially, it is the idea of outsourcing work to your customers to be done voluntarily, rather than having your own employees do it for you. Wikipedia is a great example of how crowdsourcing can succeed – it would take professionals years to write a new edition of an encycolpedia, but by inviting anyone with an internet connection to contribute, a huge, comprehensive work can be kept up-to-date on a day-to-day basis. Crowdsourcing has been used by major corporations like Pepsi and Toyota to achieve results that they couldn’t on their own. However, if you use the wrong strategy it can lead to disastrous results. The concept should be approached with an open mind but with a healthy sense of skepticism as well. Here is how to make it work.

The first step is to determine what type of crowdsourcing works best for you, if any. You can use crowd intelligence to solve complex problems or crowd creativity to get new ideas. You could also allow customers to vote on new products or let them fund projects.

It is also very important to make sure that your efforts do not get monopolized by a small, vocal minority. For example, when Island Records decided to let fans decide where to send pop star Justin Bieber, an internet campaign successfully voted to have him sent to North Korea.

Surprisingly, one of the most effective ways to solve problems using this strategy is to ask people who have no experience in the field to solve your problem. Their fresh perspective can offer new solutions, a result which was found InnoCentive.

It is worth asking whether allowing customers to make product decisions is beneficial or harmful to the brand. Consumer oriented businesses can benefit from this, but more heavily regulated businesses or business-to-business brands are much less likely to benefit.

It is also important to focus on the quality of the work accomplished by crowdsourcing, rather than the number of people who actually contribute. Hard boundaries must be set on what the objective of a crowd sourced effort is, so that it does not spiral out of control.

Finally, incentives are important. An understanding of what motivates people to take part in a crowdsourced effort ensures that it can be accomplished much more successfully. In fact, due to the complexity of crowdsourcing operations it can often make sense to hire an outsourcing firm to manage them.
