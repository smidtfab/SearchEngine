Monday, 22 November 2010

Emtional attachment can lead to success

Darren Brabham (2009) conducted investigating the crowdsourcing company, 'Threadless.com'. It's a fantastic website, I love it and am pretty sure that if I do not contribute, I will buy something.

From Instant Messaging 17 adults (18 and over), Brabham tried to tease out what gets the crowd to contribute to this website and more importantly for the owners of threadless, it's business. After investigating iStockphoto, he argues that money had a large part to play in the motivation, hence the production of good work. The positives of Crowdsourcing with iStockphoto is that the crowd could make good money even if they were an novice in photography. That is a definite pro of crowdsourcing, getting people to do something cheaply that motivates them financially.

Well Brabham argues there are some similarities with iStockphoto and Threadless in monetary rewards and also the chance for the crowd to improve their skills, however there is one major difference between the online orgs; emotional attachment, which comes from a) the community culture and b) a "Junkie culture" (Brabham 2009). Threadless has created a community, much like a social networking site, and once there is a community of friends with the same interests and values, the project can be deemed as a success. It's similar with the Junkie reason. People are addicted to 'Threadless', as one girl explains,
"some of us get iphones and then go check on the threadless blogs even if
we’re not around computers
if you go on vacation, you can go through ‘withdrawls" (Brabham 2009)


This is something iStockPhoto lacked. Perhaps it was more proffessional? Well it does not matter because the Pros of crowdsourcing here is that everyone wins! The business wins as it gets a great product. If the quality is poor, does that really outweigh getting professional designers to do the work for 10 x the price, plus you close off the potential talent out there in world?!
Also the participant wins! Whether they make money or or just want to make friends, crowdsourcing with Threadless lets you to do both.



Brabham argues that the success is down to how the crowd engages with the idea, and behind that it is the motivation. As members of Threadless blog communicate with each other, it can make the people at Threadless aware of any issues, whats good and bad and react to it. The community is imrpoving the product subconsciously!

Friday, 19 November 2010

Meeting - 18.11.2010

Yesterdays meeting was fruitful. However there is no denying that team members have been very slow in producing work. It was agreed before reading week that everyone would go away, learn as much as possible about their own roles and start their research once a topic had been fully decided. As the Scientists role is the most research intensive, it seemed fair in letting them choose the specific topic that the research would be based on. The topic surrounding the main motivations behind crowdsourcing, with a focus on looking at monetary incentives was suggested. The group collectively dismissed this topic as it was too specific and an argument would split into looking at why particular project would not work as oppose to looking at pros and cons.

So we have decided to still focus on the article by Darren Brabham (2008), 'Moving the Crowd at iStockphoto". The scientists will look find the pros and cons of these motivations and find case studies to back up their findings. I still believe that focusing on monetary incentives alone would be a good topic, but in the interest of the project and TIME, it's very important that we produce results now.
The inspiration for the argument is not entirely current. The article is 2 years old, but as long as we can look at current debates to give weight to the argument, then that should be okay.
I am personally a little frustrated. I think we have been very slow in getting the research done. This could be my fault as PM, as I did not release a detailed work structure soon enough, and perhaps my weekly report, which is updated by team members so we can all see what everyone is doing, should have also been released earlier. As PM, I have to be the one to look at myself before anyone else, and if I set deadlines right away, instead of waiting and letting people share ideas, and setting informal deadlines (through email instead of GANTT chart) perhaps we would see more results. I started off as a Macro style manager, and as the days have gone by, I have rejected that method and become more of a micro-manager. Maybe I'm at fault, but that does not mean I have been inactive. I have pushed for team members to get involved and discuss our topic so we can move to the next stage, but the response was, up until yesterday a poor one, with only 3 members at most contributing. 
However, there are positives as well. After yesterdays meeting, everyone has agreed to now give it their all in producing a great project. We are by no means in a situation where the project is in a desperate state. I think some solid focus is needed, and the work will now be condensed to fit the tighter margin. I aim to start preparing for the presentation by the 2nd of December, which gives people ample to practice. However the next 1o days will make or break us.

As Project Manager I have definitely learnt from my mistakes. I'm not sure if after releasing the GANTT chart as soon as the roles were decided would have meant that the work would be in a better state now, however I definitely can not say that it wouldn't be. I think that has been my mistake which haunts me all the time. Although my team think I'm doing a good job, I can't help but think I'm largely at fault. 
However, if we produce some good work, and everyone gets their part completed successfully, I'll be a happy man and feel that I have personally redeemed myself. Until that happens though, I simply can not rest!

Thursday, 18 November 2010

Money money money

The group decided how to refine and narrow down the project on Tuesday. We are just waiting for some confirmation/ feedback to see if the topic is OK to investigate and research.

The exact wording of the question has not been fully confirmed, but it will be around the motivations/incentives for people to participate in crowdsourced projects. Our research will concentrate specifically on the monetary benefits, posing the question, "Will a crowdsourced project fail due to lack of monetary motivation?"/ "Crowdousrcing: The biggest incentive is money for your ideas."

From this, the positives and negatives, pros and cons of crowdsourcing can be derived and delivered in what we hope will be a succinct and convincing argument.  I have been very keen in getting the topic refined. As mentioned in my previous blogs, there is just such a MASSIVE and varied amount of information on crowdsourcing. I think it's much better to have a lot of information to choose from, rather than have very little. The downside to too much choice is that it takes a while for people to make a decision, and as other group members have not been as vocal as I would have hoped, it has been slightly difficult in getting to the middle of that hour glass. Having said that, the scientists have found a good topic which I hope we will be able to really get our teeth in to!!

Ours is a current debate, and because there are so many arguments around the topic, we have found that there is no definitive answer shared by all. One argument about motivations is considered by the Godfather of Crowdsourcing, Jeff Howe. His study of 'istock photo' a crowdsourcing company which essentially undercuts competitors by 90% (wired magazine: 2006), found that one of the biggest motivations was financial. Istock photos is a brilliant crowdsourcing example. When companies, groups, individuals, practically anyone who wants professional photos for a particular need, they would usually go to a professional photographer. Companies would pay thousands of pounds to use a photographer to use their quality photographs. People would pay a huge amount and then in some cases not even have full rights to the photographs they have bought, i.e the royalties could still be with the photographer. To put t simply Istock photo allowed regular people as well as photographers to upload photos for professional use. The money would be cut by 90%, bad for the photographer, great for the customer. Customers pay a decent amount of money to someone who might not even be a photographer by trade but as the quality is so good, or more importantly, good enough, the Customer gets a good deal and the photographer gets paid well enough. 

As Istockphoto has evolved, it has opened up to more than photos. Illustrations, videos, logos are all part of the products line that the company now offers. Whats the motivation behind this? Well Jeff Howe argues that financial gain is definitely one of the larger reasons. The motivation of financial gain is something to consider. Would the quality be as good and would the business succeed if crowdsources did not offer a financial reward. As mentioned in a previous blog, there may be time when in the near future if not already that people class them selves as crowdsourcing freelancers. In this case money would be the only factor as it would be their income!

The second link below is a paper about the 'crowds' motivation by Darren Brabham. Group 12 members have used this as the major source of inspiration for creating the topic. He gives the example of itstockphoto and argues that 90% of the crowd want to make money from their involvement.

The project will develop and we will find some interesting things. The one fear that I have is that I hope we are not treading on ground that has been covered so many times that whatever we find is redundant. At the same time, as there are so many arguments about true motivation, we can still add to the debate. The scientists work continues, and I'll b back with more info from other roles too. I'm keen to see how the Lawyers get on with this.

Side note:
As an ex political science student, I found David Cameron's idea of the big society quite interesting. This is yet another form of crowdsourcing. Getting members of society to give to their community, ideas, inspiration and to breed new life into ways of doing things. Whats the incentive? A better community for its inhabitants. No money, no fun factor, no kudos = ...... no success..... well we'll wait and see.

Sunday, 14 November 2010

General to specific

We have team members who have shared many great ideas around the subject of Crowdsourcing. It's a great topic which is so varied. You find forms of crowdsourcing in any field, not just the obvious ones like technology or advertising, although in my opinion these are the most interesting and I have tried to convey this to group members. Crowdsourcing exists in any field that requires the inspiration, advice and general input of 'the many' unseated of 'the few'. As the father of Crowdsourcing sates, "outsourcing is so so 2003". Where as outsourcing looks more towards the Indian, Chinese and South American market for cheaper labour, crowdsourcing actually takes it to the next level. Organisations are constantly told to think out the box. Well what do you get if tens of thousand to even millions of people are lending their ideas, and in many cases expertise to you for nothing!?

Such things as Citizen Science, where organisations such as NASA are funding researches like 'Galazy Zoo' who use the public to help classify galaxies are very interesting. Their tag line is - "Welcome to Galaxy Zoo, where you can help astronomers explore the Universe".This is one example of crowdsourcing, which has been suggested by one of the scientists in our group.

Other group members, for example the CEO has mentioned how crowdsourcing companies have succeeded by offering the unique service of 'ideas and inspiration'. Companies such as '99design' allow businesses to post graphic work that needs to be done. It is claimed that "Within hours of a project being posted, designers from all around the world compete by uploading fully completed concept designs, which are than reviewed and rated by the project poster. At the end of the project period, a winning design is chosen and paid out the prize money" (crunchbase.com). This video explains more:
http://www.facebook.com/video/video.php?v=92387385788



Companies also use Crowdsourcing to help solves problems of efficiency and productivity within certain departments. For example 'Crowdspirit' is a company which posts challenges set by another company that needs help. People are invited to solve the problem. 'Innovators' are then chosen and they join a project team to solve issues and are rewarded if the project is successful. This is very interesting. This may hint to the fact that we may have people in a few years time classing themselves as professional freelance crowdsourcers.

Smart phone applications is probably one of the most relevant topics around crowdsourcing and one that has had one of the biggest affect on our lives than any other sub field within this topic. Companies such as Apple and Android encourage others to make 'apps' and so have a never ending supply of ideas and innovations to help promote their phones. A fantastic way of selling phones is to say 'we have apps for doing literally anything you want' and they have not spent a penny in making them. The incentive for people to make apps is really simple; kudos.

Crowdsourcing is a vast subject area and the debate goes on about how to refine and then define a topic. This will be done very soon and then the blog will be full of interesting facts, figures and ideas about 'an' area of crowdsourcing rather than a few general overviews.
