After spending over 4,500 hours ( between 11th July, 2007 and 9th January, 2009 ) on classifying 1.2 million galaxies in Galaxy Zoo One ( working on 6 seconds for each galaxy not including time for closer analysis and downloading some images ), I now have over 6,000 images from the SDSS Database stored in my hard drive. These are objects that interest me through my own intuition. In time, I intend to produce some kind of useful catalog sorting out and classifying them according to types and other meaningful categories for users' reference. Hopefully, this will be my minor contribution to the magnanimous mission of the SLOAN FOUNDATION and an expression of my personal appreciation of the beauty and inspirations made possible by this great scientific project. The following images are just a small sample of the vast collection of beautiful and awe inspiring astronomical objects kept in the treasure chest of the SDSS database waiting to be discovered by the curious adventurers.

It must be pointed out here that SDSS differs from other space observatories such as HUBBLE, HERSCHEL, SPITZER, etc in that it is a large scale astronomical survey  instrument. It's main purpose is to cover as many objects and the largest possible sky area in the shortest possible time. Therefore, the images are usually not as sharp as its space observatory counter parts which are used to home in on specific objects of interest for close-up and detailed study. However, there are exceptionally spectacular images on SDSS as well. For example, the SDSS image of the NEEDLE GALAXY ( NGC 4565 ) is reputed to be better than the HUBBLE image on the same object but this is more an exception than the rule ( See images below -  the first one is from the HUBBLE HERITAGE SITE while the second one is from SDSS Database ). One must also appreciate that this trade off between quantity ( SDSS ) and quality ( HUBBLE ) is justified by the far lower cost of operation ( including repairs and maintenance ) for ground base instruments than for the space observatories and in particular the extensive survey mission of the SDSS.
