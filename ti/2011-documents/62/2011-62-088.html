Will this years Boston Enterprise 2.0 Conference use crowdsourcing effectively?

3
November 28, 2010
by John F Moore
Image by Alex Dunne via Flickr

The Boston Enterprise 2.0 Conference did a terrible job with crowdsourcing the event submissions and selections last year.  There was a lot of frustration, primarily due to lack of clarity and communication in the selection process, which I summed up in April by noting “Do not leave crowdsourcing to the “wisdom of the crowd”“. 

The failings of a year ago were understandable as the committee was learning how to effectively use crowdsourcing as part of their conference.  It successfully created a lot of “buzz” early in the process and I am pleased to see that the lack of clarity in the process has been cleared up, with the rules noting “Top number of votes does not mean automatic inclusion in the conference. In addition to the wisdom of the crowd, the amount and depth of feedback and collaboration around a particular proposal, and the creation of a balanced program for the different topic areas will be considered“.  This matters because crowdsourcing IS a popularity contest, most often for the author of the content vs. the content itself.  Maintaining final control of the sessions enables the conference planners to ensure the voices of participants are heard but that these voices do not drown out the overall goals of the conference.

While my proposed process changes have not been implemented the conference is off to a good start and I will let you know how effectively the process works from the position of someone who is submitting a proposal.  My proposed session will focus on the use of Government 2.0 strategies and tools (Public Sector Enterprise 2.0) by the state of California.  This session will go into much greater detail about the project, and those that have followed to create innovation and jobs in California.

I will let you know how this process works, and will certainly ask for your support in voting for the proposal, as we enter 2011.

John
