¡Los traductores no son necios! 5 lecciones I aprendido de Didiergate

nov 14
Diverso

Hace unas semanas, Lionbridge VP Desiderio Hélin escribió un correo electrónico a todos los traductores independientes de Lionbridge que les pedían un descuento del 5% debido a las medidas de austeridad. Un blogger nombrado Miguel Llorens llevó muy hábilmente una campaña en línea contra Lionbridge que parecía almacenar mucha ayuda de la comunidad de la traducción.  La campaña incitó una respuesta pública del CEO Rory Cowan de Lionbridge. El asunto incluso consiguió su propio apodo: Didiergate.

Aquí están algunas de las lecciones I aprendido de este asunto:

Los correos electrónicos pueden ser peligrosos. ¿Usted ha lamentado nunca el hacer clic del botón del envío? Sé que tengo. El envío de un correo electrónico a mil personas tarda un segundo pero puede tardar años para deshacer. Aposté que habían dado Desiderio que Hélin habría hecho cosas diferentemente él otra oportunidad.

La sincronización era pobre. La petición de la reducción de costes de Sr. Hélin vino apenas algunos días antes de ganancias del expediente divulgado de Lionbridge. Esto hizo el parecer de Lionbridge una empresa de la dinero-cavadura que suda su trabajo; también hizo que parecen mentirosos.

Los traductores no son necios. La petición de la reducción de costes incitó un esfuerzo en línea concertado por los traductores por todo el mundo, usando Twitter y otros foros en línea, para resistir la petición de la reducción de costes. Lionbridge pensó probablemente que el 5% cortado encontrarían un mínimo de resistencia, pero la comunidad del traductor probó que él puede ser un grupo decidido cuando sea necesario.

La pluma es más poderosa que la espada. Un blogger poco conocido puso en marcha una campaña que alcanzó a muchos centenares de gente y trató Lionbridge un sacador poderoso en su cara. ¿David contra Goliat? Miguel Llorens nos tiene mostrado cómo el blogging potente puede ser y cómo puede ser un cambiador del juego cuando está utilizado sabiamente.

Las reducciones de costes continuarán. Cuando estaba en la conferencia de AMTA en Denver hace 2 semanas, alguien mencionó el recibir de una petición similar de la reducción de costes del thebigword (otra compañía grande de la traducción); qué ventajas yo para creer que la mayor parte del LSPs grande está intentando exprimir sus traductores. Esto empujará probablemente costes de la traducción hacia abajo a largo plazo. Y el uso creciente de la traducción automática en flujos de trabajo de la producción también aplicará la presión a la baja en precios de la traducción.

ninguno

  • Monica Oliveira
    15 noviembre 2010 en el 8:37 PM

    I think this discussion became a crusade of the good against evil and is preventing translators from being objective about the fact that translation prices are going down regardless of what big LSPs want to pay. The focus should be on how to effectively revert this or find another way to work the same time and make more money. Businesses had to reinvent themselves when faced with a crisis; McDonalds is selling healthy food.
    In my personal opinion, what is bringing the prices down is the large supply of translators in the market. This year, at the ATA, there were 5-6 booths representing universities. In the recent past, you would only find Monterey Institute and Kent University. It does not matter in which side of the political spectrum you are, supply and demand works and there is not much we can do about it.
    What I see happening is that the large customers (on the top of the food chain) will keep pushing down the prices until students (on the bottom of the chain) lose interest on becoming translators because of low pay. Prices, then, will go back up because there will be only enough translators for the amount of work out there. This happened to nurses in the USA, where, today, the medical industry is giving all kind of incentives to people who are willing to become nurses and teach in nursing schools.
    Translators are wasting their time and energy with vilifying the big LSPs, which will take them nowhere. Only an objective analysis will allow translators to make the right decisions for themselves and for the translators’ community. My friends and I boycott Walmart and this is not preventing it from being one of the largest companies in the world. Forgive me, but Miguel Llorens rhetoric reminds me of myself as a student in Brazil, in the 80’s, throwing eggs on the US Embassy walls, protesting against the American big corporations exploitation of South American countries. Translators need to focus, and put their energy in the right place. Have you seen anyone survive swimming against the current?
    Translators have been working with 2 wrong assumptions: 1) translators that charge less are not good, and 2) there is plenty of work that pays what the well established translators can charge. The big LSPs have been delivering for years to demanding customers like Microsoft and companies in the medical and pharma industries, which is a clear indication that they get quality for what they are paying. The customers that don’t negotiate prices are those that spend little with translation because this is not a business for them; 2-3 cents per word will make no difference at the end of the quarter. They work with 2 translators per language and this is all they can “employ”. How many of this type of customers do we need to feed an increasing number of translators?
    The big LSPs are in a very competitive market because the number of words their customers put in the translation market is such that they can demand lower rates in exchange of volume, and volume gives work to many translators. It is that simple, and we need to work with this fact.

    16 noviembre 2010 en el 4:29 AM

    @Monica Very often the only ones to survive are the ones swimming against the current because they are cleverer: they oblige themselves to use their minds. As these are simply changing times, we all have to react and no reaction except “whining” is wasted time. I agree it is not protesting in itself that changes things, but the reasonings, the conclusions which come with the protests, as people start to “think” and search for and find solutions for their “own” problems.

    I think you’re completely right when you say that their will be a new demand for high quality SERVICE (not translations) at a certain point (not far away I think as I notice in my own work) as the nurse example you give. The visibility of the big ones may hide the little ones, but it does not “erase” the little ones. We are reacting at the big ones as we know that their visibility influences the changes to come. Eyes opening reactions are always positive, whether it are a few translators who open their eyes or some companies (buyers).

    But I totally disagree with the way we are all talking about “prices” for our work, when in fact we are talking about “costs”. Except some idiots, many companies do not wish low “prices”, they instead wish to pull down their costs. If a company like LB includes low-price-workers, that is their business, not mine and it is only one way to lower companies linguistic costs. As the text amounts are growing, also the demand for translated tests will grow. The “cost” of the solutions for that problem must obviously stay within reasonable limits. But that cost does not depend (only) on the “prices” of single translations or translators. When this solution includes freelancer work, normal longterm reasoning companies know how much outsourcing to a freelancer costs. So they will have to find combined solutions which includes also the cost of a freelancer (or freelancers) but which cut the amounts of “work” to give out to that freelancer(s). As a matter of fact, we and many companies are talking too much about translation. We and they should reason a lot about “writing” reducing words and texts “to be translated”. That’s why machine translation must come and must be developed in a serious way. My time to work on a MT provided text will have a every year growing PRICE, so if you want me to spend little time on it, you will have to provide me with a good text. But MT is not the only solution and will be, still for a while, a solution only for big companies. Companies should work on their upstream text-creation processes. Writing less and better results in less time to spend in translation (= lower costs) and in problemless translation processes or even problemless relationships with foreign language speaking partners. It does not matter at all to a freelancer how much words he translate for “one” customer! A freelancer combines different customers to create his earnings. His “prices” are only important for “his” processes and “his” earnings and definitely not for the buyer as so many translators think (and you are saying to). Frauke
