Amazon Mechanical Turk: Easy Way to Make Extra Money, or Waste of Time?

Can You Really Make Easy Money Online with Amazon's Mechanical Turk? Find Out Here

Paul Diamond Blow, Yahoo! Contributor Network
Dec 14, 2009 "Contribute content like this. Start Here."
  • More:
  • Mechanical Turk
  • Turk
  • Amazon Mechanical Turk
  • Mechanical
  • Waste of Time
Like many others, I'm always looking for new ways to make some extra money on the web. I recently heard about Amazon Mechanical Turk, which is supposed to be a market place of sorts where "requesters" post
 small tasks called "Human Intelligence Tasks" (HITs), each one with the amount of money the requester is paying to have the task completed by the Mechanical Turk workers. The Mechanical Turk workers can accept any HIT they are qualified to complete and then are paid once their work has been reviewed and accepted by the requester. However, if the requester does not approve the work they will not pay for the task. At any given time there are about 250,000 HITs available to complete, and the Mechanical Turk workers can work on these tasks at home in their spare time. It's easy to sign up to be a Mechanical Turk worker, and since the site is run by Amazon.com -- an upstanding member of the internet community -- I figured it was not a scam and worth a shot... so I signed up with Mechanical Turk and began perusing the HITs looking for quick and easy tasks to complete and earn some extra money online in my spare time.

The biggest problem with being a Mechanical Turk worker is that it is too time consuming looking through all the HITs listed finding tasks that are worth completing. The fact is, short and simple, that most of the tasks listed pay anywhere from one penny to fifty cents or so, and most require more time and effort than is worth the meager rewards. For instance, there are scores of HITs paying one penny to have you check out a website, add comments to a blog or website, or adding someone's Facebook profile as a friend. Many of these small-paying tasks actually require you to register with the website you are checking out or create some sort of account, or you are asked to supply your email address.
