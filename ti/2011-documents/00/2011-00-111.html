Crowdfunding creative projects

By
Gijsbert Koren
 – June 11, 2010Posted in: Creative projects, Crowdfunding, IndieGoGo, RocketHub

Crowdfunding has many faces; when someone talks about crowdfunding, he or she either means investing, supporting, loaning or donating. The concept of investments, loans and donations are clear, but what is supporting exactly?

Crowdfunding creative projects is about supporting: the crowd finances a creative project in return for rewards. For example: in the case of a musician, the reward for a $10 funding could be a copy of a CD, for $25 a signed limited edition of the same CD and for $250 a meet and greet with the musician. There are quite some crowdfunding platforms using this model, especially in the field of creativity.

“Crowdfunding is a collaborative funding model; many people give smaller amounts of money to make big things happen“ Slava, IndieGoGo

IndieGoGo & RocketHub

Two open crowdfunding platforms for creative projects are IndieGoGo and RocketHub. I interviewed the founders of both platforms to get more insight into their businesses. Both IndieGoGo and RocketHub are open platforms, which means that everyone can post a project and the supporters decide which projects will make it. Two examples of closed platforms are Kickstarter and Africa Unsigned; these platforms pre-select the best projects to provide more quality. But why shouldn’t the crowd decide what quality is?

“Crowdfunding is there to be able to make an emotional connection with strangers!“ Vlad, RocketHub

IndieGoGo
IndieGoGo is “the crowdfunding platform for anything creative” and is founded in 2008. Since the start, hundreds of projects have been funded by people form over 100 countries! Which shows that crowdfunding doesn’t have any geographical boundaries!

RocketHub
RocketHub is an “open grassroots crowdfunding platform for creatives by creatives” and is founded in 2009. Vlad, one of the founders of RocketHub, explains: “Our mission is to liberate creativity.” Bet you never heard your bank manager say that!

The numbers

Both IndieGoGo and RocketHub don’t charge funders any fee. But they do charge the creatives behind the projects. At IndieGoGo creatives pay 7% over the total amount raised if the project is fully funded and 12% if it’s not fully funded. So, contrary to most crowdfunding platforms, IndieGoGo always passes on the money. “Because the founders want to execute the project anyway and in case you funded the project, you also want the project to be executed!”, says Slava. RocketHub charges its creatives 8% and the creatives only get their money if they reach their goal. “If a project is fully funded, it is validated by the crowd and when it’s not, the project was maybe not so good after all.”, explains Vlad. The average size of fundings at RocketHub is between $50 and $60 and the most popular size is $20 and the average funding needed is between $2000 and $3000.

Successful projects

So, how to successfully raise money through crowdfunding for your creative project?

Both Vlad and Slava give some great insights into critical success factors.

Vlad’s Holy Grail
1. The team and the project are interesting and innovative
2. The team has an extensive and warm online network
3. Funders get interesting rewards for their support

Slava’s Golden Rules
1. Pitch your project (give interesting rewards, make a good video and have a good project)
2. Be pro active (use email, twitter, facebook and follow up with people)
3. Get into your community (you need a big network)
4. Create an engaged audience (find people who care about your project)

RocketHub offers an interesting incentive to investors to overcome hurdles in funding projects: badges! Just like you can earn badges on foursquare, RocketHub gives you the opportunity to show off with your badges. It is for instance possible to earn the Spark badge if you are the first to fund a project, or the Broadcaster badge if you share a project via Facebook or Twitter. What more could you think of? A Capitalist badge for the biggest investor in a project and a Changemaker badge for funding a project that is less than 50% funded?

“Most projects are for 80% funded by people from within the network of the founders and for 20% by strangers” explains Slava. This implicates that crowdfunding platforms are far from able to attract investors themselves; the entrepreneurs still need to do the hard work of finding funds. Vlad adds: “Some really interesting projects succeed in turning this number around and engage 80% strangers to fund a project. If this happens, you know you have something really good in your hands.” This is exactly what happened with Darko, who crowdfunded the New York launch of his award winning movie at RocketHub and was able to convince a complete stranger to fund $5000. Darko offered a great reward for this investment: he showed the funder around in Belgrade for a weekend, where the movie was shot.

Interesting rewards are key, that is also what singer/songwriter Jeneen showed at RocketHub when she baked a pie everyday and everyone who funded her project that day, could meet her in the park to taste the pie! This special reward worked amazingly well and she successfully funded her third CD.

Vlad: “When you think out of the box and are able to offer really interesting rewards, you will convince people to fund you project. And the success of crowdfunded projects continues after they are fully funded; larger investors and producers become very interested when you are able to convince a large group of people to fund your project!”

The future of crowdfunding

How do the founders of IndieGoGo and RocketHub think about the future of crowdfunding? Slava: “We will have an economy where everyone will be leading their own funding campaigns.”, which is quite similar to what Vlad thinks: “I think that we’ve only begun to scratch the surface of what crowdfunding can accomplish. I believe that a far-out but eventual use for crowdfunding will be grassroots public works projects like parks and infrastructure upgrades. Crowdfunding has the ability to give communities the power to organize and prioritize local improvements.”

We still have a long way to go before crowdfunding gets more mainstream. One hurdle to take are rules and regulations. This is why Paul started a project at IndieGoGo to change the crowdfunding law in the United States to make crowdfunding legal!

Let’s democratize the economy and liberate creativity!
