One Step Beyond Design - Crowdsourced Manufacturing

The logical next step after crowdsourcing a product's design is to crowdsource the manufacturing of that same product. Are you scratching your head at what that could look like? I am too.

A few weeks back at CrowdConf, I met Jerry from Innovative Design Works. Jerry shared his vision of a crowdsourcing service that takes product design one step further... to manufacturing. Given the lack of engineering in my background and very limited manufacturing experience, I couldn't figure out how this would work. But a follow up article from Jerry helped me visualize the potential.

The proliferation and affordability of 3D Printers may enable crowdsourced manufacturing.

This could happen on two levels:

1. The crowdSPRING approach to crowdsourced manufacturing. A crowd of engineers, designers or manufacturers could create simple prototypes of a product using their 3D Printers. The project host could examine the different prototypes and select the winner who would be awarded with a one-time prize or a longer-term manufacturing contract.

2. The Amazon Mechanical Turk approach to crowdsourced manufacturing. A crowd of engineers, designers or manufacturers would be assigned manufacturing tasks for different components of a larger product. The project host would have the master design and assembly responsibility. This should not be a stretch of the imagination; it's what Boeing did with the Dreamliner. The difference between Boeing's efforts and a crowdsourced manufacturing approach is in the fact that Boeing had used pre-screened, qualified manufacturing partners in the operation whereas a true crowdsourced approach would have much looser ties among the project host and community of manufacturers.

Both approaches have their hurdles. The first one begs the question of how the manufacturer could add any benefit if they are working off of a single design provided by the project host.

The second approach would seem to be plagued by the same (if not more) issues that the Boeing Dreamliner project encountered in this distributed work model.

Crowdsourced manufacturing is not here yet, but it's conceivable that it's not far off either.

Crowdsourcing and Product or Service Design - Where they overlap.

The majority of today's crowdsourcing projects today are focused on tasks that require explicit knowledge; that is knowledge that is easily communicated. For example a simple crowdsourcing task may be to look at an image and assign a set of tags to it. The process, tools, and marketplace to crowdsource these types of tasks are readily available in the market today. Think of these as left-brain activities.

What is not as easy to crowdsource are projects that require tacit knowledge; that is knowledge that is not as easily communicated. Think of these as the right-brain activities. Examples of these projects may involve product or service design.

It's most logical to use the users or customers of a product or service to accomplish these types of tasks. In a physical setting, an organization may use the principles and process of "design thinking" to help with this ambiguous task of product or service design. Early on in the design process, an organization would use a focus group, ethnographic research or other forms of information gathering and observation.

Organizations that are already using crowdsourcing for other tasks may be able to reuse their crowd, process and technology for the design process, but it must be done thoughtfully... and it won't be easy.

Here are a few ways to use crowdsourcing in the process of a product or service design:

1. Use the crowd as a virtual focus group. If the crowd is populated by the users of a product or service then the crowdsourcing project can serve the purpose of a virtual focus group - one tool in the design thinking toolboox. The crowd should be guided by a brief that has open-ended questions intended to spur thought and description from the users. The project should have a structure that gets to the desired insight but is also able to respond to the group as the discussion unfolds. The virtual focus group should be enhanced by tools that allow easy communication beyond text into video, audio, and images to say the least. The virtual focus group will also be enhanced by collaboration tools that allow individuals to build off each other's comments, just as they would in a physical setting.

2. Tap the crowd for co-creation. Crowdsourcing can also serve as a user-centered design session if the brief asks users for ideas on how to design or enhance an product or service. This takes the first example further in the sense that the users are responsible for actually creating the product or service (this may also be called "co-creation"). The crowd must be interested in and capable of the design task. This doesn't necessarily mean users creating CAD drawings; it could be as simple as a sketch or hand-drawn process flow. Finally, the tools must be in place to enable these activities in a virtual environment.

This is not an easy task and it should not be assumed to be a no-brainer. Using crowdsourcing to achieve the intent of design thinking requires a pre-assembled crowd of customers or users, a technology infrastructure that allows rich communication and collaboration in a virtual setting, and a process that has flexibility to adapt in the middle of a project.

B2B Market Research Basics, Don't Forget Them

These market research basics can be easily forgotten in the rush to get to "the answer." With a carefully structured market research strategy and technique, you can avoid these mistakes and get the right information which will make your design, advertising or distribution better in the long-run.

1. Know the decision maker.
Make sure you have identified the decision maker and any and all parties or factors which influence the decision maker. Research all of them. Your research should make clear each parties influence on how the decision maker.

2. Understand the difference between intent and action.
In market research surveys or other planning activities customers can "intend" to buy something all day long. This will never translate 100% to an actual purchase. Give yourself a break between those who intend to make a purchase and your forecast of those who actually will make a purchase. Better yet, try to understand the reason for the difference and minimize it.

3. Be careful about social desirability bias.
Everyone thinks they are perfect. Most people will also seek to appease you, the researcher. Without even knowing it, they will give you the "right" answer -- the one that makes them look better and the one you want to hear. Beware of how your questions are asked. One way to avoid this is to take the spotlight off the respondent and put it on the network around them. You can also consider using a third-party, such as a consultant, to help execute the research.

4 Tips for Consulting Firms Looking to Grow Revenues and Profits

Whinot is a virtual consulting firm that is made up of a community of independent consultants who work together on small business marketing projects. Whinot applies the power of crowdsourcing to business consulting to provide Fortune 500 consulting at small business prices.

So we are a unique beast in the sense that we are a consulting firm made up of a community of small consulting firms. I guess that's why I took interest when I saw the book Spiraling Up from Hinge Marketing. Spiraling Up is a book based on years of research on high growth consultancies. Hinge is a firm that helps professional services firms achieve this high growth. So what we have here in this blog post is a whole lot of consulting firms telling each other what to do... This could get dangerous.

But, I read the book, enjoyed it and figured I would summarize what I learned in four points. Hope you enjoy.
  1. Everyone has talented people.
    If you are a professional services firm that is trying to sell new clients with a pitch that is anywhere close to, "we have a very experienced staff," then you are in big trouble. This is not different. Nobody buys experience... in the buyer's eyes, this is a given. By the very definition of the business, you are not a professional services firm without an experienced staff.
  2. Focus, Focus, Focus, Focus.
    It it's not about people, what is it about? The focus. Focus can be defined four ways. Focus on the work you do (make it specialized), the clients you target (a narrow niche), the type of problem you solve, or the business model you use to get the work done. However you Focus... make sure it's on an aspect that matters to your clients.
  3. Listen, Understand, Customize.
    Clients don't buy what you are selling. They buy solutions to their own problems (or so they think). Clients care less about thought leadership and canned offerings than they do about the solution to their problem. So with each client you have to listen, understand, and customize a solution to fit their needs. But, this doesn't mean reinvent the wheel every time. If you have focused enough (see #2), then you will have likely solved a very similar problem in the past. Use that case study to demonstrate your aptitude and use the lessons from that engagement for this client... but don't present it as a canned solution.
  4. Be smart about sales and marketing.
    Every sales and marketing dollar are not created equal. Some are more profitable and efficient than others. Use your website to prospect and convert sales leads. Make personal visits when the time is right (e.g. you need to do step #3). Use referrals and be focused in your non-referral sales dollars. Sell through partners... the list continues.
The end result, the authors claim, is higher growth rates, more efficient (profitable) growth and no need to compete on price.
