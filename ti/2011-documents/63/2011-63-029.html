An Objective Look At Crowdsourcing Your Company Logo Design

69
rate or flag this pageTweet this

By timothy82rodrigu


Finding an experienced, talented designer to create a professional logo for your company is challenging. Ideally, you want to find someone who uses a rigorous design process. That person should be willing to research your industry, and sketch and prototype concepts that communicate the right message. He or she should also take a keen interest in how you plan to use your logo. You might need it delivered in different file formats to make it easier to incorporate into Flash animations, print brochures, large-scale media (e.g. billboards), even bumper stickers.

Such a designer rarely, if ever, emerges from a crowdsourcing project. Nevertheless, many companies continue to rely on this type of arrangement for their logos. The discussion that follows will explain the concept of crowdsourcing, and present some of the hurdles you'll confront during the process. This is not meant to dissuade you from taking this path. Instead, our goal is to prepare you for the pitfalls that have ensnared others.

Logo Design Crowdsourcing Explained

Crowdsourcing is a method for placing your project in front of a large group of contractors, and inviting each to make a contribution. In the context of logo design, it usually takes the form of a contest. Designers submit their logos, and you choose from among them, and pay the winner.

On the surface, this seems like an appealing arrangement for the company. It's not uncommon to attract dozens of designs for the price you might otherwise pay to have a single professional logo designed. There are, however, a few potential hurdles.

Establishing A Reasonable Price To Attract Volume

Setting a price for your design contest presents a double-edged sword. A low price will help preserve your budget, but the quality of the contest's output may be questionable. Moreover, a low price may limit the number of designers who participate. On the other hand, establishing a high price might improve the quality of the submitted logos, but does not necessarily guarantee you'll receive a design you can use. Keep in mind most crowdsourcing sites require you to choose a winner and award payment. Consequently, your money might be wasted.

It's worth mentioning how professional designers view such projects. Most logos are submitted privately under a non-disclosure agreement, and thus cannot be used in the designer's portfolio. So they tend to avoid these contests unless the price offered is sufficiently high.

Copyright And Trademark Infringements

 Here, we arrive at one of the most prevalent problems of crowdsourcing your logo design. A lot of the work submitted by contest entrants infringes upon existing copyrights or trademarks (or both). Sometimes, only small pieces of a design are "borrowed." Other times, major portions are pilfered. While this can occur with a professional designer, it happens far more frequently during contests. There are two main reasons.

First, the site hosting the project usually lacks the manpower to police every logo submitted to every contest. Copyright and trademark infringements slip through the cracks. Second, as mentioned, most of the work is submitted privately. This means there are fewer eyes examining the work. In the unlikely event a designer is caught "borrowing" from other designs, he or she is rarely prohibited from entering other contests.

In a worst case scenario, you would unwittingly choose the pilfered logo, and use it on your brochures, website, and other marketing collateral. The problem is, doing so places you in a vulnerable position should the copyright or trademark owner seek recompense.

Should You Crowdsource Your Logo Design?

 A lot of professional designers claim that crowdsourcing will not produce a usable logo. They argue the quality of the submitted works will be so low that such contests are a waste of time, energy, and money. In truth, there is a chance you'll attract a talented logo designer who follows a strict design process. The likelihood of finding such a designer, however, is small. For most companies that want to avoid the potential headaches of crowdsourcing, hiring a professional upfront is a better solution.
