Galaxy Zoo!

Filed under Physics, Recommended websites, Technology

The internet is a wonderful time sink, filled to the brim with interesting articles and addictive flash games. From browsing youtube to obsessively checking Facebook, we all seem to spend far too much time on the internet, especially if there’s a big assignment due the next day. In other words, there’s too much bumming around and not much productivity.

Imagine my surprise then, when I stumbled upon Galaxy Zoo. Launched by a group of innovative academics, Galaxy Zoo is an online astronomy project that anyone with an internet connection can participate in. Volunteers study pictures of distant galaxies taken by the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS), and classify them according to shape (spiral, elliptical, irregular and so on). Results are then sent to a huge database, where the raw data is complied and analysed. It sounds simple, and it is! It’s quite easy to classify the galaxies, and the catalogue of shapes given is fairly comprehensive. (Could the galaxy be a disc viewed edge on? How many spirals does it have? How tightly wound do the arms appear? How big is the galaxy’s centre relative to the rest of the galaxy?) All one has to do is click buttons.

The catalogue of shapes also leaves room for an assortment of weird and wonderful varieties, ringed galaxies, merging galaxies, even gravitational lensing. It wouldn’t be the first time someone’s spotted something strange. In August last year, a schoolteacher from the Netherlands spotted a bright blue “cloud” on one of the pictures. Dubbed Hanny’s Voorwerp (the Dutch word for ‘object’) in her honour, the voorwerp has since become the subject of a number of astronomical studies.

Green to the naked eye, the voorwerp could be a cloud of superhot gas, but there aren’t any stars near it that could excite the gas to such a temperature. What’s more, the voorwerp has a giant gaping hole in its middle, and no one is exactly sure what caused the hole or even what the voorwerp itself could be. In any case, it’s a perplexing oddity.

But back to Galaxy Zoo. Since all of the data is user-generated and subject to tons of human error, why not simply write a computer programme that automatically classifies galaxies error-free? It’s harder than it sounds. Pattern recognition is one of the most powerful traits that we possess. Our ability to instantly recognise trends and, well, patterns is miles ahead of any computer, which makes our brain the perfect galaxy-classifying tool.

Furthermore, spreading out the data in small amounts makes the monumental job of galaxy classification a little easier. What would take two astronomers years to sift through takes only months when spread out among thousands of individuals. Also, any mistakes in classification that are made will be dampened down by hundreds of other correct classifications. So the effects of any mistakes (or sabotage) will be completely negligible, especially since one has to sign up with a valid email address in order to start classifying galaxies.

So of what use is Galaxy Zoo to us? Its benefits to astronomers are obvious, but how will it help solve, say, global warming? I honestly can’t say. What I do know though, is that Galaxy Zoo has made astronomy much more accessible to me, and it feels nice to contribute to “science”! At the very least, it’s much more productive than aimlessly surfing the internet.
