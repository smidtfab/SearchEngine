A framework exploring the effects of the Machiavellian disposition on the relationship between motivation and influence tactics.

By Barbuto, John E., Jr.
Publication: Journal of Leadership & Organizational Studies
Date: Sunday, September 22 2002
Share:
Print
More

The relationship between sources of motivation and influence tactics and the moderating effect of the Machiavellian disposition are explored in this review of the literature in the areas of influence tactics, Machiavellian personality, and human motivation to develop the proposed framework(s). Propositions are developed linking motivation sources, influence tactics, and Machiavellian personality. Implications for research and practice also are discussed.

**********

"Be it known, then, that there are two ways of contending, one in accordance with the laws, the other by force, the first of which is proper to men, the second to beasts. But since the first method is often ineffectual, it becomes necessary to resort to the second. A prince should, therefore, understand how to use well both man and beast" (Machiavelli, 1513/1902, p. 79).

At the heart of Machiavelli's pronouncement are the notions of power and influence. Recent research efforts have sought to examine the specific influencing strategies used by leaders (agents) to effect changes in the decisions and actions of their followers (targets). These proactive actions have been termed influence tactics (Kipnis, Schmidt, & Wilkinson, 1980; Mowday, 1978). Influence tactics have been examined extensively and from multiple perspectives over the past twenty years (Ferris et al., 1997; Kipnis et al., 1980; Yukl, 1998). Despite the attention, there remains limited understanding of the antecedents of these tactics.

Several studies have examined the antecedents of influence tactics, using such variables as gender (Strutton, Pelton & Lumpkin, 1995; Tepper, Brown & Hunt, 1993; Thacker, 1995), objectives of influence (Yukl & Falbe, 1990), power (Hinkin & Schriescheim, 1990; Judge & Bretz, 1994; Lamude, 1994), motivation (Aguinis, Nesler, Hosoda, & Tedeschi, 1993; Barbuto, Fritz, Maxx, 2002; Barbuto & Scholl, 1999; Yukl & Falbe, 1990), direction of influence (Falbe & Yukl, 1992), and personality (Blickle, 2000; Harrell-Cook, Ferris, & Dulebohn, 1999; Kipnis et al., 1980).

This paper explores the relationship between motivation and influence tactics, and the role personality plays in this relationship. The personality variable examined in this framework is the need to develop and defend power and success, as defined by Niccolo Machiavelli (1513/1902). The perspective of organizational dynamics articulated by Machiavelli has been criticized because of its honest acceptance of aggressive--and perhaps unethical--behaviors to achieve a goal. The impact of Machiavellianism on the influence process has been examined minimally (Christie & Geis, 1979; Grams & Rogers, 1989; Kets de Vries & Miller, 1985); however, in this framework, Machiavellianism is considered for its moderating value in the relationship between motivation and influence tactics.

Three models are developed to explain the expected relationships among motivation, Machiavellianism, and influence. The first model (Figure 7) depicts situational and dispositional models and the impact of high versus low Machiavellian personalities. The second model (Figure 8) explores the direct relationship between Machiavellianism and influence tactics in initial and repeated influence attempts. The third model (Figure 9) explains the moderating effect of the Machiavellian personality on the relationship between motivation sources and influence tactic use.

For almost 500 years, since the time of Niccolo Machiavelli, leadership behaviors have been widely discussed. The perspectives of Machiavelli (1513/1902) have been adopted by scholars to examine and understand political dynamics in organizations. Machiavelli is well known for such generalizations as "the ends justify the means" and the contention that unethical behavior is acceptable, even necessary, if it helps attain goals or protect political position. Yet these perspectives capture only a faint image of what Machiavelli intended in his writings. For example, Martinez (1987) found that while high Machiavellians may endorse deceit and opportunism, they nevertheless might be as moral as low Machiavellians in other areas of sociopolitical belief. A wider perspective of the Machiavellian disposition will be discussed in this paper.

INFLUENCE TACTICS

While power is the ability to influence, influence can be defined as the actual effect on an individual's behavior, beliefs, and attitudes (Hinkin & Schriesheim, 1990). Pfeffer (1981) defined influence as the ability to exercise power in order to overcome resistance in achieving a desired objective or result. Mintzberg (1973) argued that to appreciate organizational behavior, we must understand the influencers present within the organization, as well as the needs of each influencer, and the extent to which each can exercise power to satisfy those needs. Mintzberg (1983) described influencers as the individuals who seek to control the organization's decisions and actions. The ability to influence others is at the very heart of power, politics and organizational life.

The work of Kipnis et al. (1980) first operationalized proactive influence tactics by studying agents (those using influence tactics) and the many strategies employees use to get their way with superiors. Influence tactics, as articulated by Hughes, Ginnett, and Curphy (as cited in Wren, 1995), refer to actual behaviors designed to change another person's attitudes, beliefs, values, or behaviors. The original taxonomy was derived from inductive research methods and featured six influence tactics (assertiveness, rationality, ingratiation, exchange, upward appeal, and coalition).

Schriesheim and Hinkin (1990) replicated the study by Kipnis et al. (1980) and developed new items to measure the same subscales. Yukl and colleagues (Yukl & Falbe, 1990; Yukl & Tracey, 1992) examined influence tactics from the perspectives of both agent and target, and also extended the work of Kipnis et al. by identifying the additional tactics of inspirational appeal, legitimating, consultation, pressure, and personal appeal.

Yukl and Falbe (1990) determined that consultation and rational persuasion were the most frequently used influence tactics. This conclusion coincides with the research results of Hinkin and Schriesheim (1990), who found that rational persuasion has significant positive relationships with the more positively viewed bases of power (expert and referent).

Situational predictors of the influence tactics chosen by leaders also have been examined (Barbuto, 2000; Bass, 1985; Katz & Kahn, 1978), and it can be argued that leaders use more interpersonal or inspirational influence strategies when encouraging colleagues to do tasks that they highly resist. Barbuto's (2000) review of the literature found that leaders use "soft" tactics when seeking performance beyond expectations (Barbuto, Fritz, & Marx, 2000; Barry & Shapiro, 1992; Bass, 1985; Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Morrman, & Fetter, 1990).

Barbuto, Fritz, and Marx (2002) examined the relationship between the degree to which a leader expects employee resistance to a task and the influence tactics used to gain compliance from targets. Earlier, Barbuto (2000) suggested that leaders/managers often misinterpret employee resistance to requested tasks and mistakenly underestimate a follower' s willingness to perform tasks. Barbuto et al. (2001) found that leaders use soft tactics when anticipating higher resistance from workers. They also found a correlation (r = .22) between leaders' use of soft tactics and the lowest level of anticipated resistance. Anticipated resistance was positively related to leaders' use of ingratiating, consultative, and coalition influence tactics (Barbuto, et al., 2001).

Barbuto and Scholl (1999) studied the relationships between leaders' motivation sources, their perceptions of their targets' motivation sources, and their choices of influence tactics. Results indicated strong relationships between leaders' perceptions of targets' sources of motivation and the use of influence tactics. Leaders' own sources of motivation were strongly correlated with their perceptions of their targets' motivation.

SOURCES OF MOTIVATION AND THEIR RELATIONSHIP TO INFLUENCE TACTICS

Leaders who understand their personal motivation sources, most preferred influence tactic, and least preferred influence tactic are more likely to practice more effective management. Effective management practices foster creativity in individuals (Tierney, Farmer, & Graen, 1999) and increase employee motivation (Richer & Vallerand, 1995) and self-determination (Deci, Connell, & Ryan, 1989). In addition, it has been proposed (Barbuto, 2000) that follower compliance can affect influence triggers (the reactions of followers to a leader's interventions).

Motivation has been researched from a variety of perspectives. Maslow (1943) and Alderfer (1969) developed needs-based theories of motivation; Adams (1963) examined equity theory as it relates to motivation. Herzberg studied dissatisfiers (1968); McClelland (1966) looked at motivation from the viewpoint of opportunity and challenge; and Deci (1975, 1989) examined intrinsic and extrinsic motivations.

An integrative taxonomy, built on past research efforts, can account for various motivations necessary to understand individual behavior. Leonard, Beauvais, and Scholl (1999) developed an integrative taxonomy for the five sources model, incorporating the perspectives of several motivation theorists. The five sources of motivation they identified include intrinsic process, instrumental, self-concept external, self concept internal, and goal internalization.

Described briefly, intrinsic process motivation is motivation derived from the work itself. Intrinsic process motivation is evident when workers enjoy what they are doing. Instrumental motivation derives from an individual's search for tangible outcomes such as pay, promotions, or bonuses. Self-concept external motivation is derived from identification with a group and the achievement of status and recognition from that group. Self-concept internal motivation is derived from internal standards of achievement that become the basis for the ideal self. Goal internalization motivation is derived from an individual's values and their congruence with organizational goals (Leonard, et al, 1999).

Intrinsic Process Motivation and Influence Tactics

Barbuto and Scholl (1999) found that individuals' sources of motivation are related to their choices of influence tactics; they reported positive correlations between intrinsic process motivation and legitimating, ingratiation, and personal appeals influence tactics. However, Barbuto et al. (2002) reported positive correlations between intrinsic process motivation and legitimating, rational persuasion, personal appeals, pressure, exchange, ingratiating, and coalition tactics.

Of the "hard" influence tactics, the legitimating tactic focuses on the individual's situation or position. An intrinsically motivated individual might use this tactic in order to proceed with the work he or she finds enjoyable. Ingratiation involves getting others in a good mood or causing them to think favorably of the requestor before asking them to do something. Intrinsically motivated individuals might find this kind of interaction fun and a means to get what they want. A personal appeal involves the reliance on personal favors or interpersonal relationships, and intrinsically motivated individuals might find these relationships fun to maintain. The relationship between intrinsic process motivation and influence tactics used is likely to be small, given the contradictory and inconsistent findings of past research.

Proposition 1: Individuals' intrinsic process motivation will be positively related to their use of ingratiation, personal appeals, consultative, and legitimating influence tactics. These relationships are expected to be relatively weak.

Instrumental Motivation and Influence Tactics

Individuals high in instrumental motivation are driven by the exchange of tangible benefits for their efforts. They tend to weigh the efforts of an act to the corresponding "payment" for that effort. Barbuto and Scholl (1999) found that leaders high in instrumental motivation used hard influence tactics such as pressure, legitimation, coalitions, and exchanges.

Pressure involves demands, threats and intimidation. Instrumentally motivated individuals would be comfortable using these methods to achieve their tangible goals because they place such goals above interpersonal relationships or emotional requests. Legitimating tactics seek compliance based on position or situation. Instrumentally motivated individuals are more inclined to use position and hierarchical situations to achieve tangible benefits. Coalitions involve the support of others to gain compliance--similar to creating peer pressure or ganging up on another. Instrumentally motivated individuals seek opportunities to make tangible gains with the support of others. Exchange involves reciprocation, or trading, of tangible benefits. Those motivated by tangible rewards, such as those high in instrumental motivation, would be prone to use exchange tactics. Indeed, Barbuto et al. (2002) found positive correlation between instrumental motivation and exchange.

Proposition 2: Individuals' instrumental motivation will be positively related to their use of hard tactics such as exchange, pressure, coalitions, and legitimation.

Self-Concept External Motivation and Influence Tactics

Individuals high in self-concept external motivation feel motivated by gaining approval from and affiliation with others. They may avoid alienation from the group and would want group members to think highly by them. Barbuto and Scholl (1999) found a relationship between leaders' self-concept external motivation and their use of interpersonal influence tactics such as ingratiation, personal appeals, and coalitions. Barbuto et al. (2002) found positive relationships between self-concept external motivation and personal appeals and exchange influence tactics. Across both studies, only the relationship between self-concept external motivation and personal appeals tactics was replicated. For this reason, this relationship is expected to be strongest, Leaders high in self-concept external motivation may use coalition influence tactics because they value group acceptance and pursue status within the group, and would thus be more inclined to pursue compliance from others based on social and peer group pressures.

Proposition 3: Individuals' self-concept external motivation will be positively related to their use of soft and hard influence tactics such as coalitions, personal appeals, and ingratiating tactics. The strongest relationship is expected between self-concept external motivation and personal appeals tactics.

Self-Concept Internal Motivation and Influence Tactics

Individuals high in self-concept internal motivation are internally motivated. They set personal standards for performance and self monitor their progress, striving to reach optimal personal achievement. Barbuto and Scholl (1999) found self-concept internal motivation to be related to leaders' use of soft tactics (inspirational appeals and consultative tactics). However, Barbuto et al. (2002) found significant negative relationships between self-concept internal motivation and both legitimating tactics and rational persuasion. Given the conflicting results between the two studies, it is prudent to anticipate modest relationships.

Inspirational appeals are characterized as those that tap into values and emotions. Since self-concept internally motivated individuals have a clear concept of their values, they are more likely to tap into the values of others.

Consultative tactics are used by those who solicit participation from others in decision-making--usually seeking from targets at early stages of a project input about how to proceed with plans. Because self-concept internally motivated individuals have high standards for their own performance, they are likely to seek input from talented individuals to best reach their personal achievement level.

Proposition 4: Individuals' self-concept internal motivation will be positively related to their use of soft influence tactics such as inspirational appeals and consultative tactics. Modest relationships should be expected.

Goal Internalization Motivation and Influence Tactics

Individuals high in goal internalization as a motivation source will place high valence on the degree to which goals of the organization are congruent with their own values and beliefs. Barbuto and Scholl (1999) found a relationship between goal internalization motivation and leaders' use of soft tactics, such as inspirational appeals and consultation. However, Barbuto et al. (2002) did not find any significant relationships between goal internalization and influence tactics. Goal internalized individuals are more predisposed to focus on the values of the organization when influencing others, and thus may rely upon inspirational appeals that tap into the values and emotions of targets to gain compliance. They also might seek participation in decisions from those most capable of contributing in order to meet the organizational goals.

Proposition 5: Individuals' goal internalization motivation will relate to their use of inspirational appeals and consultative tactics.

MACHIAVELLIANISM AND ITS RELATIONSHIP TO INFLUENCE TACTICS

"It is necessary, indeed, to put a good colour on this nature, and to be skillful in simulating and dissembling. But men are so simple, and governed so absolutely by their present needs, that he who wishes to deceive will never fail in finding willing dupes ... A Prince should therefore be careful that nothing ever escapes his lips which is not ... the embodiment of mercy, good faith, integrity, humanity and religion ..." (Machiavelli, 1513/1902, p. 80)

Machiavelli's (1513/1902) work The Prince provided the basis of the Machiavellian personality type coined by Christie and Geis (1970). The traditional Machiavellian perspective advocates that the leader's main goal is to be in power at all costs, whereby the end justifies the means as long as power is retained.

The Machiavellian personality type has been researched extensively. Initial research found individuals who were strong in the Machiavellian disposition to be controlling, manipulative, and ruthless (Christie & Geis, 1970). However, recent research has shown that individuals higher in the Machiavellian disposition are more flexible in their choices of influence tactics than individuals lower in the Machiavellian disposition (Grams & Rogers, 1989), and are more likely to exhibit self-monitoring behaviors (Snyder, 1974). The Machiavellian personality has been positively correlated with certain types of planning for communication in interpersonal situations, indicating that high Machiavellians give thought to how to influence others (Allen, 1990).

Those who score high on a Machiavellian assessment instrument would be more flexible in choosing influence tactics most likely to lead to follower compliance (Carpenter, 1990). Those scoring low on a Machiavellian assessment would be less likely to strategically alter their behavior. Another factor contributing to the high Machiavellian's flexibility of behavior might be the ability to use self-monitoring to read and use environmental cues to determine behavior. Research has shown a strong relationship between Machiavellianism and self-monitoring (Leone, 1994; Snyder, 1974).

Under the situational model, a high Machiavellian disposition would affect the relationship between an individual's motivation source and influence tactic choice, because he or she would be able to alter behavior according to the situation. By contrast, the dispositional model indicates that the low Machiavellian is less likely to alter behavior. Therefore, the situation does not become a factor in that individual's influence behavior, demonstrating again that the individual's motivation source has a direct relationship with his or her influence tactic choice.

Machiavellianism in Initial and Repeated Influence Attempts

Machiavellians display superb negotiation skills, and their ability to influence is impressive (Christie & Geis, 1970). Kets de Vries and Miller (1985) related narcissism to the Machiavellian personality when they described the self-deceptive variety of narcissism. Narcissistic individuals were said to display a lack of empathy and fear of failure and were considered "ideal-hungry," preoccupied with their own needs, and strongly desirous of being loved, as well as having a transactional/instrumental orientation.

Grams and Rogers (1989) examined influence tactics and personality characteristics and found that the choice of influence tactic differs dramatically according to whether a person is high or low in the Machiavellian disposition. Individuals with a high Machiavellian disposition are more motivated to succeed, more assertive, and less manipulative. Additionally, resistance from the target changes the leader's influence strategy. Those high in the Machiavellian disposition prefer to use indirect (emotion) and non-rational (reward) persuasion techniques. High Machiavellians display positive emotional techniques (flattery, friendliness) that aid in their influence attempts. Individuals high in Machiavellianism want to succeed by using the least obtrusive means possible but are willing to resort to stronger or harder tactics if necessary.

Low Machiavellians, on the other hand, use logic and simple statements of their requests and seem not to understand that others might resist their logical approach. They demonstrate inflexibility in behavior (Grams & Rogers, 1989), and Fry (1985) found that low Machiavellians are particularly vulnerable in face-to-face bargaining situations. This suggests that individuals low in Machiavellianism are more likely to allow their dispositions to determine their behavior, whereas those leaders high in Machiavellianism are more likely to use situational factors (such as organizational setting) to select their behavior.

Because Machiavellian personalities are characterized by concern with a personal agenda, maintaining position power, and influencing others, they are extremely flexible in their influence tactic choices. They will use whatever tactic they feel will help them maintain power and pursue their personal (and organizational) goals. However, because the Machiavellian wants to avoid being hated and develop a `popular goodwill' among his or her followers: "Nevertheless a prince ought to inspire fear in such a way that, if he does not win love, he avoids hatred" (Machiavelli, 1513/1902, p. 83).

If given the choice, the true Machiavellian leader would use soft tactics first to influence others, then, once initial soft attempts have failed, the Machiavellian leader would resort to hard influence tactics. Although Machiavellian leaders are not afraid to utilize cruel measures to attain their goals, they will do so only when softer approaches will lead to greater harm. "Therefore, a prince, so long as he keeps his subjects united and loyal, ought not mind the reproach of cruelty; because with a few examples he will be more merciful than those who, through too much mercy, allow disorders to arise, from which follow murders or robberies; for these are wont to injure the whole people, whilst those executions which originate with a prince offend the individual only" (Machiavelli, 1513/1902, p. 75).

Proposition 6: During initial influence attempts, Machiavellian personality will be positively related to the use of soft influence tactics.

Proposition 7: During repeated influence attempts, Machiavellian personality will be positively related to the use of hard influence tactics.

Role of the Machiavellian Disposition in the Relationship Between Motivation and Influence

In addition to the direct relationship Between Machiavellianism and influence tactics specified in the previous section, Machiavellianism is expected to play a moderating role between motivation sources and influence tactics because individuals high on the Machiavellian score will be comfortable using various influence strategies and methods to get what they want. An individual with a high Machiavellian score is willing to step outside the bounds of formal authority. Low Machiavellians, on the other hand, tend to be overly naive and trusting. They are poor negotiators and lack the desire to influence others (Kirkpatrick & Locke, 1991).

The revised model below shows the effect of the high Machiavellian personality type on the relationship between an individual's motivation source and the choice of influence tactic used.

Machiavellian personality would decrease the relationship between motivation sources and soft influence tactics, while increasing the relationship between motivation sources and hard influence tactics. Machiavellians will override their own motivation sources in order to achieve their personal goals.

For example, if a high Machiavellian personality had high self-concept external motivation, he or she might be expected to use influence tactics such as coalitions, personal appeals, or inspirational appeals (Barbuto & Scholl, 1999). However, because high Machiavellian individuals can easily alter their behaviors, they may use a variety of influence tactics deemed necessary to achieve their goals. In instances where Machiavellianism is high, a weakened relationship between the individual's motivation source and influence tactics is expected.

Therefore, those highly skilled in manipulation and influence to achieve their personal goals will be very difficult to distinguish from those who are simply focused on the goals of the organization. A skilled Machiavellian will be able to appear to others as genuinely concerned with others. Low Machiavellians, by contrast, would not alter their use of influence tactics because they would not see the need to do so.

Proposition 8A: High Machiavellian personality will decrease the relationship between a leader's motivation source and influence tactic used.

Proposition 8B: Low Machiavellian personality will increase the relationship between a leader's motivation source and influence tactic used.

Discussion

In this paper, we have articulated a framework for understanding the relationships among motivation, Machiavellianism, and influence tactics. The framework explores the simple dispositional relationship between sources of motivation and influence tactics, with several expected relationships identified. While these relationships have been examined empirically, with mixed results, it is prudent to consider them further when examining this framework.

A more substantive contribution of this framework is its exploration of the moderating effects of the Machiavellian personality on the relationship between motivation and influence tactics. If moderating effects were found, as expected, this would offer some explanation for the mixed results found in past studies of the relationship between motivation and influence tactics used.

Finally, this paper makes a second potent contribution with its exploration of the direct relationship between Machiavellianism and influence tactic choice in initial and subsequent influence attempts. This relationship has been studied (Grams & Rogers, 1989), but not using established constructs or measures. The framework developed herein provides the structure for a thoughtful research inquiry.

The paper features a review and extension of the motivation, influence, and Machiavellian personality literatures. Previous research on influence tactics and the Machiavellian personality has not demonstrated or articulated the role of situational variables and their effect on this relationship. In past operationalizations, the Machiavellian disposition has been overly simplified to describe such leaders as power-hungry individuals who do not consider the needs of others and consistently act in an antagonistic, forceful, and unethical manner. Through this paper, a more accurate depiction of the Machiavellian personality is offered. Machiavellian personalities first seek to influence using charm, friendliness, and tact. When these influence attempts fail, Machiavellians exhibit influence behaviors more consistent with previous definitions of the term.

Several propositions have been developed in this paper using Barbuto and Scholl's (1998; 1999) operationalization of motivation and Yukl and colleagues' (Falbe & Yukl, 1992; Yukl & Falbe, 1990; Yukl & Tracey, 1992) conceptualization of influence tactics. Individuals' intrinsic process motivation will be positively related to their use of soft influence tactics (ingratiation, personal appeals, and consultative) and one of the hard tactics (legitimating). Individuals' instrumental motivation will be positively related to their use of hard tactics such as exchange, pressure, coalitions, and legitimating. Individuals' self-concept external motivation will be positively related to their use of select soft and hard tactics (coalitions, personal appeals, and ingratiation). Individuals' self-concept external motivation will be positively related to their use of such soft tactics as inspirational appeals and consultative tactics. And, finally, individuals' goal internalization motivation will be positively related to their use of soft tactics such as inspirational appeals and consultative.

The framework articulates the role that Machiavellianism plays in these proposed relationships. Generally speaking, the relationships proposed will have a positive, moderating effect. Finally, we proposed an ordinal relationship between a Machiavellian personality and use of soft and hard tactics during initial and secondary influence attempts. During initial influence attempts, Machiavellian personality will be positively related to the use of soft influence tactics. During subsequent influence attempts, Machiavellian personality will be positively related to hard influence tactics. Because high Machiavellian personalities are aware of the need to alter behavior given situational factors, a proposition is made that the high Machiavellian personality will increase the relationship between motivation source and influence tactic used. In contrast, the low Machiavellian personality does not see the need to alter behavior in relation to the situation. Therefore, a final proposition in this paper expects that Machiavellian personality will have a negative moderating effect on the relationships between a leader's motivation source and influence tactic used. In the framework, it is expected that as Machiavellian personality increases, the relationship between motivation and influence tactics decreases.

The propositions developed in this review need to be subjected to empirical inquiry. The moderating effects of Machiavellianism will need to be measured and assessed in the relationship between motivation sources and influence tactics used. Future research also might investigate additional personality variables such as self-monitoring (Snyder, 1974) and Jungian personality theories (Jung, 1971). In addition, an examination of how the organizational context mitigates influence, regardless of the tactic used to influence, seems prudent.

The thoughts of Machiavelli, although nearly 500 years old, still contribute significantly to our perception of influence. Without a keen understanding of how to modify other's thoughts, beliefs, and behaviors, as well as one's own, many potential leaders might lose their "kingdoms." And so, we leave you with a final quote that embodies the essence of the framework developed in this paper.

"A certain Prince of our own days, whose name it is well as not to mention, is always preaching peace and good faith, although the mortal enemy of both; had he practiced them as he preaches them, would, oftener than once, have lost him his kingdom and authority" (Machiavelli, 1513/1902, p. 82).
