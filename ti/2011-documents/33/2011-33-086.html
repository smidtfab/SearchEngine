Winter, Mason and Siddharthi Suri 2010. Available at SSRN: http://ssrn.com/abstract=1691163

This paper has more of a psychology angle to it, but also has useful information for both beginning and more experienced Turk users.

  2. Adriana K said:

    Adriana K.

    1. This paper describes how to use Mechanical Turk for behavioral research, including conceptual issues (e.g. what type of studies MTurk can accommodate or how to ensure quality of the work) and technical issues (e.g. how to use the MTurk API and command line tools).

    2. The biggest strength of this paper was that it presented in great detail, and in a nicely organized fashion, the specifics of using one particular form of crowdsourcing, and so if one has not already used MTurk, they can use it as a guide rather than having to figure out the details of the API on their own.

    3. The main weakness of this paper is that it tries to cover perhaps too much ground, which makes it seem like it lacks focus-- there is a mixture of references to other studies, reports of the authors' own experiences and implementation ideas, and at the same time a tutorial on MTurk, which could have been split into two or three papers and covered in more depth.

    4. I learned that MTurk offers payment in US dollars and Indian rupees, which probably explains why the workers are mostly from the US and India.

    5. I did not understand whether or not the amount of payment actually affects the quantity of completed work-- the sentence at the top of the right column on page 8 seems ambiguous.

    6. I would like to learn more about the design of synchronous experiments, particularly the technical details and also how to generalize the given scenario for other real-life-like experiments.

    ummary of paper: Mason and Suri explore Mechanical Turk’s usefulness as an alternate venue for human subjects research in order to increase the number of researchers who use this platform and to refine study design best practices.

    2. What is the greatest strength: Mason and Suri offered an extremely thorough overview of a huge portion (if not all) of the literature that involves user testing in Mechanical Turk.

    3. Greatest weakness: This article dates itself with the last section; if AMT changes any aspect of their HIT creation process, then that entire portion of the article is no longer relevant.

    4. Something I learned: Mason and Suri’s method for creating a synchronous experiment seemed ingenious, if overly complicated and possibly inefficient.

    5. Something I didn’t understand: While protecting data gathered from an experiment should be always be a first priority, I was puzzled as to why there are so many security loopholes within Mechanical Turk – or at least enough to merit four paragraphs within this paper.

    6. Something I’d like to know more about: While briefly mentioned in the Schmidt paper, I’d like to see more studies conducted about the usability and user experience of being involved in human subjects research within Mechanical Turk.

    1. Mason and Suri provide a thorough exploration of the variety of affordances for behavioral research on the Mechanical Turk platform, from asynchronous surveys to real-time experiments, along with practical advice on IRB issues and research validity and security.

    2. The strength of the paper is the comparative data on research gathered through Mechanical Turk with data gathered through research in a physical environment indicating the reliability of Mechanical Turk as a research environment.

    3. Given the prevalence of survey HITs, the greatest weakness is the lack of detailed suggestions on the changes in survey design necessitated by a crowdsourcing approach.
