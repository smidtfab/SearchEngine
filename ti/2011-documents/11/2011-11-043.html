Crowdsourcing: everybody’s doing it.

May 21, 2010 Juan Muniz No comments

In the quest to find inexpensive translations for their content, a handful of enterprising global companies have happened upon sourcing the very audiences they hope to reach. A few have even done it successfully(read: Facebook). As crowdsourcing becomes a viable option for international companies, language service providers are soon to start should soon start clamoring for their piece of the pie.

Crowdsourcing, of course, is the open-source initiative that portends (or threatens) to change the face of the localization industry. The idea behind it, in terms of localization, is that you can take a project that would normally be sourced to a specific linguistic supplier and instead outsource it to a large group of people in the target-language audience — preferably people who share similar interests in the content in question. Daniel Goldschmidt, cofounder of RIGI Localization Solutions, points to this in an article in the most recent edition of MultiLingual (subscription required to view the full article).

In this article, Goldschmidt asks an interesting question: “If the content is generated by the crowd, why not translate it by the crowd for the crowd?” This goes against the traditional model that most LSPs use to provide localization services, and a number of questions come to my mind immediately: How can one ensure quality translations are being provided if anyone with Internet access can contribute? Perhaps most importantly, how are LSPs supposed to make money using crowdsourcing?

Addressing my latter question first, it’s obvious crowdsourcing doesn’t make good business sense for every localization project. For companies like Wordbank, which specializes in marketing communications, there are confidentiality and other non-disclosure issues to be considered.Â  We couldn’t possibly turn to the general public to localize content for a client’s product launch, for example. There could, however, be increases in productivity, as LSPs would be able to process and localize larger volumes of content.

But then that brings quality into question. If literally anybody and everybody can provide the translations, it isn’t possible to ensure quality translations without subjecting the content to perpetual rounds of crowdsourced post-editing, or enlisting the services of skilled linguists to edit the translations — which could eat at the profits.

Crowdsourcing is another one of those features that’s here to stay. In what form LSPs embrace it will determine its success in the localization marketplace.
