  2. Adriana K said:

    Adriana K.

    1. This paper examines the methods and causes for cheating on Mechanical Turk, including what types of tasks, interfaces, and workers are more likely to cheat.

    2. The greatest strength of this paper is that its independent experiments on tasks, interfaces, and users can be used as quite concrete guidance for how Mechanical Turk should be used—for example, perhaps it might be good to portray tasks as not too straightforward, or to actually only use it for non-straightforward tasks that do not have yes/no answers.

    3. The biggest weakness of this paper is that it does not really evaluate the way in which malicious workers are identified—it would not have been so hard to hire people who cheat on purpose, in order to determine whether the “algorithm” for detection of cheating actually works.

    4. I learned that tasks which involve a switch of context are less tempting for malicious workers since they want to optimize for efficiency.

    5. I did not understand if the strategies for coping with cheating result in longer completion times because only malicious workers are excluded, or because non-cheaters are also excluded unintentionally.

    6. I wonder if one could devise clever strategies for detecting maliciousness in open-class responses (page 2)—but that would perhaps involve NLP strategies, which may or may not defeat the purpose, depending on the application.

  3. eric.diliu said:

    1. Summary of paper: This paper talks about why and how workers on MTurk cheat, and what kinds of work, interface are workers more likely to cheat on.

    2. Greatest strength: The paper used experiments in which one characteristic is changed each time and indicated what different kinds of work, interface, population have to do with cheating.

    3. Greatest weakness: Some of the experiments, for example judging relevance of website, are not convincing enough because websites that are relevant to some people may not be relevant to others; we can't really tell if they are cheating.

    4. Something I learned: The number of task-dependent share of malicious workers with and without using gold standard data didn't differ as much as I thought it would be .

    5. Something I didn't understand: Some tasks or surveys can't be finished in a too short period of time, so is the completion time of a hit be a way to judge cheating?

    6. Something I'd like to know more about: I would like to know more about the robot cheating method and how to prevent it.

  4. Stephen Goodwin said:

    Stephen Goodwin

    Reading: Eickhoff, C. and de Vries, A. How Crowdsourcable is Your Task? CSDM'11. https://www.ischool.utexas.edu/~ml/restricted/csdm2011_eickhoff.pdf

    1. This paper examines different ways workers exploit crowdsourcing tasks and experiments with several different experiments to reduce the number of malicious workers completing tasks.

    2. The greatest strength of this paper is clearly explaining how to cheat and how to prevent cheating in Section 3; this made the second half of the paper clearer.

    3. The greatest weakness of this paper is in not explaining any false positives in legitimate workers being detected as cheaters.

    4. I learned that limiting your task to just U.S. workers could drastically increase its completion time.

    5. I did not understand why there is a 16% malicious rate on workers with a 99% HIT approval rating; should his/her HIT approval rating be lower if they are detected as cheaters?

    6. I would like to know if different CrowdSourcing platforms experience differing amounts or types of cheating.
