5 Quality Controls for Crowdsourcing

  • Edit
  • Delete
  • Autopost

By no means are these mutually exclusive. They are not all required either. The best controls for you will depend on your crowd, your platform, your process and your objective.

1. Charge the crowd to participate. Charging the crowd - using money, time or some other hurdle - to participate will act as a natural filter for those who are not serious about it. I wrote more about this logic recently on DailyCrowdsource.com

2. Screen the crowd for quality criteria before allowing them to join. This may be redundant if you do number one.

3. Use a ranking system that identifies the highest quality users over time. Don't confuse quantity of contribution for quality.

4. Use a peer review system to identify the best solutions. Prediction is a whole other component to rank. One may not be great at creating great ideas, but can spot one from a mile away. Leverage that.

5. Harness quantity. If the crowd produces 50 "ideas" (or whatever it is you are asking for), there are bound to be a handful of good ones. Use that too.

Kyle,

I enjoyed reading your 5 Quality Controls for Crowdsourcing. I am with an innovation management platform vendor (Spigit) with experience in open innovation initiatives, and we have found your 5 points to be right on target. Your number 3, using a ranking system, is perhaps the most powerful concept in idea management, inside or outside an organization. Being able to know who is providing high quality contribution helps an organization refine ideas much more easily and effectively, and provides a game theory element (especially when leaderboards are used) that keeps users more engaged over time.

Your number 4, using a peer review system is also spot on. The best ideas are often networks of other ideas, and leveraging the community for idea augmentation, analysis, and selection brings more intelligence to the process and improves the outcomes. To your point, some people are better at contributing than originating, and people are often much broader than their specific roles or job titles.

Innovation management platforms do a great job of collecting and processing metrics for reputation ranking and enabling peer review systems to maximize the value of innovation communities. They also add dashboard reporting and analysis to help community managers keep a sharp eye on the behaviors of their communities and perform active quality control.
