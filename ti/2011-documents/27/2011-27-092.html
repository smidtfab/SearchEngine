An Objective Look At Crowdsourcing Your Company Logo Design

April 2, 2011 Online Teacher No comments

Finding an experienced, gifted designer to create a competent logo for your business is difficult. Ideally, you would like to locate someone who utilizes a rigorous style and design process. That individual ought to be prepared to study your sector, and design and prototype concepts that convey the right message

He or she ought to furthermore take a keen interest in how you are planning to use your logo. You may want it sent in various file formats to make it easier to integrate into Flash animation, print pamphlets, bumper stickers, or large-scale mass media (e.g. billboards).

Such a designer almost never, if ever at all, comes forth from a crowdsourcing task. Nevertheless, many businesses proceed to rely on this kind of arrangement for their trademarks. The discussion that comes after will explain the concept of crowdsourcing, and present some of the obstacles you will deal with throughout the procedure; this is not meant to dissuade you from taking this path. Instead, our goal is to prepare you for the pitfalls that have ensnared others.

Logo Design Crowdsourcing Explained

Crowdsourcing is a technique for putting your project in front of a large team of developers, and inviting each and every one to generate a share. In the context of logo design, it typically takes the shape of a competition. Developers send their logos, and you select from amongst them, and pay the victor.

On the surface, this seems like an appealing agreement for the firm. It is not unusual to bring in a bunch of designs for the cost you may otherwise pay to have a solitary specialized company logo designed. There are, however, a couple of prospective hurdles.

Setting Up A Sensible Cost To Bring In Volume

Establishing a cost for your design contest presents a double-edged sword. A reduced price can help preserve your spending budget, but the excellence of the competition’s end result may be questionable. Moreover, a lower price might restrict the amount of designers who take part; on the other hand, setting up a high price might improve the excellence of the presented logos, but does not always ensure you will get a style and design you can utilize

Keep in mind many crowdsourcing sites require you to select a winner and award payment. As a result, your funds could be squandered.

It is worth mentioning how expert developers view these kinds of projects. Many logos are sent in secretly within a non-disclosure agreement, and thus cannot be employed in the developer’s portfolio; so they are likely to steer clear of these types of contests unless of course the price presented is adequately substantial.

Copyright And Trademark Infringements

Here, we come to one of the most widespread difficulties of crowdsourcing your company logo design. Lots of the work sent in by contest entrants impedes on existing copyrights or trademarks (or both). Sometimes, only small pieces of a style are “borrowed.” Other times, major sections are lifted. While this can happen with a professional developer, it occurs far more regularly throughout contests. There are two main causes.

First, the site hosting the project generally lacks the manpower to regulate every logo sent in to each and every contest. Copyright and trademark infringements slip through the cracks. Second, as mentioned, most of the work is sent in privately. This means there are less eyes examining the work. In the unlikely event a designer is caught “lifting” from other concepts, he or she is seldom prohibited from entering additional competitions.

In a worst case scenario, you would unwittingly select the lifted logo design, and utilize it on your brochures, site, and other advertising collateral; the issue is, doing so puts you in a susceptible place should the copyright or trademark proprietor seek recompense.

Should You Crowdsource Your Logo Design?

Many professional developers assert that crowdsourcing won’t generate a workable company logo. They argue the quality of the submitted works can be so poor that such contests are a waste of time, energy, and money. In truth, there is a possibility you’ll attract a skilled logo developer who follows a strict design process. The probability of locating such a designer, however, is small. For most organizations that want to steer clear of the prospective headaches of crowdsourcing, employing a specialist upfront is a much better answer.
