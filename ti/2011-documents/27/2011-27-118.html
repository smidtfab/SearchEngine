Motivating Your Crowd for Success

Posted on | October 20, 2010 | 3 Comments

I must say, the overall reaction to my post on “predatory crowdsourcing” was far more positive than anticipated.  Nevertheless, I still managed to attract a few replies along the lines of: “No surprise coming from a venture capitalist who only cares about profits.  You probably look for companies that don’t pay their crowd.”  Therefore, I thought it would be worth one more post to clarify what I do look for in crowdsourcing companies, and the traits I believe are most likely to lead to value creation.

As you may have guessed, my analysis of a company has little to do with how (or if) it compensates its crowd.  Instead, my analysis tends to focus on how a company motivates its crowd. In other words, when I come across a new business plan, one of the first questions I ask is: “Why are people participating in this business?”  In my mind, the answer provides the clearest indicator as to whether a company can be successful, profitable and disruptive within its industry.

Through that lens, here is how I would categorize the most common crowdsourcing business models that we see (keeping in mind that there are far more models and nuances to those models than can be mentioned here):

The first category is what tends to be the most controversial among the “predatory crowdsourcing” group.  This is the model whereby companies access crowds, usually offshore, to perform menial and repeatable tasks at a lower cost.  The driver for crowd participants is very simple – a paycheck (albeit often a very small one).  It is the Crowdflower model, and it is often where the lines between outsourcing and crowdsourcing become slightly blurred.

While this model is capable of creating efficiencies within existing businesses, it is unlikely to disrupt an industry.  The limiting factor of this model tends to be the limited skill set of the crowd.  As a result, while often beneficial to a company’s bottom line, this model is rarely a game changer.

The second category includes those companies where participation is driven more by community then by individual success.  In these companies, people tend to participate based on their passion for a project or a cause. A good example would be the graphic designer who works all day, only to come home and create t-shirt designs for Threadless.  Or the scientist who comes home from his “real” job at the lab to contribute to a project he has joined on Innocentive.  Passion is a powerful driver, and, as a result, this can be a very powerful model.

The challenge with this model is that while it tends to attract a passionate community, it doesn’t necessarily attract the most talented people within an industry.  Therefore, it can be harder for a business built on these principles to disrupt an industry where talent demands a premium (which is certainly not every industry).  As a result, these companies often generate high community engagement, but can struggle to compete when it comes to quality and innovation.

The final type of crowdsourcing model is one that is specifically developed with an eye towards attracting the best talent and allowing them to rise to the top of crowd.  The people who participate in this model are primarily driven by three things: compensation, career advancement and peer recognition (to varying degrees).  In my mind, this combination of incentives is essential if a company is aimed at disrupting an existing industry.  The reason is simple: in order to drive change throughout an entire industry, you need to attract talent from the top (often referred to as “expert sourcing”).

An example of this model would be a creative director leaving a prominent ad agency to make a living in the Victors & Spoils crowd (which has happened), or a journalist leaving a prominent publication to make a living through the Kapost network (which we hope will happen soon).  In short, professional talent will only lead the disruption if the new model offers them the ability to succeed.

As I said in my last post, members of the crowd  participate for various reasons.  Some participate for lifestyle. Some participate for career advancement.  And some participate simply because it is fun.  And while different motivating factors tend to lend themselves to different business models, they are almost always aimed at creating efficiencies and gaining competitive advantage.

As investors, we tend to look for those opportunities that can be large and disruptive.  Creating a motivated crowd — with the right people in it — is a big first step.
